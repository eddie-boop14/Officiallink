Commissioned by King Carol I in 1873 and inaugurated in 1883, Peleș Castle was the summer residence of Romania's royal family and is widely regarded as one of the most beautiful castles in Europe. The 160 richly decorated rooms blend German Neo-Renaissance, Italian, Gothic, and Moorish styles — each room themed differently with hand-carved woodwork, Murano crystal chandeliers, German stained glass, and Cordoba leather walls. Peleș was the first castle in Europe fully powered by locally produced electricity. Located in the Carpathian mountain town of Sinaia at 800 metres altitude. Advance online booking is now required with timed entry slots limited to 2,000 visitors per day. Closed Mondays and Tuesdays. The adjacent Pelișor Castle, an Art Nouveau residence built for Crown Prince Ferdinand, is separately ticketed. Allow 1-2 hours for the guided castle tour.
Built in the 15th century by John Hunyadi, father of King Matthias Corvinus, this is one of the largest castles in Europe and Romania's most impressive Gothic-Renaissance fortress. The castle features massive towers, a drawbridge spanning a ravine, a Knights' Hall with marble columns and rose-window, a chapel, and a 30-metre-deep well that legend says was dug by three Turkish prisoners promised freedom upon completion (which was denied). Gothic architectural elements dominate the original 15th-century structure, while Renaissance additions came in the 16th and 17th centuries. Located in the industrial city of Hunedoara in southwestern Transylvania. The castle has served as a filming location for numerous period dramas. Allow 1-2 hours for the interior and courtyard.
Built between 1906 and 1911 for Prince Gheorghe Grigore Cantacuzino in Neo-Romanian architectural style, this castle gained international fame as the filming location for Nevermore Academy in the Netflix series "Wednesday." Set against the dramatic backdrop of the Bucegi Mountains in the Prahova Valley resort town of Bușteni, the castle blends neo-Romanian decorative elements with Art Nouveau influences. The interior features carved wooden ceilings, stained glass, and period furnishings. The grounds include formal gardens and mountain views. Art exhibitions rotate in the castle galleries. Located on the main road between Sinaia and Brașov, it combines easily with visits to Peleș Castle. The terrace café offers panoramic mountain views.
Perched on a cliff at 850 metres altitude above the Argeș River gorge, this ruined fortress was the actual stronghold of Vlad III Țepeș (the Impaler) — the historical figure behind the Dracula legend. Unlike the more tourist-friendly Bran Castle, Poenari was genuinely Vlad's military headquarters, rebuilt and expanded by him using forced labour in the 1450s. Reaching the ruins requires climbing 1,480 concrete steps up the mountainside, a strenuous ascent of approximately 30 minutes. The remaining walls and towers offer dramatic views over the Argeș valley and surrounding Carpathian peaks. Legend holds that Vlad's wife threw herself from the castle walls rather than be captured by the Ottomans. Located on the Transfăgărășan road, accessible only when the mountain pass road is open. Remote and infrequently visited compared to Bran Castle.

🇷🇴 Prejmer Fortified Church (UNESCO)
Located just 15 kilometres northeast of Brașov, Prejmer contains the largest and best-preserved fortified church complex in southeastern Europe.
Free
→
✅🏛 UNESCO
This spectacular 15th-century fortified church sits atop a hill surrounded by three concentric rings of defensive walls with towers, making it one of the most imposing fortified churches in Transylvania. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993, Biertan served as the seat of the Transylvanian Saxon Evangelical (Lutheran) bishops from 1572 to 1867. The church interior contains a remarkable carved wooden altarpiece with 28 painted panels depicting the life of the Virgin Mary, and an extraordinary door lock mechanism with 19 bolts — considered a masterwork of medieval locksmithing. The compound included a "marriage prison" — a small room where couples seeking divorce were locked together for two weeks with one bed, one plate, and one spoon, a system that reportedly resulted in only one divorce in 300 years. Located 80 kilometres from Sibiu.
The white-walled fortified church in the tiny village of Viscri (population under 500) is one of Transylvania's most evocative UNESCO sites. The 12th-century Romanesque church was fortified in the 15th and 16th centuries with concentric walls, towers, and storage rooms where villagers kept supplies during sieges. Viscri gained international attention through King Charles III (then Prince of Wales), who purchased and restored two houses in the village and became a vocal advocate for preserving Transylvanian Saxon heritage. The village itself appears frozen in time — unpaved streets, horse-drawn carts, painted gates, and a rural rhythm largely unchanged for centuries. Local women sell hand-knitted socks and preserves. The church tower offers views across the rolling Transylvanian countryside. Located on unpaved roads 40 kilometres from Sighișoara. Allow half a day including travel.
Located just 15 kilometres northeast of Brașov, Prejmer contains the largest and best-preserved fortified church complex in southeastern Europe. The 13th-century church is encircled by a massive 12-metre-high wall containing 272 individual storage rooms stacked in four tiers around the interior — each family in the village had an assigned room to store food, clothing, and valuables during sieges. The complex withstood numerous Ottoman attacks. A drawbridge and fortified entrance gate controlled access. The Gothic church interior features 15th-century frescoes and a carved stone baptismal font. The complete survival of the storage room system makes Prejmer uniquely illustrative of how Transylvanian Saxon communities organised their defence. Easily visited as a half-day trip from Brașov.

✅🏛 UNESCO
Built between 1970 and 1974 on the orders of Ceaușescu as a military strategic route across the Carpathians, this 90-kilometre road crosses the Făgăraș Mountains at an altitude of 2,042 metres through the second-highest paved road in Romania. The route features hairpin turns, dramatic cliff-edge sections, tunnels, and viaducts, with views across alpine lakes, glacial valleys, and mountain peaks. The road passes Bâlea Lake, a glacial lake at 2,034 metres accessible by cable car when the road is closed. The full road is typically open only from late June to late October due to heavy snowfall at altitude. The Top Gear television programme famously declared it "the best road in the world." The route connects Sibiu (north) to Curtea de Argeș (south) and passes near Poenari Citadel. Allow a full day for the drive with stops.
This magnificent star-shaped Vauban fortress is the largest of its kind in Romania, built by the Habsburgs between 1714 and 1738 on the site of the ancient Roman castrum Apulum. Seven bastions connected by massive walls and moats create the distinctive star shape visible from aerial views. The fortress contains several important buildings: the 13th-century Roman Catholic Cathedral (Romania's largest Romanesque church), the Orthodox Coronation Cathedral (where King Ferdinand and Queen Marie were crowned in 1922), the Union Hall where the unification of Transylvania with Romania was proclaimed in 1918, and several museums. A daily changing of the guard ceremony takes place at the main gate. The fortifications are free to explore. Alba Iulia holds deep national significance as the symbolic birthplace of modern unified Romania.
Scattered across the Orăștie Mountains in southwestern Transylvania, six Dacian fortress sites were collectively inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999. Sarmizegetusa Regia, the sacred capital and political centre of the Dacian kingdom, is the most important — its remains include circular and rectangular sanctuaries, residential quarters, and sophisticated water supply systems dating from the 1st century BC. These fortresses demonstrate an advanced pre-Roman civilisation that the Emperor Trajan fought two wars to conquer (101-106 AD), as depicted on Trajan's Column in Rome. The fortresses combine defensive structures with religious sanctuaries using a calendar-based layout. Reaching Sarmizegetusa Regia requires a mountain drive and a 3-kilometre hike through forest. The remote setting amid pristine Carpathian forests adds to the experience. Allow a full day from Sibiu or Hunedoara.
