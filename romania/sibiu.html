One of the three interconnected medieval squares that define Sibiu's Upper Town, the Grand Square is the largest in Transylvania at 142 by 93 metres. Lined with baroque palaces and merchant houses from the 14th to 18th centuries, the square served as the centre of civic and commercial life for centuries. Major buildings include the Brukenthal Palace, the Catholic Church, and the former City Hall (now the City Council). The square's most distinctive feature is the roofline — rows of dormer windows shaped like eyes peer down from the steep rooftops, giving Sibiu its famous "city with eyes" reputation. The square hosts major events including the Sibiu International Theatre Festival and Christmas markets. Outdoor cafés fill the square in warmer months.
Connected to the Grand Square by narrow passages, the Small Square was historically the centre of Sibiu's artisan guilds. The square is notable for its colourful merchant houses with arched ground-floor arcades and the cast-iron Liars' Bridge (Podul Minciunilor), a Sibiu landmark since 1859. Local legend holds that the bridge will collapse if someone tells a lie while standing on it. The bridge connects the Upper Town to the Lower Town and frames views of the Evangelical Cathedral tower. The small stairway Passage of Stairs (Pasajul Scărilor) descends steeply from the square to the Lower Town — one of Sibiu's most photographed spots. Medieval guild houses along the square now contain restaurants, craft shops, and galleries.
Standing at the passage connecting the Grand Square to the Small Square, this 13th-century tower served as the entrance gate to the inner fortified town and later as a grain store, fire watchtower, and temporary prison. The tower has been rebuilt several times following damage from fires and sieges. The current structure rises seven storeys and its clockface is visible from both squares. Visitors can climb to the top gallery for elevated views over both squares, the cathedral, the rooftops with their distinctive "eye" dormers, and the surrounding Carpathian foothills. The tower functions as a landmark orientation point — it is visible from nearly everywhere in the old town and marks the boundary between the Grand and Small squares.

🇷🇴 ASTRA National Museum Complex (Open-Air Museum)

Established by Baron Samuel von Brukenthal, Governor of Transylvania, and opened to the public in 1817, this is Romania's oldest museum and one of the oldest in Central Europe. The core collection of approximately 1,200 European paintings spans the 15th to 18th centuries and includes works by Van Eyck, Memling, Titian, Rubens, and Cranach the Elder — the only museum in Romania with works by these masters. Housed in the Baron's late-Baroque palace on the Grand Square, the museum has expanded to encompass six satellite locations across Sibiu including the Museum of History, Museum of Pharmacy, Natural History Museum, and Contemporary Art Gallery. The Brukenthal Library within the palace contains approximately 300,000 volumes including manuscripts and incunabula. Allow 2-3 hours for the main palace galleries.
Located in the Dumbrava Forest 4 kilometres south of Sibiu's centre, this is the largest open-air museum in Romania and one of the largest in Europe, covering 96 hectares around an artificial lake. Over 300 authentic buildings relocated from across Romania include farmhouses, churches, watermills, oil presses, fulling mills, and workshops representing traditional rural life from the 18th to 20th centuries. The collection documents the complete range of Romanian folk architecture and traditional crafts. Walking paths through the forested site connect distinct village recreations. Traditional craft demonstrations take place during seasonal festivals. The lakeside setting provides a peaceful natural environment. Allow 3-4 hours for a comprehensive visit. Accessible by bus from the city centre or by a pleasant walk through the Dumbrava Forest.

Built between the 14th and 16th centuries, this Gothic church features a 73.34-metre tower — the tallest in Transylvania. Originally a Catholic church, it became Lutheran following the Reformation when Sibiu's Saxon population embraced Protestantism. The interior preserves one of the largest church organs in southeastern Europe with over 6,000 pipes, medieval frescoes (partially uncovered during restoration), and an elaborate 1772 Baroque altar. The tower houses four bells and is climbable for panoramic views that are among the finest in Romania — on clear days the Făgăraș Mountains are visible. The church contains the tomb of Mihnea cel Rău (Mihnea the Bad), son of Vlad the Impaler, murdered on the church steps in 1510.
Built between 1902 and 1906, this Romanian Orthodox cathedral was modelled on Hagia Sophia in Istanbul, with a massive central dome rising 32 metres — the largest Orthodox dome in Transylvania. The exterior combines neo-Byzantine architecture with local elements, while the interior is richly decorated with frescoes and a carved marble iconostasis. The cathedral sits on Mitropoliei Street, slightly outside the medieval walled town, symbolising the Romanian Orthodox community's presence alongside the historically dominant Lutheran Saxon institutions. The building serves as the Metropolitan Cathedral of Transylvania and hosts major Orthodox services and ceremonies. The neo-Byzantine facades are particularly striking when illuminated at night.

🇷🇴 Medieval Fortifications & Towers Walk

See official

✅
Sibiu preserves one of the most complete sets of medieval fortifications in Romania, with substantial sections of three concentric defensive walls, bastions, and towers still standing. The southeastern section offers the best-preserved stretch, where visitors can walk along portions of the walls and enter several towers. Notable structures include the Carpenters' Tower, the Potters' Tower, and the Thick Tower (Turnul Gros). The fortification system was built and maintained by Sibiu's medieval guilds, each responsible for a specific tower — a common practice in Transylvanian Saxon cities. Connected tunnels and passageways between the walls created layered defence zones. The Passage of Stairs (Pasajul Scărilor) descends through the fortification levels, linking the Upper and Lower Towns. The towers are scattered around the old town perimeter and discoverable on foot.

Sibiu's main park extends south from the old town along shaded walkways lined with over a century of planted trees. Established in 1856, the park covers approximately 18 hectares and features winding paths through mature forests of linden, horse chestnut, and maple trees. A playground, small zoo, and lake with pedal boats provide family activities. The park connects to paths leading toward the Dumbrava Forest and the ASTRA Open-Air Museum, making it possible to walk from the medieval centre through continuous green space to the museum. Historical monuments and benches dot the pathways. The park functions as Sibiu's outdoor living room, especially popular for weekend promenades.
Situated at 1,442 metres altitude in the Cindrel Mountains, Păltiniș is Romania's oldest mountain resort, established in 1894 by the Transylvanian Carpathian Association. Located 35 kilometres south of Sibiu, it offers skiing in winter and hiking in summer amid alpine meadows and coniferous forests. The resort retains a quieter, more authentic character than Romania's larger ski destinations. Cross-country skiing trails and snowshoeing routes complement the downhill slopes. Summer trails lead through pristine Carpathian landscapes. The road from Sibiu to Păltiniș is scenic in its own right, climbing through the Dumbrava Forest and past traditional mountain villages. The resort serves as a base for hiking in the Cindrel and Lotrului mountains.
