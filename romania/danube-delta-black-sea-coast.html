Where the Danube River meets the Black Sea, Europe's second-longest river splits into three main channels — Chilia, Sulina, and Sfântu Gheorghe — creating a vast 5,800 square kilometre wetland that is Europe's largest and best-preserved delta. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1991 and a Biosphere Reserve, the delta hosts over 300 bird species including Europe's largest colony of white pelicans (over 2,500 breeding pairs), cormorants, herons, egrets, and the globally rare pygmy cormorant. The waterways are navigable only by boat, and traditional villages accessible solely by water maintain a way of life centred on fishing. The delta contains floating reed islands, sandbars, forests, and lakes. Boat excursions depart from Tulcea, the gateway city. Multi-day trips with overnight stays in delta villages offer the deepest experience. Best wildlife viewing from April to September.
Situated at the point where the Danube begins to split into its delta channels, Tulcea is the obligatory departure point for all boat trips into the Danube Delta. The city's port area is lined with boat operators offering day trips, multi-day excursions, and transfers to delta villages. The Danube Delta Eco-Tourism Museum Centre in Tulcea provides an excellent introduction to the delta's ecosystems, history, and communities before venturing into the wetland itself. The city sits on seven hills above the Danube, with a monument to Romanian independence on the highest point offering river views. Tulcea also houses the Museum of History and Archaeology with Greco-Roman artefacts from the ancient port of Aegyssus. Regular ferry services operate from the port to delta settlements including Sulina and Sfântu Gheorghe.
Located at the mouth of the Sulina channel where the Danube empties into the Black Sea, Sulina is the easternmost settlement in the European Union. Accessible only by boat (a 3-4 hour ferry from Tulcea), the small town was once one of the busiest ports in Europe during the 19th century when the European Danube Commission made it the primary navigable channel for international shipping. The cosmopolitan cemetery contains graves of British, Greek, Turkish, and other sailors and merchants, reflecting its former international character. A lighthouse marks the point where river meets sea. The town's beach stretches along a wild, undeveloped coastline. The journey from Tulcea passes through the heart of the delta with abundant birdlife visible from the ferry.
At the end of the southernmost Danube channel, this isolated fishing village of several hundred inhabitants is reachable only by boat from Tulcea (approximately 3 hours). The village sits between the delta's wetlands and a vast, wild Black Sea beach that stretches for kilometres with virtually no development — one of the most pristine coastal stretches in Europe. The local Lipovan community, descendants of Old Believer Russians who settled here centuries ago, maintains traditional fishing practices. Fresh fish, especially sturgeon and carp, is prepared in traditional recipes at village guesthouses. The surrounding delta waters are rich with wildlife including pelicans, sea eagles, and otters. The annual Anonimul International Film Festival, held in August, screens films on the beach.

Founded around 600 BC as the Greek colony of Tomis, Constanța is Romania's oldest continuously inhabited city and the principal port on the Black Sea. The Roman poet Ovid spent his final years in exile here — a statue in Ovid Square commemorates him. The Art Nouveau Casino, built in 1910 on the seafront promenade, is the city's most iconic building though it has stood vacant for decades pending restoration. The old town contains the Roman Mosaic Building, a vast 2nd-century mosaic floor spanning over 850 square metres — one of the largest in the ancient world. The Great Mahmudiye Mosque (1910) with its 50-metre minaret, the Orthodox Cathedral, and the Archaeological Museum with extensive Greco-Roman collections are clustered in the compact historic centre. The seafront promenade offers views across the Black Sea.
Housed in a neoclassical former City Hall building overlooking Ovid Square, this museum contains one of Romania's most important archaeological collections. Exhibits span from the Greek colonisation of the Black Sea coast in the 7th century BC through Roman occupation and the Byzantine period. The Glycon serpent sculpture, a unique 2nd-century marble statue of a snake with a human-like head, is the museum's most celebrated piece. Extensive collections of Greek pottery, Roman sculpture, gold jewellery, and coins document the ancient commercial importance of the Tomis port. The Roman floor mosaics in the adjacent Roman Edifice can be visited on a combined ticket. The museum provides essential context for understanding how this coastline connected the ancient Mediterranean world with the Danube frontier.
Discovered in 1959 during construction work, this enormous mosaic floor covers approximately 850 square metres and dates from the 3rd to 4th century AD, making it one of the largest surviving Roman mosaic floors in the world. The multi-coloured geometric and floral patterns once decorated the floor of a commercial building in ancient Tomis. The mosaic is now protected by a modern structure built directly over the excavation site on the hillside between the old town and the port. Visitors walk on elevated platforms above the mosaic for viewing. Some sections show remarkably well-preserved colour and detail. The mosaic demonstrates the wealth and sophistication of Roman-era Constanța when Tomis was an important provincial capital and trading hub on the western Black Sea coast.
Stretching 8 kilometres along a narrow strip of land between the Black Sea and Siutghiol Lake, Mamaia is Romania's oldest and most popular seaside resort. The fine sand beach is backed by a continuous strip of hotels, clubs, restaurants, and entertainment venues that create the country's liveliest summer atmosphere. A gondola lift runs the length of the resort, offering aerial views of both the sea and the lake. The beach is well-maintained with facilities including sunbed rental, water sports, and beach bars. Siutghiol Lake on the western side provides a calmer alternative for water activities. The resort connects directly to Constanța's northern edge, making city sightseeing easily combinable with beach time. Peak season runs from June through September when the Black Sea reaches swimming temperatures.
Founded in 657 BC by Greek colonists from Miletus, Histria (Istros) is the oldest Greek settlement on the western Black Sea coast and one of the most important archaeological sites in Romania. Located 65 kilometres north of Constanța on the shores of Sinoe Lake (once the Black Sea coastline, which has since receded), the excavated ruins reveal layers of Greek, Roman, and Byzantine occupation spanning over 1,300 years. Visible remains include city walls, thermal baths, basilicas, a residential quarter, and the economic quarter with workshops. An on-site museum displays pottery, coins, sculpture, and tools recovered during over a century of excavations. The surrounding lagoon landscape, now part of the Danube Delta Biosphere Reserve buffer zone, provides a hauntingly isolated setting. Allow 2-3 hours including the museum and ruins.
Romania's southernmost seaside settlement, just 2 kilometres from the Bulgarian border, Vama Veche developed as a countercultural beach village during the communist era when artists, musicians, and free spirits gathered at this then-undeveloped stretch of coast. The village retains its bohemian identity despite increasing commercialisation, with a relaxed atmosphere distinct from the mainstream resort culture of Mamaia. Beach camping, live music, and an alternative scene characterise the summer months. The beach itself is smaller and less manicured than Mamaia but prized for its authentic character. Annual music festivals and cultural events draw young Romanians seeking a non-conformist seaside experience. The surrounding coastline is part of a marine reserve. Best visited June through September.
