Designed by Italian-Brazilian architect Lina Bo Bardi and inaugurated in 1968 by Queen Elizabeth II, the building is an engineering landmark — its main body is suspended above ground by four massive red concrete columns, creating a 74-metre free span beneath, one of the largest in the world. Houses over 11,000 works including significant European paintings by Raphael, Rembrandt, Van Gogh, Monet, Renoir, Picasso, and Matisse alongside extensive Brazilian and Latin American collections. Bo Bardi's signature glass easel display system presents paintings as if floating in space. Located on Avenida Paulista, São Paulo's most iconic avenue. The open plaza beneath the building hosts a weekly antiques market and cultural events. Allow 2-3 hours.
Founded in 1905, São Paulo's oldest art museum occupies a stunning late 19th-century building designed by Ramos de Azevedo in the Luz district, sensitively renovated by Paulo Mendes da Rocha in the 1990s. The collection of over 11,000 works focuses on Brazilian art from the 19th century to present day, with particular strength in modernist works from the 1920s Week of Modern Art movement. The building features exposed brick interior walls and glass-roofed courtyards created during the renovation. Located adjacent to Luz Station and the Jardim da Luz park. The Pinacoteca Estação, in the neighbouring Luz Station building, houses the Memorial da Resistência documenting political repression during Brazil's military dictatorship. Allow 2 hours.
Housed in a building designed by Lina Bo Bardi beneath the marquise of Ibirapuera Park, the museum was founded in 1948 and holds over 5,000 works of modern and contemporary art. The collection includes significant Brazilian modernist works alongside international artists. The museum's Sculpture Garden, designed by Roberto Burle Marx, occupies a large outdoor area within Ibirapuera Park combining art with tropical landscaping. Hosts the annual Panorama da Arte Brasileira exhibition showcasing emerging artists. The building's integration with the park creates a unique museum experience where indoor and outdoor spaces flow together. Educational programmes and workshops run throughout the year. Located within Ibirapuera Park alongside other cultural institutions.
Located beneath the stands of the historic Estádio Municipal Paulo Machado de Carvalho (Pacaembu), this immersive museum tells the story of football's central role in Brazilian culture and identity. Opened in 2008 with 15 themed rooms spread across 6,900 square metres using large-scale video projections, interactive installations and sound design rather than traditional glass-case displays. Highlights include a recreation of the atmosphere of a packed stadium, rooms dedicated to World Cup victories, and the evolution of tactics and playing styles. The museum explores football's social impact — from racial integration to national identity. The art deco Pacaembu Stadium itself, built in 1940, is architecturally significant. Allow 2 hours.
Opened in 1954 for the 400th anniversary of São Paulo's founding, this 158-hectare urban park was landscaped by Roberto Burle Marx with buildings designed by Oscar Niemeyer and structural engineer Joaquim Cardozo. The park contains multiple cultural institutions including MAM, the Afro-Brazilian Museum, and the Biennial Pavilion — one of the largest exhibition spaces in Latin America where the São Paulo Art Biennial has been held since 1951. Three interconnected lakes, extensive running and cycling paths, and open green spaces draw an estimated 14 million annual visitors. The Grande Marquise, a striking concrete canopy designed by Niemeyer, connects several buildings. Free to enter. Active recreation includes jogging, cycling, skating and outdoor fitness.
São Paulo's most symbolic thoroughfare, stretching 2.8 kilometres along a ridge that was once lined with coffee baron mansions in the early 20th century. Today the avenue mixes skyscrapers, cultural institutions, restaurants and street life. MASP, Japan House, Instituto Moreira Salles and Centro Cultural FIESP line the route. Every Sunday, the avenue closes to vehicles and opens to pedestrians, cyclists, skaters and street performers — a beloved weekly tradition drawing hundreds of thousands. The Casa das Rosas, a surviving 1930s mansion, now operates as a literary centre. The Trianon-MASP park (Parque Tenente Siqueira Campos) opposite MASP preserves a remnant of original Atlantic Forest. Free to walk at any time.
Completed in 1965 at 168 metres and 46 floors, this was São Paulo's tallest building for decades and remains one of the most recognisable features of the downtown skyline. The rooftop restaurant and bar on the 41st floor, Terraço Itália, offers 360-degree panoramic views across the vast metropolitan area. Built by the Italian-Brazilian community and designed by architect Franz Heep. The tower stands at the intersection of Avenida Ipiranga and Avenida São Luís, a corner immortalised in the famous Caetano Veloso song "Sampa." The neighbouring Edifício Copan, designed by Oscar Niemeyer with its distinctive undulating concrete façade, is visible from the terrace and considered one of the world's largest residential buildings.
Construction began in 1913 and was completed in 1967, though the towers were only finished in 2002. The neo-Gothic design by German architect Maximilian Hehl incorporates Renaissance elements and can hold 8,000 people, making it one of the largest cathedrals in the world. The organ is one of the largest in Latin America with over 12,000 pipes. The crypt beneath the main altar contains the tombs of significant figures in São Paulo's history including indigenous chief Tibiriçá, one of the first indigenous converts to Christianity. Located at Praça da Sé, the geographic centre of São Paulo marked by a Marco Zero plaque from which all distances in the state are measured. The surrounding square is a major public gathering point. Free to visit.
Founded in 1598, making it one of São Paulo's oldest institutions, though the current eclectic-style church dates to the early 20th century with interior decorations completed by Benedictine monk Dom Adelbert Gresnigt. The church interior features rich Beuron art style paintings, elaborate carved wooden altarpieces and stained glass. The monastery's bakery produces celebrated breads and cakes sold at a small shop. Sunday Mass at 10am features Gregorian chant performed by the resident monks — a deeply atmospheric experience that draws large congregations. The monastery sits on a hilltop in the city centre overlooking the Anhangabaú valley. The organ was built in Germany and installed in 1954. Free to visit the church during service times.
Opened in 1933 in a grand neoclassical building featuring 72 stained glass windows depicting agricultural scenes by Russian-Brazilian artist Conrado Sorgenicht Filho. The market houses over 290 stalls selling exotic fruits, spices, cheeses, meats, fish and imported goods from around the world. The famous mortadella sandwich from Hocca Bar — overstuffed with Italian mortadella on crusty bread — has become a culinary icon and draws visitors specifically to try it. The upper mezzanine level contains restaurants specialising in traditional São Paulo dishes including pastel de bacalhau (salt cod pastry). The building's architectural details include 32-metre-high ceilings and art deco ironwork. Located in the historic Cantareira district near Luz station.
Heart of the largest Japanese diaspora community in the world, São Paulo's Liberdade neighbourhood has been home to Japanese, Korean and Chinese immigrants since the early 20th century. Red torii gates span the main streets and Japanese-style lampposts line the sidewalks. The Japanese Immigration Museum traces the history of migration from 1908 onwards. The weekend street fair along Rua Galvão Bueno sells Japanese snacks, crafts, and imported goods. Temples, authentic restaurants, and Asian grocery stores create an immersive cultural enclave. The neighbourhood hosts major celebrations for Japanese festivals including Tanabata Matsuri (Star Festival). Metro accessible via Liberdade station. Free to explore the streets and market.
São Paulo's creative and bohemian neighbourhood, renowned internationally for its vibrant street art scene. Beco do Batman (Batman Alley), a narrow street completely covered in colourful graffiti and murals, has become one of the city's most photographed locations — the art changes regularly as artists repaint walls. The neighbourhood is filled with independent art galleries, design studios, vintage shops, craft breweries and bars. The nightlife scene centres on Rua Aspicuelta and surrounding streets. Local artists have open studios and the area hosts regular art walks. The creative energy draws comparisons to Berlin's Kreuzberg or London's Shoreditch. Free to explore. Best experienced on foot during afternoons and evenings.
One of three Japan House cultural centres worldwide (alongside London and Los Angeles), located on Avenida Paulista in a building redesigned by Kengo Kuma featuring a distinctive façade of interlocking hinoki cypress wood panels. Rotating exhibitions explore Japanese art, design, technology and culture through immersive installations. The ground floor includes a curated shop selling Japanese crafts and a restaurant serving authentic Japanese cuisine. Opened in 2017 as part of a Japanese government initiative to deepen cultural exchange. The building itself demonstrates Japanese architectural philosophy — natural materials, clean lines and harmony between interior and exterior spaces. Allow 1-2 hours.
Built between 1895 and 1901 using materials shipped from Britain, this magnificent Victorian railway station was designed by British engineers and modelled on grand European stations. The structure features a distinctive clock tower, iron and glass roof spans imported from Glasgow, and ornate façade details. The building was constructed during the height of São Paulo's coffee boom when the city was rapidly expanding. Still a functioning railway and metro station serving millions of commuters. Houses the Museum of the Portuguese Language (Museu da Língua Portuguesa), an interactive museum celebrating the Portuguese language, reopened in 2021 after a 2015 fire. The surrounding Luz cultural district includes the Pinacoteca and Jardim da Luz. Free to admire the architecture.
