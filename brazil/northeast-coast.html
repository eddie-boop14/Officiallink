A former fishing village set within the Jericoacoara National Park, accessible only by 4x4 vehicles across sandy tracks — the streets remain unpaved sand by design. The Sunset Dune (Duna do Pôr do Sol) draws crowds each evening to watch the sun sink into the ocean from atop shifting white sand. Pedra Furada, a naturally arched rock formation on the eastern coast, is the village's iconic landmark. The consistent trade winds from July to January make Jericoacoara one of the world's premier kitesurfing and windsurfing destinations. The protected national park status prevents high-rise development, maintaining the village's low-key character. Lagoa do Paraíso and Lagoa Azul — freshwater lagoons surrounded by dunes and palm trees — offer calm swimming in crystal-clear water. Located 300 kilometres from Fortaleza.
A beach village famous for its dramatic red sandstone cliffs that drop directly to wide golden sand. The cliffs feature a carved crescent moon and star symbol that has become the village's trademark — said to have been carved by a visiting hippie in the 1970s when the area was discovered by backpackers. The main street, Broadway, runs one block back from the beach and is lined with restaurants, bars and craft shops. Buggy excursions along the coast visit neighbouring beaches, sand dunes and the fishing village of Majorlândia. The consistent winds attract kitesurfers and windsurfers. Traditional jangada fishing boats still operate from the beach. Located 160 kilometres south of Fortaleza on the Ceará coast. The red and golden cliff colours are most vivid at sunrise and sunset.
A surreal landscape of 155,000 hectares of rolling white sand dunes stretching to the horizon, punctuated between May and September by thousands of freshwater lagoons of vivid blue and green that form in the valleys between dunes after the rainy season. Despite its desert appearance, the region receives significant rainfall — the lagoons can reach 3 metres deep and support fish populations that arrive via underground water connections. The park name means "bed sheets of Maranhão," describing how the white dunes appear from above. Access is primarily through the gateway towns of Barreirinhas or Santo Amaro do Maranhão, with 4x4 transfers to the park boundary. No infrastructure exists within the dunes — all visits require guides. The Lagoa Azul and Lagoa Bonita are the most visited swimming lagoons. Peak lagoon season is June to August.
The capital of Maranhão state and a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1997, São Luís is the only Brazilian state capital founded by the French (1612) before falling to Portuguese and briefly Dutch control. The historic centre contains over 3,500 colonial buildings distinguished by their facades covered in Portuguese azulejo tiles — the largest collection of tile-fronted colonial architecture in Latin America. The tiles were originally used as ballast in ships and later adopted as decorative protection against the tropical climate. The city is known as the "Brazilian Athens" for its strong literary tradition and as "Jamaican Reggae" for its unique reggae music scene — São Luís hosts the largest reggae culture outside Jamaica. Bumba Meu Boi, a syncretic folk festival, is celebrated with elaborate performances in June.
A vibrant beach village perched on cliffs above a series of spectacular beaches, 85 kilometres south of Natal. The Baía dos Golfinhos (Dolphin Bay) is accessible by a trail through Atlantic Forest and is home to a resident pod of spinner dolphins regularly seen from the beach and cliffs. The Santuário Ecológico de Pipa protects a stretch of coastal Atlantic Forest with trails and a clifftop viewing platform. The main village has evolved from a fishing hamlet into a cosmopolitan resort with restaurants, boutiques and nightlife while maintaining its beach-town character. Praia do Amor (Love Beach) features dramatic red cliffs and strong surf. Buggy excursions connect neighbouring beaches. The warm water (26-28°C year-round) and consistent waves attract surfers.
A UNESCO World Heritage volcanic archipelago of 21 islands located 354 kilometres off Brazil's northeast coast, with strictly controlled visitor numbers to protect its extraordinary marine environment. The main island hosts Baía do Sancho, repeatedly voted the world's best beach by travel publications, accessed by descending through a crack in the cliff via metal ladders. The surrounding waters contain some of the clearest visibility in the Atlantic (up to 50 metres), with sea turtles, spinner dolphins, reef sharks and rays. A daily environmental preservation fee is charged per visitor per day, and a separate national park fee applies for accessing trails and beaches within the marine park. Maximum overnight visitors are capped. Flights from Recife and Natal. Diving and snorkelling are world-class. Advance planning essential.
A beach resort 60 kilometres south of Recife known for its natural tide pools (piscinas naturais) that form at low tide on coral reefs approximately 100 metres offshore. Jangada rafts ferry visitors to the pools where warm, crystal-clear water reaches waist height and tropical fish are easily visible. The name "Port of Chickens" derives from a coded reference used during the slave trade era when enslaved people were smuggled into Brazil after abolition, hidden among legitimate cargo of chickens. The main beach and village are compact and walkable. Surrounding beaches including Maracaípe (known for seahorse estuaries) and Muro Alto (calm natural pools) offer diverse coastal experiences. The town has developed into a major tourist destination with hotels, restaurants and dive operators.
A vehicle-free village on Tinharé Island, 60 kilometres south of Salvador, accessible by boat from the mainland terminal at Valença or by catamaran direct from Salvador's Mercado Modelo (2.5 hours). The island's beaches are numbered sequentially — First Beach is the liveliest with bars and nightlife, Second Beach is the main swimming and restaurant strip, Third and Fourth beaches grow progressively quieter with coconut palms and natural pools at low tide. The 17th-century fortress at the village entrance and the lighthouse provide panoramic views. The surrounding archipelago includes Boipeba island, even more remote and undeveloped. No cars or motorbikes are permitted — luggage is transported by wheelbarrow. The warm Bahian waters are swimmable year-round.
A vast national park in the mountainous interior of Bahia state, centred on the historic diamond-mining town of Lençóis. The landscape features flat-topped mountains (chapadas), deep canyons, underground rivers, caves and waterfalls. Cachoeira da Fumaça (Smoke Waterfall) at 340 metres is one of Brazil's tallest — the water disperses into mist before reaching the bottom, hence the name. The Gruta da Pratinha and Gruta Azul contain underground rivers with blue-tinted water. The Poço Encantado (Enchanted Pool) and Poço Azul produce intense blue optical effects when sunlight enters the cave openings between April and September. Multi-day trekking routes connect the park's highlights through cerrado and Atlantic Forest vegetation. The gateway town of Lençóis retains colonial architecture from the 19th-century diamond rush. Allow minimum 3 days.
The flagship visitor centre of Projeto TAMAR, Brazil's pioneering sea turtle conservation programme established in 1980. Located in the village of Praia do Forte, 80 kilometres north of Salvador on the Coconut Coast (Costa dos Coqueiros). The centre houses open-air tanks with rescued sea turtles and rays, plus exhibitions documenting the project's work protecting five species of sea turtle along Brazil's coast. During nesting season (September to March), guided night walks observe turtles laying eggs on the beach. TAMAR's work has significantly increased survival rates of hatchlings along the Brazilian coast. The village of Praia do Forte itself is a charming pedestrianised resort with a ruined 16th-century castle (Castelo Garcia d'Ávila), restaurants and calm swimming beaches with natural reef pools.
