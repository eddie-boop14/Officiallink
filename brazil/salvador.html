Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985, the Pelourinho is one of the largest and best-preserved collections of Portuguese colonial architecture in the Americas. The cobblestone streets are lined with pastel-coloured buildings from the 17th to 19th centuries featuring ornate balconies and stucco work. The district's name derives from the Portuguese word for pillory — the post where enslaved people were publicly punished — a reminder of Salvador's history as a major slave trade port. Today the neighbourhood is a vibrant cultural hub with live music performances, capoeira demonstrations, art galleries and restaurants. Largo do Pelourinho, the central square, is one of Brazil's most photographed locations. The Tuesday night Olodum drum performances draw large crowds. Free to explore the streets.
Widely considered the finest example of Portuguese Baroque architecture in Brazil, the church interior is almost entirely covered in gold leaf — estimated at over 800 kilograms of gold adorning the carved wooden altarpieces, walls and ceiling. Construction began in 1708 and was completed over several decades by both Portuguese craftsmen and enslaved artisans. The cloister features hand-painted azulejo tile panels depicting scenes from Horace's mythology and Portuguese proverbs. The adjacent Third Order Church has a unique Spanish Plateresque sandstone façade, the only one of its kind in Brazil, hidden for centuries under plaster until rediscovered in 1936. Located on Largo do Cruzeiro de São Francisco, connected to Terreiro de Jesus square.
Built between 1657 and 1672 as the church of the Jesuit college, this became Salvador's cathedral in 1765 after the Jesuits were expelled from Brazil. The Mannerist-Baroque façade is constructed from imported Portuguese lioz limestone. Inside, the sacristy contains an exceptional series of 17th-century oil paintings depicting the lives of Jesuit saints. The high altar is an elaborate gilded wooden structure. The building occupies a prominent position on Terreiro de Jesus, Salvador's most important colonial square, surrounded by the medical faculty of the Federal University of Bahia housed in the former Jesuit college. The square itself functions as an open-air gathering point with capoeira performances, street vendors and cultural activities.
Dedicated to Bahia-born Jorge Amado, one of Brazil's most translated and internationally renowned authors, whose novels like "Gabriela, Clove and Cinnamon" and "Dona Flor and Her Two Husbands" brought Bahian culture to global readership. The foundation occupies a colonial building on Largo do Pelourinho, housing manuscripts, personal objects, photographs and editions of Amado's works translated into 49 languages across four floors. Also preserves the legacy of his wife, writer and photographer Zélia Gattai. The institution organises literary events and cultural programming. Amado's vivid descriptions of Salvador's people, food and customs shaped international perceptions of Bahian culture and made Pelourinho famous worldwide.
Originally built in 1873 as a hydraulic lift, rebuilt in its current art deco form in 1930, this 72-metre public elevator is Salvador's most recognisable landmark and a vital transport link between the Cidade Alta (Upper City) and Cidade Baixa (Lower City). The elevator makes the journey in approximately 30 seconds, carrying 128 passengers per trip. From the upper platform, panoramic views extend across the Baía de Todos os Santos (All Saints' Bay) — one of the largest bays in the world. Over 28,000 passengers use it daily. At the base, the Mercado Modelo market and cruise ship terminal are within walking distance. The elevator is named after Antônio de Lacerda, who funded the original hydraulic system.
Located in the Lower City at the base of the Elevador Lacerda, this market occupies a building that originally served as the customs house. Over 260 stalls sell traditional Bahian handicrafts including berimbaus (capoeira instruments), Fita do Bonfim ribbons, lacework, carved wooden figures, and Candomblé religious items. The basement, which once held enslaved people awaiting sale, now hosts capoeira demonstrations and cultural performances. Live capoeira circles (rodas) are a regular occurrence. The market was rebuilt after a fire in 1984. Bahian street food including acarajé is available from traditional baianas vendors outside. Located on Praça Maria Felipa overlooking All Saints' Bay. Free to browse.
Built between 1746 and 1772, this is the most important site of religious devotion in Salvador and a powerful symbol of the syncretic blend of Catholic and Candomblé traditions that defines Bahian spirituality. The church fence is perpetually covered with coloured Fita do Bonfim ribbons — visitors tie them with three knots, each representing a wish, and tradition holds the wishes come true when the ribbon falls off naturally. The Sala dos Milagres (Room of Miracles) contains thousands of ex-voto offerings. The annual Festa do Bonfim in January features the ritual washing of the church steps (Lavagem do Bonfim) by women in traditional Bahian white dress — one of Salvador's most important cultural events. Located in the Bonfim peninsula neighbourhood.
Consistently ranked among Brazil's best urban beaches, this sheltered crescent of sand sits within All Saints' Bay rather than facing the open Atlantic, producing calm, warm waters ideal for swimming. The beach is framed by the colonial-era Forte de Santo Antônio da Barra (Farol da Barra), a 17th-century fortress housing a nautical museum and one of the oldest lighthouses in the Americas. Sunset views from the fort are a daily ritual for locals and visitors. The surrounding Barra neighbourhood contains restaurants, bars and the lively Barra shopping district. Particularly spectacular during New Year's Eve and Carnival when the waterfront fills with celebrations. Free public access. Located south of the city centre.
The Forte de Santo Antônio da Barra dates to 1534 (rebuilt 1702), making it one of the oldest military fortifications in Brazil. The lighthouse atop the fort, operational since 1698, is one of the oldest in the Americas. The fort now houses the Museu Náutico da Bahia (Nautical Museum of Bahia) displaying navigation instruments, ship models, maps and artefacts from Brazil's maritime history. The promontory location offers sweeping views where All Saints' Bay meets the Atlantic Ocean — considered one of the finest sunset vantage points in Salvador. The surrounding rocky coastline and coral pools are popular for tide-pool exploration at low tide. The fort marks the meeting point of the bay and ocean waters.
Housed in a restored 17th-century sugar mill estate (Solar do Unhão) on the waterfront of All Saints' Bay, this museum was adapted by architect Lina Bo Bardi in the 1960s. The collection focuses on modern and contemporary Bahian and Brazilian art. The colonial complex includes the main house, chapel, slave quarters and warehouse buildings arranged around a courtyard, representing the complete structure of a colonial sugar operation. Saturday evening jazz concerts on the waterfront terrace are a beloved Salvador tradition drawing locals and visitors. The location offers dramatic sunset views across the bay towards Itaparica Island. The building's transformation from site of enslavement to cultural institution carries deep symbolic weight.
Built by enslaved Africans and their descendants over approximately 100 years, from the late 17th to late 18th century, working during their limited free time — primarily at night and on holy days. The church is a powerful symbol of Afro-Brazilian faith and resilience. The Brotherhood of the Rosary of the Black People, founded in 1685, continues to maintain the church. Sunday Mass incorporates Candomblé-influenced music with African drums and rhythms blending with Catholic liturgy, creating a uniquely Bahian religious experience. The blue-and-white Baroque façade sits at the lower end of Largo do Pelourinho. Inside, the altars feature dark-skinned saints, reflecting the congregation's African heritage. Free to visit.
An urban reservoir transformed into a public park featuring eight large sculptures of Candomblé orixás (spiritual deities) rising from the water, created by Bahian artist Tati Moreno. The sculptures — representing Oxalá, Oxóssi, Ogum, Xangô, Iansã, Oxum, Nanã and Iemanjá — stand between 3 and 4 metres tall and have become one of Salvador's most distinctive landmarks. The surrounding 2-kilometre paved pathway is popular with joggers, walkers and cyclists. The site represents Salvador's deep connection to Candomblé, the Afro-Brazilian religion brought by enslaved West Africans. Illuminated at night for atmospheric viewing. A vivid public expression of the religious and cultural traditions that define Salvador's identity. Free to visit.
