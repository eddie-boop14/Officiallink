The Brazilian side offers the panoramic view — a sweeping perspective across the entire 2.7-kilometre curtain of 275 individual falls plunging up to 82 metres into the Iguaçu River gorge. The main walkway extends 1.2 kilometres along the canyon rim to a viewing platform at the base of the Devil's Throat (Garganta do Diabo), where the mist from the most powerful section creates permanent rainbows on sunny days. The falls sit within Iguaçu National Park, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1986, protecting 185,000 hectares of Atlantic Forest. Flow rates peak between November and Marché when the river carries up to 12,750 cubic metres of water per second. Eleanor Roosevelt reportedly exclaimed "Poor Niagara!" upon seeing them. Allow half a day for the Brazilian side.
One of the largest remnants of Atlantic Forest in southern Brazil, the national park extends far beyond the waterfalls across 185,262 hectares of subtropical and tropical forest. The park supports over 400 bird species, 80 mammal species including jaguars, tapirs, and giant otters, and thousands of plant species. The Poço Preto trail offers a 9-kilometre walk through pristine forest with kayaking opportunities on the Iguaçu River. Macuco Safari combines a jungle drive with a boat ride directly to the base of the falls. The park was established in 1939, decades before the UNESCO inscription, recognising the ecological significance of this forest corridor. Park entry is included with the falls ticket. Coatis (raccoon-like mammals) are abundant — visitors should not feed them.
The most thrilling way to experience the falls — a three-part adventure beginning with an electric vehicle ride through the national park forest, followed by a short jungle trek with interpretation of the Atlantic Forest ecosystem, culminating in a high-speed inflatable boat ride directly into the spray zone at the base of the cascades. Passengers get thoroughly soaked as the boat navigates the rapids and approaches the curtain of falling water. Waterproof bags are provided for electronics. The entire experience takes approximately two hours. The forest section of the trip often yields wildlife sightings including toucans, butterflies and coatis. The boat ride operates year-round though water volume varies seasonally. Pre-booking recommended during high season.
Located at the entrance to Iguaçu National Park, this conservation centre houses over 1,400 birds of approximately 150 species within 16 hectares of Atlantic Forest. Large walk-through aviaries allow visitors to enter enclosures with macaws, toucans, flamingos and hummingbirds moving freely overhead. The park focuses on the conservation of endangered Atlantic Forest species and is the only institution in the world dedicated to conserving this specific biome's birdlife. Rescued birds that cannot be returned to the wild find permanent homes here. The butterfly house contains hundreds of native species. Educational programmes highlight the critical status of the Atlantic Forest, which has been reduced to approximately 12% of its original extent. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
A binational hydroelectric dam operated jointly by Brazil and Paraguay on the Paraná River, producing approximately 15% of Brazil's and 86% of Paraguay's electricity. The dam structure stretches 7,919 metres across the river and stands 196 metres high — the iron and steel used in construction could build 380 Eiffel Towers. Completed in 1984 after 16 years of construction involving over 40,000 workers. Multiple tour options range from a standard panoramic visit to extended technical tours accessing the interior of the powerhouse and the dam crest. The Friday and Saturday night illumination tour dramatically lights the spillway. Named a finalist for the American Society of Civil Engineers' Seven Wonders of the Modern World. Allow 2-3 hours depending on tour type.
The point where Brazil, Argentina and Paraguay meet at the confluence of the Iguaçu and Paraná rivers, marked by painted obelisks on each country's bank in their respective national colours. The Brazilian side has been developed into a cultural complex with shows celebrating the indigenous Guarani, Portuguese, Spanish and Afro-Brazilian heritage of the three nations. The viewing platform overlooks the river junction where the green waters of the Iguaçu meet the brown Paraná. Each country's marker is visible from the others across the water. The nightly show features traditional music and dance. The site illuminates at sunset for atmospheric viewing. Located 8 kilometres from Foz do Iguaçu city centre.
While the Brazilian side provides the panoramic view, the Argentine side offers an immersive experience — extensive walkway systems totalling over 3 kilometres bring visitors directly above, alongside and beneath individual cascades. The Upper Circuit follows the cliff edge above the falls, while the Lower Circuit descends to river level for close-up views. A separate narrow-gauge railway transports visitors to the Garganta del Diablo (Devil's Throat) walkway, which extends directly over the edge of the most powerful section where the spray creates a permanent cloud. Boats from Isla San Martín approach the falls from below. The Argentine park is larger and typically requires a full day. Border crossing from Brazil is straightforward with passport. Two days recommended to see both sides.
One of the largest Buddhist temples in Latin America, built by the Chinese community of Foz do Iguaçu and featuring traditional architecture with 120 handcrafted statues imported from China. The complex includes multiple buildings arranged around landscaped gardens with ornamental bridges, koi ponds and meditation spaces. The main hall contains a large gilded Buddha and intricate ceiling paintings. Foz do Iguaçu's unique position as a tri-border city has attracted communities from over 80 nationalities, and the temple reflects this multicultural character. The grounds offer a peaceful contrast to the dramatic energy of the waterfalls. Visitors are asked to dress modestly and maintain quiet respect. Free to visit. Allow 1 hour.
