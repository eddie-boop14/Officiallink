Completed in 1931 after nine years of construction, standing 30 metres tall with arms stretching 28 metres wide atop Corcovado mountain at 710 metres elevation. Designed by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and sculpted by French artist Paul Landowski, the statue is made of reinforced concrete clad in soapstone mosaic tiles. Reached via the Corcovado Rack Railway, Brazil's oldest tourist railway inaugurated in 1884, which travels through Tijuca National Park. A small chapel dedicated to Our Lady of Aparecida sits within the pedestal. Voted one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007. Pre-booking of timed entry is essential as daily visitor numbers are capped. Allow 1.5-2 hours for the full experience including train journey.
Rising 396 metres above Guanabara Bay, this granite and quartz monolith is reached by glass-walled cable car operating since 1912 — Brazil's first cable car and the third built worldwide. Two-stage ride ascends first to Morro da Urca (220 metres) where restaurants, bars and a performance venue await, then continues to the Sugarloaf summit for 360-degree panoramic views across Rio's coastline, Corcovado, and the surrounding mountains. Part of a UNESCO World Heritage Cultural Landscape inscribed in 2012. The surrounding natural monument protects significant Atlantic Forest remnants with hiking trails on Morro da Urca. Sunset sessions with live DJ performances are a signature experience. Named by 16th-century Portuguese sailors who thought its shape resembled traditional refined sugar moulds.
A 250-step staircase connecting Lapa to Santa Teresa neighbourhoods, covered with over 2,000 hand-placed tiles from more than 60 countries worldwide. Created by Chilean-born artist Jorge Selarón who began transforming the derelict steps in 1990, working continuously until his death in 2013. Selarón described the project as his tribute to the Brazilian people. The predominant colours are red, green and yellow — the colours of the Brazilian flag. Tiles were donated by visitors and collected by Selarón from travels around the world. The staircase has appeared in music videos, documentaries and international media. Free to visit at any time. Located between Rua Joaquim Silva and Rua Pinto Martins. Best photographed in morning light.
Covering 39 square kilometres within Rio's city limits, Tijuca is the world's largest urban rainforest and a remarkable example of ecological restoration. The original Atlantic Forest was cleared for coffee plantations in the early 19th century and replanted from 1861 under orders of Emperor Pedro II, making it one of the first large-scale reforestation projects in history. The park contains numerous waterfalls including Cascatinha Taunay, natural springs, and over 200 species of birds. Pedra da Gávea, a massive granite peak at 844 metres, offers one of Rio's most dramatic hikes with panoramic ocean views. The park encompasses Corcovado mountain and Christ the Redeemer. Free to enter. Multiple trail systems range from easy walks to challenging rock scrambles.
Stretching 4 kilometres in a sweeping crescent between Forte de Copacabana and Leme, this is one of the world's most recognised urban beaches. The distinctive black-and-white Portuguese wave-pattern promenade was designed by Roberto Burle Marx. Each New Year's Eve, over three million people gather on the sand for one of the planet's largest celebrations. The beach is divided into numbered postos (lifeguard stations) that serve as landmarks — each attracting different crowds and activities. Forte de Copacabana at the southern end houses a military museum and a café with exceptional beach views. The neighbourhood behind the beach contains a dense concentration of hotels, restaurants and shops. Free public access along the entire length.
Made world-famous by the 1962 bossa nova song "The Girl from Ipanema" by Antonio Carlos Jobim and Vinicius de Moraes, this 1.5-kilometre beach sits between the Arpoador rocks and Jardim de Alá canal. Different postos attract distinct crowds — Posto 9 is popular with younger beachgoers and artists, while Posto 8 draws a mixed crowd. The dramatic Two Brothers mountain (Morro Dois Irmãos) provides the backdrop at the western end. Sunset from Arpoador rock at the eastern tip draws nightly applause from gathered crowds. The surrounding neighbourhood features upscale boutiques, restaurants and galleries along Rua Garcia d'Ávila. Every Sunday, Praça General Osório hosts the Hippie Fair with crafts and souvenirs.
Opened in 2015 in the revitalised Porto Maravilha waterfront district, designed by Spanish neofuturistic architect Santiago Calatrava. The striking building extends over a reflecting pool on Pier Mauá with a cantilevered structure inspired by the bromeliads of Rio's Botanical Garden. The permanent exhibition is structured around five major questions — Cosmos, Earth, Anthropocene, Tomorrows, and Us — using immersive multimedia installations developed with partners including NASA, Google and MIT. The museum focuses on sustainability, climate change and humanity's relationship with the planet. Over four million visitors in its first years of operation. Located at Praça Mauá alongside the MAR (Rio Art Museum). Allow 2-3 hours minimum.
Inaugurated in 2013 as part of Rio's port revitalisation, occupying two connected buildings — a restored 1916 palace and a former bus terminal — linked by a dramatic undulating concrete roof designed by Bernardes Arquitetura. The museum hosts wide-ranging exhibitions exploring Rio's visual culture, from colonial art to contemporary works, with particular emphasis on the city's social and cultural diversity. The rooftop offers panoramic views across Guanabara Bay. Located on Praça Mauá adjacent to the Museum of Tomorrow. The building's transformation from colonial-era architecture to modern museum exemplifies the regeneration of Rio's historic port district. Educational programmes focus on art and visual culture for schools and communities.
Housed in an imposing 1908 building designed by Adolfo Morales de los Ríos in the style of the Louvre, containing nearly 20,000 works spanning paintings, sculptures, drawings and prints. The core collection originates from Portuguese King João VI, who brought his art collection to Brazil when fleeing Napoleon's invasion in 1808. Highlights include significant European and Brazilian art from the 17th to 20th centuries. The building's neoclassical façade and grand interior galleries on Avenida Rio Branco reflect Rio's Belle Époque ambitions. The collection represents the most comprehensive survey of Brazilian art history in the country. Located in the cultural corridor of central Rio alongside the Municipal Theatre and National Library.
One of Brazil's most visited cultural centres, housed in a beautifully restored 1906 neoclassical building that originally served as a commercial exchange. Hosts major international exhibitions, film screenings, theatre performances and concerts across multiple floors. The building features ornate marble interiors, a rotunda with stained glass dome, and art deco details. Located in the historic centre near Praça XV, surrounded by colonial-era architecture. Consistently ranks among the world's most visited art centres. The programme emphasises accessibility with many exhibitions offered free of charge. The building itself is an architectural highlight of Rio's downtown cultural corridor.
Officially named Estádio Jornalista Mário Filho, built for the 1950 FIFA World Cup when it held nearly 200,000 spectators — a world record attendance of 199,854 watched the 1950 final between Brazil and Uruguay. Renovated for the 2014 World Cup with current capacity of 78,838, it also hosted the opening and closing ceremonies of the 2016 Olympics. The guided tour visits the pitch, changing rooms, warm-up area, players' tunnel and press conference room, alongside a museum displaying memorabilia from Pelé, Garrincha, Zico and other legends. Tours last approximately 50 minutes. Home ground for Flamengo and Fluminense. Has hosted concerts by Frank Sinatra, Tina Turner and Paul McCartney drawing crowds exceeding 180,000 in its original configuration.
Designed by celebrated Brazilian architect Oscar Niemeyer and completed in 1984, this purpose-built parade venue stretches 700 metres through central Rio with grandstands seating approximately 90,000 spectators. During Carnival, Rio's top samba schools compete here in elaborately choreographed parades with thousands of costumed dancers, enormous floats and live percussion. The competition takes place over two main nights with the top six schools of each division performing. Outside Carnival season, the venue hosts concerts and cultural events. The complex includes the Carnival Memorial Centre documenting the history and artistry of Rio's samba schools. The stands are divided into numbered sectors with varying proximity to the parade route.
Founded in 1808 by Regent Prince Dom João to acclimatise spices from the East Indies, opened to the public in 1822. The 140-hectare garden contains over 7,000 species of tropical and subtropical plants across themed collections including a celebrated orchid nursery with 600 species. The iconic Avenue of Royal Palms, planted with towering imperial palms, forms the main approach. Features a Japanese garden, medicinal plant section, sensory garden, and Victorian-era greenhouses. The garden borders Tijuca National Park and is home to wild marmosets, toucans and other native wildlife. Albert Einstein visited during his 1925 trip to Brazil. Functions as an active botanical research centre under Brazil's Ministry of the Environment. Allow 2-3 hours.
A public park surrounding a 1920s Italianate mansion at the base of Corcovado mountain, now housing the School of Visual Arts. The palatial building features a central courtyard with a pool and café surrounded by arched colonnades, with Christ the Redeemer visible rising directly behind — one of Rio's most photographed compositions. The surrounding grounds include walking trails through Atlantic Forest remnants, a small aquarium built into rock caves, and gardens with tropical vegetation. The starting point for one of the hiking trails to Christ the Redeemer passes through the park. Free to enter the grounds. The mansion's eclectic architecture mixes Renaissance, Roman and art deco elements, commissioned by industrialist Henrique Lage in the early 20th century.
A striking modernist cathedral completed in 1979 after 15 years of construction, designed by Edgar Fonseca in the form of a truncated cone rising 75 metres high with a diameter of 106 metres at the base. The interior is illuminated by four enormous stained glass windows stretching from floor to ceiling in cruciform pattern, each 64 metres tall. The building can accommodate 20,000 standing or 5,000 seated. The design deliberately breaks from traditional cathedral architecture, reflecting Brazil's modernist movement. A subterranean Sacred Art Museum contains historical religious artefacts. Dedicated to São Sebastião, the patron saint of Rio de Janeiro. Located in the Lapa district near the Arcos da Lapa aqueduct.
Built in the mid-18th century to carry fresh water from the Carioca River to the city centre, this imposing double-arched aqueduct spans 270 metres with 42 arches rising to 17.6 metres. Since 1896, the top of the aqueduct has carried the Santa Teresa tramway (Bondinho de Santa Teresa), one of Rio's most charming historic transport links connecting the bohemian hilltop neighbourhood of Santa Teresa with the city centre. The structure is the most prominent surviving example of colonial-era infrastructure in Rio. The surrounding Lapa neighbourhood is the epicentre of Rio's nightlife and live music scene, with bars and clubs hosting samba and forró performances. Free to view. The tram ride across the arches offers elevated views of the surrounding district.
South America's largest marine aquarium, opened in 2016 in the revitalised Porto Maravilha waterfront zone. Houses approximately 350 species and thousands of marine animals across 28 themed tanks totalling over 4.5 million litres of water. Highlights include a 3.5-million-litre open ocean tank with sharks, rays and large fish visible through a panoramic viewing tunnel. The exhibition follows a journey through Brazilian aquatic ecosystems from the Amazon basin to open ocean. Located adjacent to the Museum of Tomorrow on Praça Mauá. Interactive touch pools and educational programmes available. Research partnerships focus on marine conservation and breeding programmes. Allow 2-3 hours for the full circuit.
