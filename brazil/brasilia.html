The defining symbol of Brasília, designed by Oscar Niemeyer and completed in 1960, featuring twin 28-storey office towers flanked by two contrasting domes — the shallow concave bowl of the Senate and the deeper convex cup of the Chamber of Deputies. The design concept represents the balance of legislative power. The building sits at the eastern end of the Esplanada dos Ministérios (Esplanade of Ministries), forming the focal point of Lúcio Costa's master plan. Guided tours visit the plenary chambers, halls and art collection including works by major Brazilian artists. The reflecting pools and surrounding lawns create dramatic architectural photographs at any time of day. The building exemplifies the Brazilian modernist movement's ambition to express democratic ideals through architectural form.
The official residence of the President of Brazil, completed in 1958 as the first permanent structure built in Brasília. Designed by Oscar Niemeyer, the palace is distinguished by its external colonnade of inverted arches in white marble — a motif that became the symbol of Brasília itself and appears on the Federal District's coat of arms. The building sits on the shore of Lago Paranoá (Paranoá Lake) surrounded by landscaped gardens by Roberto Burle Marx. The chapel, Ermida Dom Bosco, was the first building in the new capital. Public access is restricted to external viewing from the guard perimeter, though the iconic columns are best appreciated from outside. The name "Alvorada" means dawn, symbolising the new era the capital represented.
The working headquarters of the President of Brazil, located on the north side of the Praça dos Três Poderes (Three Powers Square) — the civic heart of Brasília where the executive, legislative and judicial branches face each other across an open triangular plaza. Designed by Oscar Niemeyer with the same inverted-arch colonnade as the Alvorada palace. The ceremonial ramp where the president ascends to the main entrance is a deliberate architectural statement — visible from across the square, it stages democratic transitions as public spectacle. Guided tours on Sundays visit the main halls, Noble Hall and art collection. The square also contains the Panteão da Pátria (National Pantheon) and Oscar Niemeyer's Pombal sculpture.
Completing the trio of government buildings on the Praça dos Três Poderes, the Supreme Federal Tribunal occupies the south side of the square. Designed by Oscar Niemeyer, the building features a distinctive facade with a blindfolded Justice sculpture by Alfredo Ceschiatti positioned at the entrance. The architectural dialogue between the three buildings — Congress, Planalto Palace and the Tribunal — physically embodies the separation of powers enshrined in the Brazilian constitution. The interior houses significant artworks and the plenary chamber where Brazil's highest judicial decisions are made. Guided tours available. The Three Powers Square itself is an open-air modernist sculpture park with works by Bruno Giorgi, Alfredo Ceschiatti and Niemeyer himself.
Completed in 1970, Niemeyer's hyperboloid structure consists of 16 identical curved concrete columns weighing 90 tonnes each, arranged in a circle and joined at the top, creating the impression of a crown or hands reaching toward heaven. Between the columns, stained glass panels by Marianne Peretti flood the interior with blue, green and bronze light. Four bronze angels by Alfredo Ceschiatti are suspended from the ceiling. Visitors enter through a darkened underground passage — a deliberate transition designed to make the sudden explosion of light and space in the main nave overwhelming. The cathedral can accommodate 4,000 people. Three aluminium angels by Ceschiatti are suspended at different heights. Located on the Esplanade of Ministries. Free to visit.
Named after the Italian priest Saint John Bosco, who in 1883 reportedly had a prophetic dream about a great civilisation arising between the 15th and 20th parallels — the latitudes where Brasília was built. The church's walls are composed entirely of 80 stained glass panels in 12 shades of blue, from deep violet to pale sky, designed by artist Cláudio Naves Filho. When sunlight streams through, the interior is bathed in an ethereal blue glow that shifts throughout the day. A massive Murano glass chandelier weighing 2.5 tonnes hangs from the centre. The architectural style is Gothic-modern, designed by Carlos Alberto Naves. The luminous blue interior creates one of the most striking religious spaces in the Americas. Free to visit.
A pristine white dome designed by Oscar Niemeyer, opened in 2006 on the Esplanada dos Ministérios as part of the Cultural Complex of the Republic alongside the National Library. The semi-spherical structure, 26 metres high and 70 metres in diameter, hosts rotating exhibitions of modern and contemporary Brazilian and international art in a single vast circular gallery. The building's pure geometric form — a half-sphere connected to the ground by a sweeping ramp — demonstrates Niemeyer's signature approach of reducing architecture to essential curves. The ramp entrance and external plaza are used for public gatherings and cultural events. Adjacent to the National Library, designed by the same architect. The building is particularly photogenic against Brasília's characteristic wide skies.
Covering 420 hectares — larger than New York's Central Park — this is one of the largest urban parks in the world and the green heart of Brasília. Named after the wife of President Juscelino Kubitschek, who conceived and built the capital. The park contains artificial lakes, swimming pools, sports courts, cycling and running paths, go-kart tracks, playgrounds, and an equestrian centre. Designed as integral to Lúcio Costa's master plan, providing recreational green space for a city built from scratch in the late 1950s. The park hosts weekend markets, outdoor concerts and community events. Brasília's characteristic cerrado (tropical savanna) vegetation mixes with planted areas. Free to enter. A gathering point for Brasília residents, particularly on weekends.
Spanning 1,200 metres across Lago Paranoá, this award-winning bridge features three 60-metre-high asymmetric steel arches that cross and support the roadway from alternating sides, creating a distinctive crisscrossing silhouette. Designed by architect Alexandre Chan and structural engineer Mário Vila Verde, completed in 2002. Named after Juscelino Kubitschek, the president who built Brasília. The bridge won the IABSE Outstanding Structure Award in 2003. Dramatically illuminated at night, the arches glow against the dark sky and reflect in the lake waters. The bridge has become a contemporary symbol of the city alongside Niemeyer's older monuments. Best viewed and photographed from the lake shore or during evening illumination. Pedestrian and cycling paths run alongside the roadway.
The ceremonial central axis of Brasília, a broad boulevard flanked by 17 identical ministerial buildings stretching from the Television Tower to the Three Powers Square. This is the monumental spine of Lúcio Costa's Plano Piloto (Pilot Plan), the airplane-shaped city layout that earned Brasília its UNESCO World Heritage inscription in 1987 — the youngest city ever designated. The esplanade was conceived as a civic space for democratic expression, and major demonstrations and celebrations take place along its length. The scale is deliberately imposing — designed for a capital city viewed from automobiles rather than on foot. The Television Tower at the western end offers an observation deck with views along the entire axis. Free to walk. Best experienced by starting at the Congress building and walking westward.
