Spanning approximately 150,000 square kilometres across the states of Mato Grosso and Mato Grosso do Sul — larger than England — the Pantanal is the world's largest tropical wetland. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2000, the ecosystem's seasonal flooding cycle transforms the landscape: during the wet season (November to March), up to 80% of the floodplain submerges, concentrating wildlife along elevated areas. During the dry season (May to October), receding waters create pools teeming with fish that attract massive gatherings of predators and birds. The Pantanal contains the highest concentration of wildlife in the Americas, with animal densities exceeding those of the Amazon. Access is primarily via Cuiabá (northern Pantanal) or Campo Grande (southern Pantanal). Most visitors stay at fazendas (ranches) converted into eco-lodges.
A 145-kilometre raised dirt road running from Poconé to Porto Jofre through the heart of the northern Pantanal, crossing 122 wooden bridges over streams and wetlands. Originally planned as a trans-Pantanal highway in the 1970s, construction was abandoned, making this unfinished road one of the world's greatest wildlife drives. Caimans, capybaras, giant anteaters, anacondas, marsh deer and hundreds of bird species are routinely visible from the road. The southern terminus at Porto Jofre on the Cuiabá River is the primary base for jaguar-spotting boat excursions. The road is accessible year-round in 4x4 vehicles though conditions deteriorate in the wet season. Multiple eco-lodges are scattered along the route. Self-driving is possible but guided safaris maximise wildlife encounters.
The remote settlement at the southern end of the Transpantaneira road has become the world's premier destination for observing jaguars in the wild. Boat-based safaris along the Cuiabá, Piquirí and Três Irmãos rivers offer sighting success rates exceeding 90% during the dry season (June to October), when jaguars hunt along riverbanks as caimans and capybaras congregate near remaining water. The Pantanal's jaguar population is one of the densest on Earth, with individuals habituated to boats making close observation possible without disturbance. Several eco-lodges operate boat safari programmes with experienced local guides. The area also supports giant otters, ocelots, and tapirs. The best months are August and September when water levels are lowest and wildlife is most concentrated.
The main access route into the southern Pantanal, this 120-kilometre road from Buraco das Piranhas to Porto da Manga traverses some of the most scenically dramatic wetland landscapes in the region. Unlike the Transpantaneira's slightly elevated profile, sections of the Estrada Parque flood seasonally, and the road passes through vast open marshes with sweeping horizons. The southern Pantanal offers a different ecosystem character — more open grassland and shallow lakes compared to the forested northern section. Traditional pantaneiro cowboy culture is more visible here, with working cattle ranches continuing centuries-old herding traditions. The road connects with the Miranda and Aquidauana rivers, important waterways for boat-based wildlife excursions. 4x4 vehicle essential.
Capital of Mato Grosso state and the primary gateway city for the northern Pantanal, with an international airport receiving flights from São Paulo, Brasília and other major cities. The city sits at the geographic centre of South America, marked by a monument in the central district. The municipal market (Mercado do Porto) offers regional cuisine including traditional Pantanal dishes like pacu fish and arroz com guariroba. From Cuiabá, it is 100 kilometres to the start of the Transpantaneira road at Poconé. Tour operators based in the city organise multi-day Pantanal excursions including transfers, lodge accommodation and guided activities. The Chapada dos Guimarães national park, featuring dramatic sandstone formations, is 65 kilometres northeast and is often combined with Pantanal visits.
Known as the ecotourism capital of Brazil, this small town on the edge of the southern Pantanal is celebrated for its limestone-filtered rivers of extraordinary clarity — visibility reaches 50 metres in some stretches, allowing snorkellers to float among schools of native fish. The Rio da Prata and Rio Sucuri offer guided float snorkelling through submerged forests. The Gruta do Lago Azul (Blue Lake Cave) contains an underground lake of intense blue colour within a limestone cavern. Bonito pioneered Brazil's sustainable tourism model — daily visitor numbers to each attraction are strictly limited, advance booking is mandatory, and all activities require certified local guides. The system has successfully maintained pristine conditions despite growing tourism. Located 300 kilometres from Campo Grande.
A national park 65 kilometres from Cuiabá featuring dramatic sandstone cliffs, rock formations and the 86-metre Véu de Noiva (Bridal Veil) waterfall — the park's most photographed feature. The plateau rises 800 metres above the surrounding Pantanal lowlands and is composed of ancient sandstone formations dating back hundreds of millions of years, eroded into towers, arches and canyon walls of vivid red and orange. The park sits within the cerrado (tropical savanna) biome and contains cave paintings estimated at 4,000 years old. The Mirante viewpoint offers panoramic views across the vast flatlands extending toward the Pantanal. Often combined with a Pantanal trip as a complementary landscape experience. Guided trails are required within the national park. Allow a full day.
The traditional way to experience the Pantanal is by staying at a fazenda — a working cattle ranch that has opened its doors to eco-tourism. These ranches have operated in the Pantanal for over 200 years, and their cowboys (pantaneiros) possess unmatched knowledge of the wetland's wildlife, waterways and seasonal rhythms. Typical activities include horseback riding through flooded plains, piranha fishing, nocturnal caiman spotting by torchlight, birdwatching at dawn, and boat excursions along rivers and lagoons. Lodges range from rustic to comfortable and most offer all-inclusive packages with meals, transfers from gateway cities, and guided activities. The immersive multi-day format — typically 3-5 nights — is essential for meaningful wildlife encounters, as the Pantanal rewards patience and repeated observation.
