About

The Musée national du Moyen Âge — Cluny occupies one of the most distinctive sites in Paris: a 15th-century Gothic mansion built by the abbots of Cluny over the intact ruins of 2nd-century Gallo-Roman thermal baths in the Latin Quarter. The baths, known as the Thermes de Lutetia, were constructed in the 1st or 2nd century CE and represent the most substantial visible Roman structure surviving in Paris. The frigidarium — the cold room — stands to a height of 14 metres and retains its original barrel vault. The adjacent Hôtel de Cluny was built in 1330 by Pierre de Chalus, abbot of Cluny, as a Parisian residence for abbots visiting from the great Burgundian monastery. The current building dates largely from a rebuilding in the late 15th century and is one of the finest remaining examples of Flamboyant Gothic civil architecture in France. A contemporary reception pavilion designed by architect Bernard Desmoulin was added in 2018, and the museum reopened after renovation in 2022.

The permanent collection spans roughly 1,000 years of medieval history across nearly 1,600 objects. The central masterpiece is the Lady and the Unicorn, a series of six tapestries woven in Flanders around 1500 and considered among the most significant works of medieval art in existence. Five panels represent the five senses; the sixth — inscribed "À mon seul désir" — is interpreted as representing a sixth sense of free will or love. The tapestries are housed in a dedicated circular room on the upper floor. Other collection highlights include the original heads of the Kings of Judah from Notre-Dame Cathedral (decapitated during the Revolution), stained glass panels from the Sainte-Chapelle, Limousin enamels, ivory carvings, medieval goldsmithing, and an extensive collection of stained glass — the largest in any French museum. A medieval garden is accessible from the courtyard.

The full-price ticket is €14 for adults, covering permanent collections and current temporary exhibitions. The reduced rate is €12 for qualifying categories. Admission is free for under-18s, EU residents aged 18–25, jobseekers, and all visitors on the first Sunday of every month. The Paris Museum Pass is accepted. The museum offers late opening until 21:00 on the 1st and 3rd Thursday of each month. Tickets are purchased at billetterie.musee-moyenage.fr.

FAQ
How much does the Musée de Cluny cost?
€14 full price, €12 reduced rate. Free for under-18s, EU residents aged 18–25, jobseekers, and all visitors on the first Sunday of every month. Paris Museum Pass accepted. Book at billetterie.musee-moyenage.fr. Resellers charge €18–€30 for the same entry — there is no added value.
What are the Musée de Cluny opening hours?
Tuesday to Sunday 9:30–18:15. Ticket desk closes at 17:30, last entry at 17:45. Late opening until 21:00 on the 1st and 3rd Thursday of each month (desk closes 20:30). Closed every Monday, 1 January, 1 May, and 25 December. Early closing at 17:00 on 24 and 31 December.
What is the Lady and the Unicorn tapestry?
The Lady and the Unicorn is a series of six 15th-century tapestries woven in Flanders, considered one of the most significant surviving works of medieval art. Five panels represent the five senses; the sixth is interpreted as depicting a sixth sense of love or understanding. The tapestries are displayed in a dedicated rotunda on the upper floor of the museum. They form the centrepiece of the permanent collection at musee-moyenage.fr.
How do I get to the Musée de Cluny?
Metro line 10 to Cluny-La Sorbonne, or line 4 to Saint-Michel or Odéon. RER B to Cluny-La Sorbonne, or RER C to Saint-Michel. Buses 21, 27, 38, 47, 63, 85, 86 and 87 serve the area. Address: 28 rue du Sommerard, 75005 Paris.
Are there late openings at the Musée de Cluny?
Yes. The museum stays open until 21:00 on the 1st and 3rd Thursday of each month. The ticket desk closes at 20:30 on those evenings. Regular hours on all other days are Tuesday–Sunday 9:30–18:15. Check musee-moyenage.fr for the current schedule before visiting.
