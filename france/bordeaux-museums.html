The World's Only Museum Dedicated to Wine Civilisation

📍 🚊 Cité du Vin
La Cité du Vin opened in 2016 in a sculptural building designed to evoke swirling wine in a glass, located on the Garonne riverfront in the Bassins à Flot district. The permanent exhibition spans 3,000 square metres across 20 thematic chapters tracing the history, geography, culture, and civilisations of wine worldwide. Every standard admission ticket includes a wine tasting at the panoramic Belvédère on the 8th floor, with views across the city. The experience combines immersive multimedia installations with physical artefacts — it is designed as a sensory journey, not a conventional museum. Advance booking is strongly recommended, particularly in summer and for weekend visits.
🇫🇷 Bassins des Lumières
Europe's Largest Digital Art Centre — WWII Submarine Base
€12.50
€19–€31
→
📍
Bassins des Lumières occupies four of the submarine pens of the Base sous-marine de Bordeaux, a massive reinforced concrete structure built by the German navy during the Second World War on the left bank of the Garonne. The venue, operated by Culturespaces, projects large-scale digital artworks across the walls, ceilings, and water surface of the pens — each roughly 110 metres long and 11 metres high. The scale is genuinely exceptional; the effect of images reflected in dark water at this size is unlike conventional gallery or cinema experiences. Programming rotates two or three times a year with major exhibitions typically dedicated to a single artist or movement. Advance booking recommended for weekend and holiday sessions.

📍 🚊 Musée d'Aquitaine
The Musée d'Aquitaine traces the human history of the Bordeaux region from prehistoric settlements through Gallo-Roman Burdigala, the medieval wine trade, the Atlantic slave trade, and colonial Bordeaux up to the 20th century. The collection of approximately 80,000 objects includes some of the most significant prehistoric Venus figurines found in France, Gallo-Roman sculptures, and an important section covering the slave trade that does not minimise Bordeaux's historic role as one of France's leading slaving ports. No online ticketing — tickets are sold at the door only on the day of visit. Free entry on the first Sunday of each month (except July and August). Allow 2–3 hours.

📍 🚊 CAPC
The CAPC occupies the Entrepôt Lainé, a vast early 19th-century warehouse originally used to store colonial goods. The central nave — 70 metres long with a vaulted iron roof — is one of the most architecturally striking exhibition spaces in France. The CAPC was one of the first institutions in France to give substantial exhibition space to contemporary art, opening in 1973 before becoming a permanent museum in 1984. The collection focuses on works from the 1960s to the present, with particular strengths in minimal and conceptual art. No online ticketing — tickets sold at the door. The café and bookshop in the nave are worth visiting independently. Allow 1.5–2 hours.
🇫🇷 Musée des Beaux-Arts (MusBA)
Six Centuries of European Painting — Palais Rohan Gardens
€6/8
€9–€15
→
📍
The Musée des Beaux-Arts occupies the north and south gallery wings flanking the gardens of the Palais Rohan — the former archbishop's palace that now serves as the city hall. The collection spans European painting and sculpture from the 15th century to the early 20th century, with notable works by Titian, Rubens, Van Dyck, Delacroix, and Renoir. The museum explicitly states it does not offer online ticketing; all tickets are purchased at the reception desk on the day of visit. Free entry on the first Sunday of each month (except July and August). Allow 1.5–2 hours.
🇫🇷 Musée Mer Marine
Maritime Heritage & Ocean Exploration — Bacalan District
€14
€21–€35
→
⚠️ Needs verify
Opened in 2019 in the Bacalan district near the Cité du Vin, this privately funded museum covers maritime history, ocean exploration, and marine ecosystems. The 13,000-square-metre space displays ship models, navigational instruments, maritime art, underwater photography, and a large collection of ethnographic objects from seafaring cultures. The building itself — a striking modernist black volume — is a Bacalan landmark. Temporary exhibitions complement the permanent collection. A natural companion to the Cité du Vin. Closed Mondays. Allow 1.5–2 hours.
🇫🇷 Muséum de Bordeaux — Sciences et Nature
Natural History Museum — Jardin Public
€6
€9–€15
→
⚠️ Needs verify
Bordeaux's natural history museum, completely renovated and reopened in 2019 after 10 years of work. Located in the Jardin Public, the museum presents the natural world through interactive, family-friendly exhibitions covering biodiversity, evolution, and environmental challenges. The building is a handsome 19th-century structure. The Jardin Public itself is worth exploring — a 10-hectare English-style garden dating from 1746. Closed Mondays. Allow 1–1.5 hours for the museum plus time in the garden.
🇫🇷 Musée des Arts Décoratifs et du Design
Decorative Arts & Design — Hôtel de Lalande
€4.50
€7–€11
→
⚠️ Needs verify
Housed in an elegant 18th-century hôtel particulier near the Cathédrale Saint-André, this museum displays French decorative arts: furniture, ceramics, glassware, metalwork, and textiles from the 18th and 19th centuries. The rooms themselves are period settings — you walk through a recreated 18th-century Bordeaux interior. The ground floor hosts contemporary design exhibitions. A quieter alternative to the bigger museums. Closed Mondays and Tuesdays. Allow 45 minutes–1 hour.
