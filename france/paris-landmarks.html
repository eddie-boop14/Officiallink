One of the most opulent buildings in Paris, designed by Charles Garnier and inaugurated in 1875. The Beaux-Arts exterior conceals an even more lavish interior: the grand staircase in polychrome marble, the foyer with its mirrors and chandeliers, and the auditorium with its ceiling painted by Marc Chagall in 1964. Gaston Leroux's Phantom of the Opera was inspired by the building's underground lake. Self-guided visits of the public areas are available during the day when no rehearsals are scheduled. The gift shop sells excellent architecture books. Allow 1–1.5 hours for a visit.
Located in the Hôtel Salé, a magnificent 17th-century mansion in the Marais, this museum holds the world's largest public collection of Picasso's work. The collection was acquired by the French state in lieu of inheritance tax after Picasso's death in 1973 and spans every period and medium of his career. The mansion's grand staircase and period architecture provide a striking counterpoint to Picasso's modernist work. Allow 1.5–2 hours.
A purpose-built gallery in the Jardin des Tuileries housing Claude Monet's eight monumental Water Lilies murals in two oval rooms. Monet donated the paintings to France the day after the Armistice in 1918 as a monument to peace. The lower level houses the Walter-Guillaume collection of Impressionist and Post-Impressionist works. The natural daylight in the oval rooms changes the paintings throughout the day. Allow 1–1.5 hours.
The hilltop village of Montmartre retains a distinct atmosphere from the rest of Paris — narrow cobbled streets, vineyard, windmills, and the Place du Tertre where artists paint portraits. The Sacré-Cœur basilica at the summit, built from 1875 in Romano-Byzantine style using travertine that bleaches whiter with rain, offers one of the most iconic views over Paris. The funicular railway saves the climb. The neighbourhood's artistic heritage includes Picasso, Modigliani, and Van Gogh, who all lived and worked here. The area around Rue Lepic and Rue des Abbesses is the most authentic. Allow 2–3 hours.
An underground ossuary holding the remains of approximately six million people, transferred from overflowing Parisian cemeteries between 1786 and 1860. The public tour covers 1.5 kilometres of tunnels through the former limestone quarries beneath the Left Bank. The bones are stacked in decorative arrangements along the passage walls, with skull-and-crossbone patterns and inscriptions. The temperature underground is a constant 14°C. Queues can be very long — book online with timed entry. The experience is atmospheric rather than macabre. Allow 45 minutes underground plus queuing time.
The most visited cemetery in the world, opened in 1804 and named after Louis XIV's confessor. Over 70,000 burial plots cover 44 hectares of hilly, tree-lined pathways. Notable graves include Jim Morrison, Oscar Wilde (with its lipstick-covered tombstone), Edith Piaf, Frédéric Chopin, Marcel Proust, Molière, and Colette. The Mur des Fédérés, where the last Communards were shot in 1871, is a site of left-wing pilgrimage. Free entry. Maps of notable graves are available at the entrance gates. Allow 1.5–2 hours for a thorough exploration.
Multiple operators run sightseeing cruises along the Seine, passing under 37 bridges and past most of Paris's major monuments. Bateaux Mouches (from Pont de l'Alma), Bateaux Parisiens (from the Eiffel Tower), and Vedettes du Pont-Neuf (from the Île de la Cité) are the main operators. Day cruises last approximately one hour. Evening dinner cruises run 2–3 hours. The Seine was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1991 along with the banks and monuments. The perspective from the water is unique. Book directly with the operator to avoid reseller markups.
Europe's largest contemporary art space, located in the Art Deco wing of the Palais de Tokyo near the Eiffel Tower. Unlike a traditional museum, the Palais de Tokyo has no permanent collection — instead, the vast raw concrete spaces host ambitious temporary exhibitions, installations, and performances by emerging and established contemporary artists. The building is open until midnight, making it one of the few art venues for evening visits. The bookshop and restaurant (Monsieur Bleu) are excellent. Allow 1–2 hours.
A contemporary art museum designed by Frank Gehry, opened in 2014 in the Bois de Boulogne. The building itself — twelve glass sails billowing around a central structure — is the main attraction, a masterpiece of deconstructivist architecture. The foundation hosts temporary exhibitions from the LVMH corporate collection and international loans, along with music and performance events. The rooftop terraces offer views over the Bois and the Paris skyline. Accessible by a free shuttle from Place Charles de Gaulle. Allow 1.5–2 hours.
France's national stadium in Saint-Denis, built for the 1998 FIFA World Cup. Capacity 80,000 for football and rugby, expandable to 90,000 for concerts. The stadium hosted the 1998 World Cup final (France 3–0 Brazil) and the 2007 Rugby World Cup final. Guided tours cover the pitch, dressing rooms, and presidential suite. Accessible by RER B or metro line 13 to Saint-Denis. The stadium was a venue for the 2024 Paris Olympic Games. Allow 1.5 hours for a guided tour.
