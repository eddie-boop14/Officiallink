About

The Musée d'Orsay occupies the Gare d'Orsay, a Beaux-Arts railway terminal built on the Left Bank of the Seine for the 1900 Paris World Fair. The station was decommissioned in 1939 — its platforms too short for modern trains — and spent decades as a venue for auctions, theatre and even an Orson Welles film set before President Giscard d'Estaing saved it from demolition in 1977. Italian architect Gae Aulenti redesigned the interior, and the museum opened in December 1986.

The collection covers the period from 1848 to 1914 — bridging the gap between the Louvre (pre-1848) and the Centre Pompidou (post-1914). The upper-floor Impressionist galleries are among the most celebrated in the world: Monet's series paintings, Renoir's Moulin de la Galette, Degas's dancers, Van Gogh's self-portraits and Bedroom, Cézanne's Card Players and Apples and Oranges, Gauguin's Tahitian works. The ground floor holds the monumental sculptures and early realist paintings of Courbet and Manet. The middle level covers Art Nouveau and symbolism. Around 3 million people visit each year across 16,000 m² of gallery space displaying some 4,000 works.

The official ticket is €16 online — a timed entry ticket that provides direct access to the entrance queue, which resellers market as "skip-the-line." On Thursday evenings from 18:00 the rate drops to €12. The museum is currently undergoing renovation of its entrance forecourt (March 2026 through summer 2028) — the collections remain fully accessible throughout, but access routes may differ from older guides. Any exit is final — plan your visit in advance.

FAQ
How much does the Musée d'Orsay cost?
€16 for adults booked online at musee-orsay.fr. Thursday evenings from 18:00 the rate drops to €12. Free for under-18s worldwide, EU residents under 26, and all visitors on the first Sunday of each month. Also free on 14 July. Resellers charge €25–€65 for the exact same timed entry ticket — there is no added value.
What are the opening hours?
Tuesday to Sunday 9:30–18:00 (last entry 17:00, galleries close 17:30). Thursday late opening until 21:45 (last entry 21:00, galleries close 21:15). Closed every Monday, 1 May and 25 December. Entrance forecourt renovation underway March 2026–summer 2028 — all galleries remain open.
What is the best time to visit?
Thursday evening after 18:00 — fewer crowds, €12 ticket, and the light through the station's giant clock face at sunset is one of Paris's distinctive sights. Wednesday and Friday mornings are the next best option. Avoid Saturday and avoid Tuesday, when Louvre closures push crowds here.
Is the entrance forecourt renovation affecting visits?
The entrance forecourt is under renovation from March 2026 through summer 2028. All galleries remain fully open throughout. Access points differ from older guides and photos — check musee-orsay.fr before visiting for current entry routes.
Is there an audio guide?
An audio guide is available for €6 on-site, covering 300+ commentaries in 10 languages. It is collected after the entrance check and is an add-on to admission — not included in the base ticket price. Book your admission at billetterie.musee-orsay.fr.
