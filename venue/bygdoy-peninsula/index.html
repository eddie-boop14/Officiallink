About

Bygdøy is a peninsula extending into the Oslofjord approximately 3 km west of Oslo city centre. The area is characterised by a combination of major cultural institutions, Royal property, and public outdoor recreational spaces. The peninsula is home to five significant museums: the Viking Ship Museum (currently closed for major expansion as the Viking Age Museum), the Fram Museum, the Kon-Tiki Museum, the Norwegian Maritime Museum, and the Norwegian Museum of Cultural History (Norsk Folkemuseum), one of the world's largest open-air museums with over 155 relocated historic buildings.

The southern tip of the peninsula contains two beaches, Huk and Paradisbukta. Huk is a larger facility with designated areas including a naturist section, accessible via a path through the woods. Paradisbukta is a smaller, sheltered cove on the eastern shore. Both beaches are free to access and open to the public. The Royal Farm (Kongsgården) at Bygdøy has been a Royal residence since the 1840s; it remains a working farm on the western side of the peninsula and is not open to visitors.

Public transport to Bygdøy operates via Bus 30 from Nationaltheatret and Oslo S year-round, with a journey time of approximately 15 minutes. From late April through September, a seasonal ferry service (Ruter line 91) runs from Rådhusbrygge 3, directly in front of Oslo City Hall, to two stops on the peninsula — Dronningen and Bygdøynes — with a crossing time of approximately 10 minutes. The ferry is included in standard Oslo public transport tickets and the Oslo Pass.

Best time: Weekday mornings in June–August combine long daylight hours with lower visitor numbers at the museums. Saturday afternoons in summer are the most congested, particularly at Huk beach and around the Fram and Kon-Tiki museums. The seasonal ferry fills quickly on warm weekend days — Bus 30 is a reliable alternative with more frequent service.
FAQ
Is Bygdøy Peninsula free to visit?
The peninsula itself is a free public space open to all. The five museums on the peninsula charge individual admission fees — visit each museum's official website for current prices.
How do I get to Bygdøy Peninsula from central Oslo?
Bus 30 runs from Jernbanetorget (Oslo S) and Nationaltheatret directly to the museums at Bygdøy. In summer, a seasonal ferry (Ruter line 91) operates from Rådhusbrygge 3 (City Hall pier). Journey time is around 15–20 minutes by bus. Both are covered by standard Oslo public transport tickets.
Which museums are on Bygdøy Peninsula?
Five museums are located on the peninsula: the Viking Ship Museum (currently undergoing major expansion as the Viking Age Museum), the Fram Museum, the Kon-Tiki Museum, the Norwegian Maritime Museum (Norsk Maritimt Museum), and the Norwegian Museum of Cultural History (Norsk Folkemuseum).
Are there beaches at Bygdøy?
Yes. Huk beach is a popular public beach at the tip of the peninsula with separate areas including a naturist section. Paradisbukta is a sheltered cove on the eastern side. Both are free to access and have no set opening or closing times.
Is the Viking Ship Museum open at Bygdøy?
The Viking Ship Museum (Vikingskipshuset) has been closed for major renovation and expansion as the new Viking Age Museum. Check khm.uio.no for current reopening status and ticket information.
