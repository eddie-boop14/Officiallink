About

The Avenue des Champs-Élysées stretches 1.9 kilometres through the 8th arrondissement, linking Place de la Concorde at the eastern end to Place Charles-de-Gaulle and the Arc de Triomphe at the west. The name translates as the Elysian Fields — the paradise of Greek mythology — and the avenue has served as the ceremonial heart of Paris since Louis XIV had it laid out in 1667 as a royal promenade extending the axis of the Tuileries garden.

The lower section near Concorde is flanked by the Grand Palais and Petit Palais, built for the 1900 Universal Exhibition. The upper section is lined with flagship luxury stores, cinemas, cafés, and the Lido cabaret. The pavement is wide enough that walking the full length is genuinely pleasant — the avenue has been traffic-calmed and partially pedestrianised in recent years, with plans to fully transform it by 2030.

The Arc de Triomphe at the top requires a separate ticket to access the roof terrace — book directly at paris-arc-de-triomphe.fr. The avenue itself costs nothing. Every 14 July, the national Bastille Day military parade runs the full length. On New Year's Eve, the avenue fills for midnight fireworks over the Arc.

FAQ
Is the Champs-Élysées free?
Yes. The avenue is a public street — free to walk at any time. The Arc de Triomphe at the top requires a ticket to access the roof terrace.
How long is the Champs-Élysées?
1.9km from Place de la Concorde at the eastern end to Place Charles-de-Gaulle and the Arc de Triomphe at the western end. A leisurely walk takes about 25–30 minutes.
What is on the Champs-Élysées?
Flagship stores (Louis Vuitton, Cartier, H&M), cinemas, cafés, restaurants, and the Lido cabaret. The Grand Palais and Petit Palais are just off the avenue near Concorde. The Christmas market runs November to January.
When is the best time to visit?
Weekday mornings are quietest. July 14 (Bastille Day) hosts the national military parade. New Year's Eve draws huge crowds for the midnight fireworks over the Arc de Triomphe.
How do I get there?
Metro Line 1 stops directly on the avenue: Franklin D. Roosevelt (midpoint), George V (upper section), and Charles-de-Gaulle–Étoile (Arc de Triomphe end).
