About

Plaza del Ayuntamiento is Valencia's main public square, located at the geographic and administrative centre of the city. The square takes its current name from the Ayuntamiento de Valencia (City Hall), designed in the early 20th century by architects Francisco Mora Berenguer and Carlos Carbonell in an eclectic style with baroque-influenced interiors. The square was pedestrianised in 2020. The buildings surrounding it — including the Central Post Office (Palacio de las Comunicaciones) — date largely from the first half of the 20th century. A circular fountain, inaugurated in 1963, occupies the centre of the square.

The square has served as Valencia's primary civic gathering point for over a century. Its name has changed several times: it was called Plaza de San Francisco until 1900, then Plaza de Emilio Castelar, then Plaza del Caudillo from 1939 to the end of the Franco era, and finally received its current neutral name in 1987. The plaza is the primary venue for the Las Fallas festival each March — a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage event. During the festival, the square hosts daily Mascletà firecracker displays at 14:00 from 1–19 March. The area around the plaza forms part of Valencia Historic Centre, a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The square is a transport hub as well as a civic landmark. Metro stations Xàtiva (200m, Lines 3, 5, 9) and Colón (500m) are within walking distance, and more than a dozen bus lines stop at or near the square. Valencia's main railway station, Estación del Norte, is located approximately 200 metres to the south. Flower stalls operate in the square on a regular basis. The City Hall building houses a small historical museum and a flag collection including the original Senyera (the 1928 Valencian flag). Interior visits are available Tuesday–Friday 10:00–14:00 and 15:00–19:00 for €3, with reservations required.

Best time: Weekday mornings (Mon–Thu, 09:00–11:00) see the lightest foot traffic. The square is busiest on Saturdays and during any public event. During Las Fallas (1–19 March), the daily 14:00 Mascletà draws very large crowds — arrive at least 30 minutes early to get a position in the square. The square is used for New Year's Eve celebrations, which draw peak crowds on 31 December.
FAQ
Is Plaza del Ayuntamiento free to visit?
Yes. The square is a public outdoor space, accessible 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, at no cost. Any website selling entry tickets to the plaza is fraudulent — no ticket exists or is required. For information on Valencia, visit visitvalencia.com.
What are the opening hours?
The square has no opening or closing hours. It is a public outdoor space accessible at all times. The City Hall interior can be visited on guided tours Tuesday to Friday, 10:00–14:00 and 15:00–19:00, for €3. Reservations for City Hall visits: +34 962-081-390.
How do I get to Plaza del Ayuntamiento?
Metro Lines 3, 5, and 9 stop at Xàtiva station, approximately 200 metres from the square. Bus lines C1, 6, 8, 11, 35, and 70 stop directly at the plaza. Valencia's main train station, Estación del Norte, is a 2–3 minute walk to the south. The square is also reachable by Valenbisi bike-share, with a station on the plaza itself.
Can I visit inside the City Hall (Ayuntamiento)?
Yes. Guided visits to the City Hall interior run Tuesday to Friday, 10:00–14:00 and 15:00–19:00. The visit costs €3 and includes the ceremonial halls, the Sala de Cristal, and the historical collection including the original 1928 Senyera flag and the sword of King Jaume I. Reservations are required: +34 962-081-390.
What is the Mascletà and when does it happen?
The Mascletà is a daily daytime firecracker display held in Plaza del Ayuntamiento at 14:00 every day from 1 to 19 March as part of the Las Fallas festival. It is free to attend — the square is a public space. Las Fallas is recognised by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.
