About

The Jewish Museum Berlin is Europe's largest Jewish museum, housed in two buildings in the Kreuzberg district. The newer building, designed by Polish-American architect Daniel Libeskind and clad in zinc, opened in 2001 and is one of Germany's most recognisable architectural landmarks. Libeskind named the design 'Between the Lines' — its zigzag form is cut through by a straight void, and the intersections create empty spaces called voids that represent the absence left by the Holocaust.

The permanent exhibition covers 2,000 years of German-Jewish history across five chapters — from medieval Ashkenaz through emancipation, persecution under National Socialism, and Jewish life in Germany today. The collection includes photographs, ritual objects, letters, and everyday items. Free for all visitors, it takes around two hours. Timed entry tickets are recommended and available free at jmberlin.de. All visitors must carry photo ID.

The museum also houses ANOHA: The Children's World, built around a Noah's Ark with 150 animal sculptures. The café, museum shop, and garden are accessible without a ticket. The garden behind offers views of Libeskind's architecture and a quiet break from the city. Audio tours are available free via the JMB app.

FAQ
Is the Jewish Museum Berlin free?
The permanent exhibition is free for everyone — no ticket needed. Temporary exhibitions in the Old Building cost €10 (adult) or €4 (reduced rate). Under 18 is always free for all exhibitions. A free time-slot reservation at jmberlin.de is recommended to avoid queues at the door.
Do I need to book tickets in advance?
Recommended for the permanent exhibition — free time-slot tickets can be reserved at the official ticket shop at jmberlin.de. Walk-in tickets are available at the desk but queues build quickly on weekends. ANOHA children's museum requires a pre-booked ticket — walk-ins are not accepted. A €3 booking fee applies per basket for ANOHA.
How do I get to the Jewish Museum Berlin?
U-Bahn: Kochstraße/Checkpoint Charlie (U6) or Hallesches Tor (U1, U3, U6) — both around 800 metres walk. Bus 248 stops directly at Jüdisches Museum. Bus M29 stops at Lindenstraße/Oranienstraße, M41 at Zossener Brücke. Address: Lindenstraße 9–14, 10969 Berlin.
