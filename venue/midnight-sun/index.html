About

The midnight sun is a phenomenon where the sun remains above the horizon at midnight, occurring only at latitudes above the Arctic Circle (66.5°N). Stavanger is located at 58.9°N — approximately 7.6 degrees south of the Arctic Circle — and does not experience true midnight sun. However, around the June solstice (June 21), Stavanger receives approximately 18–19 hours of daylight. The sky does not reach full darkness at night during June and early July; astronomical twilight persists through the short night, keeping the sky light enough to see outdoors without artificial lighting at midnight.

The nearest cities with true midnight sun are Bodø (67.3°N, approximately 850 km north of Stavanger), where the sun does not set from approximately June 3 to July 9, and Tromsø (69.7°N), with midnight sun from approximately May 20 to July 22. Further north, Svalbard experiences midnight sun from approximately April 20 to August 22. Official information on midnight sun locations, dates, and viewing spots in Norway is available at visitnorway.com.

For visitors to Stavanger, the extended summer daylight has practical implications for sightseeing. Hiking Preikestolen and Kjerag is possible until very late in the evening during June and July. Fjord cruises on Lysefjord operate in full daylight until nearly 23:00. Sunrise hikes to Preikestolen, departing the car park at 03:00–04:00, occur in near-daylight conditions at midsummer. Official weather and sunrise/sunset data for Stavanger is published at yr.no, the Norwegian Meteorological Institute's public forecast service.

FAQ
Can you see the midnight sun in Stavanger?
No. Stavanger is at latitude 58.9°N, below the Arctic Circle (66.5°N). True midnight sun — where the sun stays above the horizon at midnight — does not occur in Stavanger. At midsummer (around June 21), Stavanger receives approximately 18–19 hours of daylight and the sky never becomes fully dark, but the sun does set.
Where is the nearest place to see the midnight sun from Stavanger?
The nearest city with true midnight sun is Bodø, approximately 850 km north of Stavanger. Bodø experiences midnight sun from approximately June 3 to July 9. Further north, Tromsø has midnight sun from May 20 to July 22, and Svalbard from April 20 to August 22.
What is the daylight like in Stavanger in summer?
Around the June solstice (June 21), Stavanger receives approximately 18–19 hours of daylight. Sunrise is around 04:30 and sunset around 22:45. The sky does not reach full darkness at night — civil twilight persists through the short night hours, making it light enough to read outdoors at midnight.
What is the best time of year to visit Stavanger for long daylight hours?
June and July offer the longest days, with the June solstice (June 21) giving the maximum daylight of approximately 18–19 hours. May and August also offer very long evenings. By September, daylight has reduced significantly but conditions for hiking Preikestolen and Kjerag are still good with fewer crowds.
What is the official source for daylight and weather information for Stavanger?
The Norwegian Meteorological Institute at yr.no provides official weather and daylight forecasts for Stavanger. For planning trips to see the midnight sun further north in Norway, visitnorway.com lists destinations, dates, and viewing locations.
