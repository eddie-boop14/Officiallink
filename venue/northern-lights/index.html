About

The aurora borealis occurs when charged particles from the sun collide with gases in Earth's upper atmosphere at altitudes of 90–300 km. Tromsø sits at 69.6°N latitude, directly beneath the auroral oval — a ring around the magnetic poles where aurora activity is most frequent and intense. The city has been a base for aurora research since Norwegian physicist Kristian Birkeland conducted experiments here in the early 1900s.

The viewing season runs from late September to late March, when Tromsø's skies are sufficiently dark. Polar night — the period of continuous darkness — lasts from approximately 26 November to 15 January, providing the longest windows for observation. The phenomenon requires three conditions to be met simultaneously: solar activity producing a Kp-index of 3 or higher, clear skies, and darkness. The Norwegian Meteorological Institute (met.no) and NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center (swpc.noaa.gov) publish forecasts for both conditions.

Viewing is possible from within Tromsø on nights of high solar activity (Kp 5+), but darker locations significantly improve weaker displays. Telegrafbukta beach, at the southern tip of Tromsøya island, faces south across open water and is one of the most accessible dark sites on foot — approximately 300 m from the Arctic University Museum. The Tromsø Bridge walkway and the Tromsdalen valley on the mainland also reduce urban light interference. The aurora most commonly appears as green arcs; at higher activity levels, curtains and spirals of green, red, and purple become visible overhead.

The equinox periods (late September and mid-March) statistically show elevated geomagnetic activity.
FAQ
When is the Northern Lights season in Tromsø?
The aurora season runs from late September to late March. Dark skies are required, so the season aligns with Tromsø's night hours. Polar night (continuous darkness) lasts from approximately 26 November to 15 January, providing the longest daily viewing windows.
Do I need to book a tour to see the Northern Lights?
No tour is required. The aurora is visible from public outdoor locations across Tromsø at no cost. Organised tours take visitors to darker sites outside town and include weather monitoring. Licensed operators are listed at visittromso.no.
What aurora forecast tools should I use?
Use swpc.noaa.gov (NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center) for the 3-day Kp-index forecast — a Kp of 3+ is generally sufficient at Tromsø's latitude. Use met.no for Tromsø cloud cover forecasts. Both are free and official.
Where are the best free viewing spots in Tromsø?
Telegrafbukta beach on the southern tip of Tromsøya island has open southward sky and reduced light pollution. The Tromsø Bridge walkway provides views in both directions. Driving 10–15 km out of the city centre on the E8 road towards Tromsdalen greatly reduces light interference.
Can the Northern Lights be seen from inside the city?
Yes — on high-activity nights (Kp 5+) the aurora is visible overhead even in urban areas. For weaker displays (Kp 3–4), moving to Telegrafbukta or the city outskirts significantly improves visibility. Strong displays produce green curtains and spirals visible directly above the city centre.
