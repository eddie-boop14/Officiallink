Built in 1377 as a customs point and defensive fortress on the border between Transylvania and Wallachia, Bran Castle sits dramatically on a rocky outcrop above the village of Bran, 30 kilometres southwest of Brașov. While Bram Stoker never visited Romania, the castle's silhouette matched his description of Dracula's lair, forever linking the two. The historical connection to Vlad the Impaler is tenuous — he may have passed through but never resided here. The castle's genuine story involves Queen Marie of Romania, who adopted it as a summer residence and designed much of the interior. Today it operates as a museum displaying royal furnishings, armour, and artwork. A separate Time Tunnel and Torture Rooms exhibition requires additional tickets. Romania's most visited attraction. Located 30 minutes by bus from Brașov. Allow 1-3 hours.
Built between 1211 and 1225 by Teutonic Knights and later reinforced by local Saxon inhabitants, this hilltop fortress served as a refuge for the people of Râșnov during Ottoman and Tatar invasions. Unlike aristocratic castles, Râșnov was a communal fortress — each family maintained a room within its walls stocked with provisions for sieges. The interior contained 30 houses, a school, a chapel, and a 146-metre-deep well that took 17 years to dig (legend says two Turkish prisoners dug it in exchange for freedom). Located 15 kilometres from Brașov on the road to Bran Castle, making both easily combinable in a day trip. A funicular takes visitors to the fortress from the town below. Panoramic views across the Carpathian foothills.

Construction began in 1383 and took nearly a century to complete. The church received its distinctive name after a devastating fire in 1689 blackened its exterior walls. Standing 65 metres tall, it is the largest Gothic church in Romania and the largest between Vienna and Istanbul. The interior houses the largest mechanical organ in Romania with over 4,000 pipes, one of Europe's finest collections of Anatolian prayer rugs (over 110 pieces donated by Saxon merchants), and a 3.6-tonne bronze bell. The church served the German-speaking Lutheran community of Brașov (historically known as Kronstadt). Located in the heart of the old town just off Council Square, it remains an active Evangelical church with regular organ concerts.
Originally built in wood in 1392 and reconstructed in stone between 1495 and 1564, this Orthodox church anchors the historic Schei quarter where ethnic Romanians lived when Saxons controlled the walled city. The church blends Gothic, Byzantine, and Baroque architectural elements accumulated over centuries of additions and restorations. Beside it stands the First Romanian School Museum, housed in a 16th-century building where the first books were printed in the Romanian language. The churchyard contains the graves of notable Romanian cultural figures. The Schei quarter itself is reached through the narrow Schei Gate, through which Romanians were once required to pay a toll to enter the Saxon city.
The 58-metre tower dates from the 13th century and served as a watchtower and fire lookout for the medieval city. It stands in the centre of Council Square (Piața Sfatului), the historic heart of Brașov where the former Town Hall was located. The tower was damaged and rebuilt several times, most notably after the 1689 fire. Today it houses a small local history exhibition and visitors can climb to the top for panoramic views of the old town, the surrounding mountains, and the Hollywood-style "BRAȘOV" sign on Tampa Mountain. Council Square itself is lined with colourful merchant houses in Renaissance, Baroque, and neoclassical styles, now occupied by restaurants and cafés.

🇷🇴 Rope Street (Strada Sforii)

Free
→
✅
Located next to St. Nicholas Church in the Schei quarter, this 16th-century building housed the first school where teaching was conducted in the Romanian language. The deacon Coresi printed the first Romanian-language books here in the 1550s and 1560s, making it a foundational site in the development of Romanian literary culture. The museum displays original printing presses, early Romanian-language texts including the first Romanian translation of the Gospels, handwritten manuscripts, and historical educational materials. The building preserves the original classroom where generations of Romanian students were educated when the Romanian language had no official status in Saxon-controlled Transylvania.
Measuring just 111 to 135 centimetres wide and 83 metres long, Strada Sforii is one of the narrowest streets in Europe. Built in the 17th century between two buildings on Strada Cerbului and Strada Poarta Schei, it originally served as a firefighters' access passage through the densely built medieval city. The name literally translates to "String Street" or "Rope Street" in Romanian. Two people can barely pass each other along its length. The alley has become one of Brașov's most photographed curiosities. Located in the heart of the old town, it connects major pedestrian routes and is easily found between the fortification walls. The street is uncovered and accessible at all times.

Rising 960 metres above sea level directly behind the old town, Tampa Mountain is Brașov's most prominent natural landmark, identifiable from across the city by the large Hollywood-style "BRAȘOV" letters on its slopes. A cable car operates from the base near the old town to near the summit, providing panoramic views of Brașov, the surrounding Carpathian peaks, and the Bârsa Plain. Multiple hiking trails wind through the forested slopes, with the most popular route from the old town taking approximately 45 minutes to climb on foot. The mountain is a protected nature reserve. The summit viewpoint offers spectacular sunset views over the medieval rooftops and church spires below.
Built in 1559 by the Tailors' Guild, Catherine's Gate is the only original medieval city gate still standing in Brașov. The distinctive structure features a central tower flanked by four corner turrets, a design found in few other Central European cities. The four small towers symbolised the medieval city's right to impose capital punishment. Named after nearby St. Catherine's monastery, the gate provided a ceremonial entrance to the fortified city from the Schei quarter. Nearby stands the Schei Gate (Poarta Schei), built in 1828 as a neoclassical replacement entrance. Together the two gates frame one of Brașov's most atmospheric streets. Both gates are viewable from the exterior at all times.
These two semi-circular bastions were built in the 15th century to strengthen Brașov's fortification system. The White Tower (Turnul Alb), built in 1494, stands on a hill above the southeastern corner of the old town and is reached via a short uphill walk. The Black Tower (Turnul Negru), dating from the same period, received its name after being blackened by the 1689 fire. Both towers offer excellent elevated views over the old town rooftops, the Black Church, Council Square, and the surrounding mountains. The walk between the two towers follows a section of the preserved medieval city wall. The White Tower occasionally hosts small exhibitions. The towers and connecting wall walk provide some of the best photography angles in Brașov.
Romania's premier ski resort sits at 1,030 metres altitude in a sunny clearing surrounded by spruce forests, just 12 kilometres from Brașov city centre. The resort has hosted World Cup alpine skiing events and offers 24 kilometres of ski runs served by modern cable cars and gondolas. During summer it transforms into a hiking and mountain biking destination with trails leading into the Postăvaru Mountains. The meadow setting was originally a bear-hunting ground for Transylvanian nobility. Today the area remains part of one of Europe's densest brown bear habitats. Hotels, restaurants, and sports facilities operate year-round. Regular bus service connects the resort to Brașov. The gondola operates in summer for scenic rides and mountain-top walking access.
