Founded in the 12th century by Transylvanian Saxon colonists, the citadel of Sighișoara was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999 as an outstanding example of a small fortified medieval town. Unlike many European citadels that serve purely as museums, Sighișoara's remains continuously inhabited, with residents living inside the same buildings that have stood for centuries. The defensive walls, originally reinforced with 14 towers (9 survive), encircle steep cobblestone streets lined with colourful 16th and 17th-century merchant houses with terracotta rooftops. Each tower was built and maintained by a different guild — tanners, blacksmiths, tailors, butchers, and others — whose names the towers still bear. Walking the citadel takes visitors through layers of medieval, Renaissance, and Baroque architecture within a remarkably compact area. Allow 2-3 hours.
Rising 64 metres above the main square, the Clock Tower has been Sighișoara's most prominent landmark since the 14th century. The current structure dates primarily from a reconstruction after a 1676 fire. The elaborate mechanical clock mechanism, installed in the 17th century, features carved wooden figurines representing the days of the week on the side facing the citadel interior, and Justice, Peace, Law, and Day and Night figures on the exterior face. Inside, the History Museum occupies multiple floors displaying medieval weapons, pharmacy equipment, furniture, and photographs documenting Sighișoara's evolution. The top floor observation deck provides panoramic views over the citadel rooftops, the surrounding countryside, and the Târnava Mare river valley. The tower serves as the main entrance gate into the citadel.
This yellow three-storey house on the citadel's main square is where Vlad II Dracul (father of Vlad the Impaler) lived between 1431 and 1435 while serving as military commander of the Transylvanian border. His son, Vlad III — later known as Vlad Țepeș (the Impaler) and the historical inspiration for Bram Stoker's Dracula — was born here in 1431. A small wrought-iron dragon plaque marks the facade, referencing Vlad II's membership in the Order of the Dragon (Dracul meaning dragon). The ground floor operates as a restaurant, while the upper floors function as a small museum displaying period items. The house stands directly on the citadel square near the Clock Tower, making it unmissable during any citadel visit.

Perched on the hilltop above the citadel, this 14th-century Gothic church is reached via the Scholars' Stairway (Scara Școlarilor), a remarkable covered wooden staircase of 175 steps built in 1642 to protect schoolchildren climbing to the hilltop school during winter. The church interior preserves remarkable 15th and 16th-century frescoes depicting biblical scenes, though many were whitewashed during the Reformation and only partially restored. The surrounding Saxon cemetery on the hillside contains ornate tombstones spanning several centuries. The hilltop location provides elevated views over the citadel below and the Transylvanian countryside. The covered stairway itself, with its wooden tunnel enclosing the original stone steps, is one of Sighișoara's most atmospheric features.
Built in the 13th century by Dominican friars, this church features transitional Romanesque-Gothic architecture with thick stone walls and simple pointed arches. After the Reformation, the monastery was dissolved and the church became a Protestant place of worship. The interior has been adapted over centuries but retains its medieval spatial qualities, with a Gothic choir and barrel-vaulted nave. Today the church regularly hosts classical music concerts and cultural events, its medieval acoustics providing an atmospheric setting. Located within the citadel walls near the Clock Tower, the church forms part of the compact cluster of historic buildings around the main square. The former monastery buildings adjacent have been repurposed for community and cultural use.

Of the original 14 defence towers built around the citadel between the 14th and 16th centuries, nine survive and can be viewed on a walking circuit of the walls. Each tower was assigned to a specific artisan guild responsible for its construction, maintenance, and defence during sieges. Surviving towers include the Tinsmiths' Tower, Bootmakers' Tower, Tailors' Tower, Furriers' Tower, Butchers' Tower, Ropemakers' Tower, Tanners' Tower, and Blacksmiths' Tower. Some towers have been converted into residences, guesthouses, or small museums. The Tinsmiths' Tower near the main gate retains its original form most clearly. Walking the perimeter reveals how the towers were positioned to create overlapping fields of fire and to command views of approach routes. The walls between towers are partially intact, creating a semi-continuous defensive circuit.

The compact main square of the citadel is surrounded by pastel-coloured merchant houses from the 16th and 17th centuries, their distinctive tall facades leaning at slight angles after centuries of settlement. The Clock Tower dominates one end, while Vlad Dracul House and several restaurants and cafés line the perimeter. In medieval times this was the commercial heart of the town where markets were held and guild celebrations took place. Today the square hosts the annual Medieval Festival in July, when the citadel fills with costumed performers, artisan demonstrations, jousting, and traditional food. The intimate scale of the square — small enough to cross in a minute — creates an unusually atmospheric enclosed space. Street musicians and artisan stalls appear in warmer months.
Built in 1642, this covered wooden stairway of 175 steps (originally 300) climbs steeply from the citadel to the hilltop Church on the Hill and the former German school. The tunnel-like wooden roof was constructed to protect students from rain and snow during their daily climb to school. The atmospheric passage, with light filtering through gaps in the wooden planks and worn stone steps polished by centuries of footsteps, is one of Transylvania's most evocative structures. The stairway climbs approximately 70 metres in elevation. At the top, the hilltop plateau contains the church, the old school building (now housing a cultural centre), and the historic Saxon cemetery with its ornate headstones and views across the valley.
