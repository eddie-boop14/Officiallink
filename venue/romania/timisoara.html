Dating from the 18th century under Habsburg rule, Union Square is Timișoara's oldest and arguably most beautiful public space. The square is considered the most important concentration of Baroque architecture in Romania, with uniformly elegant facades in pastel colours creating a theatrical enclosed setting. Two cathedrals face each other across the space — the Roman Catholic St. George's Cathedral on the south side and the Serbian Orthodox Cathedral on the north — reflecting the city's multicultural character. The Baroque Palace (now the National Museum of Art) anchors the eastern end. At the centre stands the Holy Trinity Plague Column, erected in 1740 after a devastating epidemic. The ground floors are occupied by cafés and restaurants with outdoor terraces that fill the square in warmer months. Named Union Square in 1919 to mark Banat's union with Romania after World War I.
This sweeping pedestrianised boulevard stretches approximately 500 metres from the Orthodox Metropolitan Cathedral at one end to the Romanian National Theatre and Opera House at the other. The square played a central role in the December 1989 Romanian Revolution — it was here that Timișoara became the first city in Romania to rise against the Ceaușescu regime. Demonstrators gathered in enormous numbers, and the square was the scene of both shootings and the eventual triumph of the revolution. Memorial plaques and crosses mark spots where people fell. The flanking buildings display an eclectic mix of Secessionist, Baroque, and neoclassical architecture. A central garden strip with lawns and flower beds creates a promenade atmosphere. The National Theatre building (1875) features an ornate neo-Renaissance facade.
The smallest of Timișoara's three interconnected central squares, Liberty Square served as the city's administrative centre during the Habsburg period. The Old Town Hall (Primăria Veche), a yellow Baroque building dating from 1734, dominates the square and still functions as a municipal building. The square also contains the Military Casino, a neoclassical structure built in 1852, and a statue of the Virgin Mary atop a Baroque column. The Nepomuk statue, a common feature in Habsburg-era cities, stands nearby. Liberty Square connects Union Square to Victory Square via pedestrianised streets, creating a continuous architectural promenade through the historic centre. The intimate scale of the square contrasts with the grandeur of Victory Square, offering a quieter corner in the old town.

Completed in 1946 after two decades of construction, the Metropolitan Cathedral is Timișoara's most visually striking building. Its design draws on the 16th-century painted monasteries of Moldavia, with eleven towers topped by colourful glazed tile roofs in geometric patterns visible from across the city. The central dome rises 90 metres, making it one of the tallest Orthodox churches in Romania. The interior is entirely covered in frescoes and mosaics blending Byzantine tradition with Romanian artistic themes, and the iconostasis is richly carved and gilded. The cathedral sits at the southern end of Victory Square and houses a museum of religious art in its basement containing icons, vestments, and liturgical objects from across the Banat region. The building serves as the seat of the Metropolitan Archbishop of Banat. The striped exterior creates a distinctive silhouette unlike any other Romanian cathedral.
Built between 1736 and 1773 by an Austrian architect, this Roman Catholic cathedral exemplifies the Baroque architectural style that characterises Timișoara's Habsburg heritage. The twin-towered facade faces Union Square, creating a formal axis with the Serbian Orthodox Cathedral opposite. The interior features an elaborate Baroque altar, ceiling frescoes, and an 18th-century organ. The cathedral served the substantial Catholic population that settled in Timișoara after the Ottomans were expelled in 1716, when the Habsburgs encouraged German (Swabian), Hungarian, and other Catholic settlers to repopulate the region. The crypt contains tombs of former bishops. The building's proportions and decorative programme reflect the confidence and resources of the Habsburg administration in transforming Timișoara from an Ottoman garrison town into a Central European city.
Built between 1863 and 1865 in Moorish Revival style, the Great Synagogue (Sinagoga Mare) is one of the largest synagogues in Romania and among the most architecturally significant in southeastern Europe. The ornate facade features horseshoe arches, decorative brickwork, and twin towers characteristic of Moorish-inspired synagogue design common in the Habsburg Empire. The interior contains a magnificent painted ceiling, wrought-iron galleries, and an elaborate ark. Timișoara was home to a thriving Jewish community that numbered over 10,000 before World War II and contributed significantly to the city's commercial and cultural life. The synagogue has been restored and hosts cultural events and concerts alongside religious services. Located in the Cetate (Fortress) neighbourhood, a short walk from Union Square. The building stands as a testament to the multicultural fabric that defined Timișoara for centuries.

Housed in the eastern wing of the Baroque Palace on Union Square, the National Museum of Art displays Romanian and European art collections spanning from the 16th century to the contemporary period. The palace itself, built between 1752 and 1754 as the residence of the provincial governor, is one of the finest Baroque buildings in Romania with restored ceremonial halls. Permanent collections include Romanian modern art, decorative arts, and European paintings originating from the collection amassed by the 19th-century art collector Zsigmond Ormós. Temporary exhibitions rotate throughout the year. The museum became an independent institution in 2006, separating from the National Museum of Banat. The building's location on Union Square means visitors can combine the museum visit with exploration of the surrounding Baroque architecture.
Huniade Castle (Castelul Huniazilor) was originally built between 1308 and 1315 by Charles Robert of Anjou, King of Hungary, who established his royal residence in Timișoara. John Hunyadi (Iancu de Hunedoara) restored the castle in 1443 after earthquake damage. During the Ottoman occupation from 1552 to 1716, it served as the residence of the regional pasha. The building later became an Austrian military barracks before housing the Museum of Banat from 1947. The museum contains archaeological, historical, and natural science collections documenting the Banat region from prehistory through the Roman period, medieval era, and modern times. The castle has undergone extensive restoration in recent years. The Maria Theresia Bastion nearby serves as the museum's temporary exhibition space during ongoing renovation work. The castle grounds include a park popular with local residents.
Spread across 17 hectares at the edge of the Green Forest (Pădurea Verde) in northeastern Timișoara, this open-air museum recreates a traditional Banat village complete with a civic centre, wooden church, farmhouses, workshops, and an Alley of Ethnicities. The museum is unique among Romania's open-air museums for explicitly representing the multiple ethnic communities that have lived in the Banat region — Romanian, Hungarian, German (Swabian), Serbian, Slovak, Ukrainian, and Bulgarian houses each showcase distinct architectural traditions and domestic interiors. The wooden church, built in 1746 in Remetea-Luncă and relocated by 24 pairs of oxen, is the centrepiece. The Festival of Ethnicities takes place every first Sunday of May. The museum reopened in Marché 2026 after a period of reorganisation, with newly accessible buildings and improved visitor information.
This small but fascinating museum recreates a complete Romanian apartment from the communist era, furnished with authentic furniture, technology, household goods, and food packaging from the Ceaușescu period. Every object is original — from the television set and typewriter to the crockery and propaganda materials. The museum provides an immersive experience of daily life under communism, revealing both the austerity and the creative survival strategies of ordinary Romanians. Located in a residential building in the Fabric neighbourhood, the museum feels like stepping into someone's home frozen in time. Particularly relevant in Timișoara given the city's role as the birthplace of the 1989 revolution that ended communist rule. The collection has been assembled by local enthusiasts passionate about preserving this recent but rapidly disappearing material culture.

Timișoara earned its nickname "City of Roses" partly through this park, which features extensive rose gardens alongside mature trees, walking paths, and a small lake. Central Park (Parcul Central) connects directly to Roses Park, creating a continuous green belt around the southern edge of the old town. Together the parks provide over 20 hectares of green space within walking distance of all three main squares. Central Park contains a children's playground, sports facilities, and the Hydro Castle (Castelul Apei), a historic 19th-century water tower that once served the city's water supply system. The parks are bordered by residential neighbourhoods featuring Secessionist villas and apartment buildings. Dog-walkers, joggers, and families use the parks year-round, making them a window into local daily life beyond the tourist centre.
The Bega River was canalised in the 18th century, making Timișoara the first city in the Habsburg Empire connected to the sea via an inland waterway (the Bega canal links to the Tisza, then the Danube, and finally the Black Sea). The riverbanks have been developed into a popular promenade with cycling and walking paths stretching several kilometres through the city. Points of interest along the route include the Podul Metalic, an iron bridge engineered by the workshop of Gustave Eiffel, a small Japanese garden with a traditional red bridge (Alpinet Park), and the Water Palace (Palatul Apelor), a restored Baroque building. In summer, cafés and bars spill onto the riverbanks and stationary boat-bars operate on the water. Boat cruises on the Bega offer a different perspective of the city's architecture. The river promenade provides a relaxed counterpoint to the architectural grandeur of the old town squares.
