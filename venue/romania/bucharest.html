Built between 1984 and 1997 under dictator Nicolae Ceaușescu, the Palace of the Parliament weighs over four million tonnes and contains more than 1,100 rooms spread across 365,000 square metres. Construction required over 20,000 workers and 700 architects, led by 28-year-old Anca Petrescu. An entire historic neighbourhood was demolished to make way for the building, displacing over 40,000 residents. Today it houses Romania's parliament, the National Museum of Contemporary Art, and conference facilities. Guided tours visit a fraction of the opulent marble halls, crystal chandeliers, and gilded rooms. Reservations must be made by phone at least 24 hours in advance. Passport or ID required for entry. Allow 1-2 hours for the standard tour.
Built between 1886 and 1888 through public subscription — Romanians donated money following the appeal "Give one leu for the Athenaeum." The circular concert hall seats 800 and is renowned for exceptional acoustics. The interior features a 75-metre-long fresco depicting scenes from Romanian history that wraps around the auditorium dome. Classified as a historical monument and recognised as part of European Heritage since 2007. Home venue for the George Enescu Philharmonic Orchestra and the prestigious George Enescu International Festival held every two years. The building's Ionic-columned portico and copper dome are among Bucharest's most recognisable architectural landmarks.
This central square witnessed the pivotal moments of Romania's 1989 revolution, when Ceaușescu made his final disastrous public appearance from the balcony of the former Central Committee building to a crowd that turned hostile. The Memorial of Rebirth, a 25-metre marble pillar piercing a metal form, commemorates the revolution's victims. Surrounding the square are several important buildings including the former Royal Palace (now the National Museum of Art), the Athenaeum, the University Library (rebuilt after being burned during the revolution), and the Athénée Palace hotel. The square functions as an open-air memorial linking Romania's royal past, communist era, and democratic present.
The current granite structure was completed in 1936, replacing earlier temporary versions built from wood. Standing 27 metres tall, the arch commemorates Romania's victory in World War I and the country's reunification. Base-reliefs carved in marble from Transylvania's Rușchița quarries were created by leading Romanian sculptors including Ion Jalea and Constantin Baraschi, alongside ten Italian sculptors. The arch sits at one of Bucharest's busiest intersections on Kiseleff Boulevard, echoing the Champs-Élysées layout that inspired Bucharest's "Little Paris" reputation. Interior staircase access to the top is available on weekends from June through October, offering panoramic city views.
Built in 1724 in Brâncovenesc architectural style, this tiny church is considered one of the most beautiful in Bucharest despite its modest size. The ornate stone carvings on the exterior combine Romanian, Ottoman, and Byzantine decorative elements. The interior preserves original 18th-century frescoes and a carved wooden iconostasis. A peaceful inner courtyard contains medieval tombstones and architectural fragments collected from demolished Bucharest churches. The church operates as an active monastery with a renowned Byzantine music choir. Located just one block from the busiest nightlife streets in the Old Town, it offers a striking contrast of sacred tranquillity.

Housed in the former Royal Palace on Revolution Square, the museum contains three major collections: European Art (works by El Greco, Rembrandt, Monet, Renoir), Romanian Medieval Art, and Romanian Modern Art. The European collection began with royal acquisitions and donations and now comprises over 3,000 works. The palace itself served as the official royal residence until 1947 and retains grand ceremonial halls. The building was damaged during the 1989 revolution and underwent extensive restoration. The Throne Hall and other palatial rooms provide a magnificent setting for the permanent exhibitions. Allow 2-3 hours for a comprehensive visit across all galleries.
Located in the former Post Office Palace on Calea Victoriei, this museum holds Romania's national historical collections spanning from prehistory through the medieval period to modern times. Highlights include the Romanian Crown Jewels, Trajan's Column plaster cast (a full-scale copy of the Rome original depicting the Roman conquest of Dacia), and extensive Dacian gold and silver treasures. The building itself features an impressive neoclassical facade and grand interior halls. The numismatic collection is one of the largest in southeastern Europe. Ongoing renovation work means some galleries may be temporarily closed. Allow 2 hours for available exhibitions.
Founded in 1936 on the shores of Herăstrău Lake, this open-air museum contains over 270 authentic buildings relocated from villages across Romania, including farmhouses, churches, windmills, and workshops. The collection represents architectural traditions from every Romanian region, spanning the 17th to 20th centuries. Wooden churches with carved gates from Maramureș and half-timbered Saxon houses from Transylvania stand alongside clay dwellings from Wallachia and Moldavia. Buildings are arranged to recreate village settings complete with gardens and agricultural equipment. The lakeside setting within Bucharest's largest park makes it a peaceful escape. Allow 2-3 hours to walk the extensive grounds.
Opened in 2004 within the west wing of the Palace of the Parliament, MNAC provides a striking contrast — a modern glass-fronted gallery inside Ceaușescu's colossal monument. The collection focuses on Romanian and international contemporary art including painting, sculpture, video, and multimedia installations. The rooftop terrace offers one of Bucharest's finest panoramic views across Constitution Square, Unirii Boulevard, and the city skyline. Rotating exhibitions complement the permanent collection. The juxtaposition of contemporary artistic expression within a symbol of totalitarian excess is itself a powerful statement about Romania's cultural evolution.
Founded in 1834 and named after zoologist Grigore Antipa who directed it for 51 years, this is one of the largest natural history museums in southeastern Europe. The collection encompasses over four million specimens including extensive dioramas of Romanian ecosystems, a whale skeleton, a planetarium, and interactive exhibits. The museum underwent a major €8 million modernisation completed in 2011, adding multimedia displays, a 3D cinema, and hands-on exhibits. Located on Victory Square near the Romanian Athenaeum. Particularly engaging for families and children. Exhibits cover everything from Carpathian wildlife and Danube Delta ecosystems to deep-sea creatures and dinosaur fossils.

Built between 1656 and 1658, this cathedral sits atop Dealul Mitropoliei (Metropolitan Hill) and serves as the centre of Romanian Orthodox faith. The interior contains remarkable 17th-century frescoes restored multiple times and a richly carved iconostasis. The cathedral bell tower was added in 1698. Adjacent stands the Patriarchal Palace, administrative headquarters of the Romanian Orthodox Church. The hilltop location provides views over central Bucharest. The cathedral houses relics of Saints Dimitrie Basarabov and Constantine Brâncoveanu, important figures in Romanian religious history. It remains an active place of worship with regular services.
Consecrated in 2018 after decades of planning and construction, this is the tallest Orthodox church in the world at 120 metres and the largest in Romania. The cathedral stands near the Palace of the Parliament, creating a dramatic juxtaposition of Romania's secular communist past and its Orthodox spiritual identity. The interior is designed to accommodate 5,000 worshippers, with the iconostasis and frescoes still being completed in phases. Construction was funded by the Romanian Orthodox Church and private donations. The building uses a cross-shaped plan combining traditional Romanian Orthodox architecture with modern engineering. The massive gilded domes are visible across the Bucharest skyline.

✅
Spanning over 180 hectares around Herăstrău Lake, this is Bucharest's largest and most popular park, officially renamed King Michael I Park in 2017. The park stretches from the Arch of Triumph north to Băneasa Bridge. A 6-kilometre path circles the lake, popular with joggers, cyclists, and walkers. Boat rentals are available on the lake. The park contains the Village Museum, several restaurants and cafés, a summer theatre, sports clubs, and exhibition pavilions. The surrounding neighbourhood features some of Bucharest's finest villas in various architectural styles from 19th-century neoclassical to Art Nouveau. Allow 2-3 hours for a leisurely visit combining the park with the Village Museum.
Bucharest's oldest and most storied boulevard stretches 2.8 kilometres from the Old Princely Court to Kiseleff Boulevard. Originally designed in 1692 to connect the court to Mogoșoaia Palace and first paved with oak beams, the avenue became the centre of Bucharest's social and cultural life. Walking its length passes many of the city's most important buildings: the National Museum of Art (former Royal Palace), the Romanian Athenaeum, the CEC Palace, Revolution Square, and the National Military Circle. The belle époque architecture along this stretch earned Bucharest its "Little Paris" nickname. Weekend pedestrianisation of sections has made it increasingly popular for strolling.
The compact pedestrianised area centred on Strada Lipscani and surrounding streets forms Bucharest's historic commercial district, named after the Leipzig merchants who once traded here. The neighbourhood contains the ruins of the Old Princely Court (Curtea Veche) where Vlad the Impaler once held court, the 16th-century Curtea Veche Church (Bucharest's oldest), and the beautiful Macca-Villacrosse Passage with its fork-shaped glass-roofed arcade. After decades of neglect, the area was extensively restored in the 2000s and is now packed with restaurants, bars, and cafés. Street-level architecture reveals layers from the medieval, Ottoman, and belle époque periods. The Choral Temple, a striking 1857 synagogue, stands nearby.
