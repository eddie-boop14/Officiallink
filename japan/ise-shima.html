Japan's most sacred Shinto shrine, dedicated to sun goddess Amaterasu Omikami — mythological ancestor of the Imperial family. Founded approximately 2,000 years ago. Buildings constructed in ancient Japanese style (Yuitsu-shinmei-zukuri) using unpainted hinoki cypress, completely rebuilt every 20 years in ritual renewal ceremony (Shikinen Sengu) — last rebuilt 2013, next 2033. Visitors cannot approach main shrine building directly — worship from behind multiple fences. Approach through ancient cedar forest along Isuzu River creates deeply atmospheric experience. Okage Yokocho street at entrance recreates Edo-period townscape with traditional food shops and restaurants. Akafuku mochi (red bean rice cakes) sold here since 1707. Ise udon (soft, thick noodles) is regional speciality. Free to visit. Allow 2 hours including approach and Okage Yokocho.
Outer Shrine dedicated to Toyouke Omikami, deity of food, clothing, and housing. Traditional pilgrimage order visits Geku before Naiku. Rebuilt every 20 years alongside Naiku using identical ritual process. Old and new shrine sites sit side by side — empty plot awaiting next construction visible beside current buildings. More compact and less crowded than Naiku. Surrounding Sengukan Museum explains the shrine's unique architecture and rebuilding process through detailed models and exhibits. Forest approach shorter than Naiku but equally atmospheric through ancient trees. Multiple auxiliary shrines (betsugu) scattered through surrounding forest. Located near Ise-shi Station, 15 minutes by bus from Naiku. Daily food offerings ceremony has been performed twice daily for Amaterasu for over 1,500 years uninterrupted. Free to visit. Allow 1 hour.
Two rocks in the sea connected by sacred shimenawa rope, representing the married creator deities Izanagi and Izanami in Shinto mythology. Larger rock (husband, 9 metres) topped with small torii gate, smaller rock (wife, 4 metres) connected by heavy rice-straw rope replaced three times yearly in ceremony. On clear mornings during summer solstice period, sun rises between the two rocks — highly significant in Shinto sun worship tradition. Mount Fuji occasionally visible between rocks in winter clear conditions. Adjacent Futami Okitama Shrine features frog statues (kaeru means both "frog" and "return" in Japanese — visitors pray for safe return of loved ones). Located in Futami, 15 minutes from Ise by train. Coastal walk along bay passes the rocks. Free to view. Best photographed at sunrise.

Small island in Toba Bay where Mikimoto Kokichi successfully produced the world's first cultured pearl in 1893, transforming pearls from aristocratic rarity to accessible luxury. Pearl Museum traces the science and history of pearl cultivation from Mikimoto's early experiments through modern techniques. Ama diver demonstrations — traditional female free-divers who historically harvested natural oysters perform diving exhibitions in white costume (demonstrations scheduled multiple times daily). Pearl workshop explains grading systems and quality differences. Mikimoto pearl shop sells jewellery at various price points. Connected to Toba mainland by bridge. Located in Toba, 20 minutes from Ise by train. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
One of Japan's largest aquariums with over 1,200 species, notable for housing the only dugong in Japan (one of very few in captivity worldwide). Twelve exhibition zones cover habitats from local Ise Bay to coral reefs, rivers, polar regions, and deep sea. Sea otter feeding demonstrations and penguin walks among popular shows. Living fossils section displays horseshoe crabs, nautilus, and other ancient species. Aquarium built into coastal hillside with multiple levels. Japanese spiny lobster (Ise-ebi) native to these waters displayed in local marine section. Located in Toba, walking distance from Mikimoto Pearl Island and Toba Station. Combined visit with Pearl Island natural. Allow 2-3 hours.
Deeply indented bay dotted with small islands and pearl cultivation rafts — floating platforms where oysters grow cultured pearls underwater. Sightseeing cruise boats navigate between islands and pearl rafts, some including pearl extraction demonstration on board. Bay's calm, nutrient-rich waters have supported pearl farming since Mikimoto's pioneering work. Rias coastline (sunken river valley) creates dramatic geography with numerous inlets. Yokoyama Observatory above bay provides panoramic viewpoint across entire inlet system. Ama diver huts along coastline where traditional female divers rest between dives — some converted to visitor-accessible seafood barbecue experiences. Located in Shima city at southern tip of Ise-Shima region. G7 Summit held at Shima Kanko Hotel overlooking this bay in 2016. Allow 1-2 hours for cruise.

Archaeological site and museum preserving remains of the Saiku — palace complex where unmarried imperial princesses (Saio) served as chief priestesses of Ise Grand Shrine from 7th to 14th century. Each new emperor appointed a princess to serve at Ise, where she lived in this palace conducting purification rituals. Museum displays excavated artifacts including ceramics, ritual objects, and architectural remains revealing court life transplanted to this remote location. Reconstructed buildings show palace architecture. Saio Matsuri festival (June) recreates the princess's ceremonial procession in full Heian-period costume. Located in Meiwa town between Ise's outer and inner shrines. Little-known outside Japan despite significant historical importance. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
16.3-kilometre toll road connecting Ise and Toba across Mount Asama (555m) with panoramic viewpoints. Summit observation area provides 360-degree views — Ise Bay, Pacific Ocean, Shima Peninsula, and on clear days Fuji-san visible across the water. Kongoshoji Temple at summit founded 6th century, historically important Shingon Buddhist temple with connections to Kobo Daishi. Footbath (ashiyu) at summit rest area. Mountain terrain covered in forest with seasonal colour. Driving route takes approximately 30 minutes without stops. Combined naturally with visits between Ise Grand Shrine and Toba. Car or taxi required — not accessible by public transport. Several viewing platforms along route. Allow 1 hour including stops and summit visit.
