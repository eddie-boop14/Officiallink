🇯🇵 Kushida Shrine
Founded 757 AD, guardian shrine of Hakata (Fukuoka's historic merchant district).
Free
→
📍
Built over the grave of Sugawara no Michizane (845-903 AD), deified as Tenjin, god of learning and scholarship. Students across Japan visit to pray for exam success — wooden prayer tablets (ema) covered in handwritten wishes hang throughout grounds. Over 6,000 plum trees bloom January-Marché — legend states plum tree flew from Kyoto to follow Michizane into exile. Arched bridge approach crosses three ponds representing past, present, and future. Treasure house displays shrine's collection of swords, scrolls, and art. Starbucks at entrance designed by Kengo Kuma features interlocking wooden beam ceiling. Located in Dazaifu city, 30 minutes from central Fukuoka by train. Approach street lined with mochi (rice cake) shops selling umegae-mochi grilled plum cakes.
Founded 806 AD by Kobo Daishi (founder of Shingon Buddhism) upon his return from studying in China — first Shingon temple established in Japan. Houses Fukuoka Daibutsu, a 10.8-metre seated wooden Buddha completed in 1992 — largest wooden seated Buddha in Japan. Five-storey pagoda (2011) is first built in Fukuoka in over 400 years. Basement of main hall features "Hell and Paradise" passage — visitors walk through complete darkness before emerging into illuminated Buddha hall, symbolising journey from darkness to enlightenment. Hexagonal Rokkaku-do hall dates from Muromachi period. Located in Hakata's Gofuku-machi temple district within walking distance of Hakata Station. Compact grounds can be visited in 30-45 minutes.
Founded 757 AD, guardian shrine of Hakata (Fukuoka's historic merchant district). Home shrine of Hakata Gion Yamakasa festival — one of Japan's most dramatic festivals where teams race through streets carrying one-tonne decorated floats each July. Permanent display float (kazariyama) stands inside shrine grounds year-round at approximately 13 metres tall, elaborately decorated with samurai and mythological figures. Camphor tree in grounds estimated over 1,000 years old. Hakata Machiya Folk Museum adjacent displays traditional Hakata crafts including textile weaving demonstrations. Located in central Hakata within walking distance of Canal City. Compact grounds can be visited in 20-30 minutes. Morning visits see locals paying daily respects.

🇯🇵 Kyushu National Museum

📍
Remains of Fukuoka's largest castle, built 1601 by Kuroda Nagamasa after the Battle of Sekigahara. Stone walls and several turrets survive across hilltop site within Maizuru Park. No main tower remains, but Tamon Yagura and Shiomi Tower provide elevated viewing points across city, Hakata Bay, and surrounding mountains. Castle grounds planted with over 1,000 cherry trees — one of Fukuoka's premier hanami locations in spring. Korokan archaeological site within grounds preserves remains of Japan's 7th-9th century diplomatic guest house for Korean and Chinese envoys. Ohori Park with central pond adjoins castle grounds. Free to explore grounds and walk atop stone walls. Allow 1-1.5 hours for castle and park.
Museum covering Fukuoka's 2,000-year role as Japan's gateway to Asia. Centrepiece is the Gold Seal (Kin-in), a solid gold stamp presented by Chinese Emperor Guangwu to a Japanese envoy in 57 AD — designated National Treasure and one of Japan's most significant archaeological discoveries, found by a farmer on Shikanoshima island in 1784. Exhibits trace trade routes connecting Fukuoka to Korea, China, and Southeast Asia. Mongol invasion displays include artefacts from Kublai Khan's 13th-century fleet discovered in Hakata Bay. Located in Momochi seaside area near Fukuoka Tower. Modern building houses permanent collection and rotating special exhibitions. Allow 1.5 hours.
Opened 2005 as Japan's fourth national museum, first built in over 100 years. Distinctive curved glass building by Kikuchi Takeo set into hillside behind Dazaifu Tenmangu shrine, connected by moving walkway through forest. Focus on Japan's cultural exchanges with Asia — displays trace how Japanese art, religion, and trade were shaped by contact with Korea, China, and Southeast Asia. Permanent exhibition "Cultural Exchange" spans Jomon period through Edo period across vast ground floor. Interactive children's zone (Ajippa) lets families handle cultural objects from Asian countries. Special exhibitions feature major loans. Located in Dazaifu — combine with Tenmangu shrine visit. Allow 2 hours.

📍
Japan's tallest seaside tower at 234 metres, completed 1989 for Asia-Pacific Expo. Covered in 8,000 half-mirrors reflecting sky and sea. Observation deck at 123 metres provides panoramic views across Hakata Bay, Fukuoka cityscape, and surrounding mountains. Located in Momochi seaside district alongside beaches and shopping. Evening illumination patterns change seasonally — tower has become canvas for light displays including Valentine's, Christmas, and festival designs. Sky Lounge bar and restaurant on observation level. Momochihama Beach adjacent to tower offers urban beach experience. Less crowded than Tokyo or Osaka observation towers. Located near Fukuoka City Museum — can combine visits. Allow 30-45 minutes.
Massive shopping and entertainment complex designed by American architect Jon Jerde, built around artificial canal with fountain shows throughout the day. Five buildings connected by walkways contain over 250 shops, restaurants, cinema, two hotels, and performance spaces. Ramen Stadium on fifth floor gathers eight ramen shops from across Japan — visitors can sample regional styles in single location. Fountain shows synchronised to music and light run at regular intervals. Grand Hyatt Fukuoka occupies one building. Located in central Hakata between Hakata Station and Tenjin shopping district. Canal design creates unique open-air atmosphere despite being commercial complex. Popular evening destination when illuminated. Free to enter and browse.
