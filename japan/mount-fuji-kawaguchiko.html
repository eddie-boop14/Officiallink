📍
Japan's highest peak at 3,776 metres and most recognisable natural landmark. UNESCO World Heritage Site listed as cultural (not natural) site reflecting centuries of spiritual significance. Official climbing season runs July to mid-September when mountain huts operate and trails are maintained. Four main climbing routes — Yoshida Trail most popular with best infrastructure. Most climbers ascend from 5th Station (2,300m) reachable by bus, taking 5-8 hours up and 3-5 hours down. Goraiko (sunrise from summit) is climbers' primary goal — many begin ascent at night. Advance reservation now required for Yoshida Trail during peak season to manage overcrowding. Mountain weather changes rapidly with temperatures near freezing at summit even in summer. Viewing from base areas possible year-round.
Five-storey peace memorial pagoda on hillside of Arakurayama Sengen Park, famous for views framing pagoda with Mount Fuji in background — one of Japan's most photographed and reproduced images. Pagoda built 1963 as war memorial, reached by climbing 398 stone steps from park entrance. Cherry trees lining stairway and surrounding hillside make spring (mid-April) the most sought-after photography season. Autumn foliage equally spectacular. View dependent on weather — clear mornings most reliable for Fuji visibility. Located in Fujiyoshida city, accessible by train from Kawaguchiko area. Observation deck behind pagoda provides the classic viewpoint. Free to visit park and pagoda grounds. Allow 1 hour including climb.
Five volcanic lakes at Mount Fuji's northern base: Kawaguchiko (largest, most developed), Yamanakako (largest by surface area), Saiko (quietest), Shojiko (smallest), and Motosuko (deepest — Fuji view here depicted on 1,000-yen banknote). Kawaguchiko is tourism hub with hotels, museums, and lakeside cycling paths. Retro Bus routes connect lakes and surrounding attractions. Mount Fuji reflected in still lake water (known as "inverted Fuji" or sakasa-Fuji) is iconic image — best seen in early morning calm. Each lake has distinct character from developed resort to remote wilderness. Kawaguchiko Ohashi Bridge and northern shore offer classic Fuji viewpoints. Area approximately 2 hours from Tokyo by bus or train.

Museum designed by Shigeru Ban (Pritzker Prize architect) in Fujinomiya explaining Mount Fuji's cultural and spiritual significance. Building features inverted cone structure reflected in water pool to create Fuji silhouette. Interior spiral ramp ascent simulates climbing the mountain — walls display photographs, art, and information progressing from base to summit. Exhibits cover geological formation, religious pilgrimages, artistic depictions (including Hokusai's woodblock prints), and natural ecology. Rooftop terrace provides Fuji views. Located on southern approach to Mount Fuji in Fujinomiya, separate from Kawaguchiko area. Comprehensive introduction before climbing or visiting Fuji region. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
Eight spring-fed ponds in small village between Lake Kawaguchiko and Lake Yamanakako, fed by snowmelt from Mount Fuji filtered through volcanic rock over approximately 80 years. Crystal-clear water reveals depths of up to 8 metres. Designated National Natural Monument and UNESCO World Heritage component site. Traditional thatched-roof farmhouses surround ponds creating postcard-ready scenes with Fuji backdrop. Each pond has individual name and legend. Village atmosphere preserved with water mills, gardens, and souvenir shops selling local crafts. Water so pure it was historically used for purification before pilgrimages up Mount Fuji. Compact village walkable in 30-45 minutes. Can be crowded midday with tour groups.
Museum dedicated to Kubota Itchiku (1917-2003), textile artist who spent decades reviving the lost tsujigahana silk dyeing technique from the Muromachi period. Displayed kimonos function as wearable paintings — "Symphony of Light" series depicts Mount Fuji across seasons using kimonos as continuous landscape when displayed side by side. Building designed by Kubota himself using Gaudi-inspired organic architecture with coral limestone walls and natural wood. Gardens feature maple trees, moss, and pyramid structure. Located on southern shore of Lake Kawaguchiko. Michelin Green Guide awarded three stars. One of Japan's most unusual art museums. Allow 1 hour.

→
📍
Dense forest growing on hardened lava flows from Mount Fuji's 864 AD eruption. Trees and roots spread over uneven volcanic rock creating distinctive landscape where moss-covered lava formations and tree roots intertwine. Forest floor's porous lava absorbs sound creating unusual quiet. Multiple marked hiking trails of varying lengths traverse the forest including paths to Ryuguketu and Narusawa ice caves — lava tubes containing year-round ice formations. Saiko Bat Cave within forest area. Forest borders Lake Saiko and Lake Shojiko. Marked trails are safe and well-maintained. Compass anomalies from volcanic iron deposits are real but minor. Located on Fuji's northwest base, accessible by bus from Kawaguchiko Station.
Open-air museum of 20 restored thatched-roof farmhouses (kayabuki) on the shore of Lake Saico, reconstructed after the original village was destroyed by typhoon in 1966. Each farmhouse repurposed as craft workshop, gallery, shop, or café showcasing traditional Japanese culture — pottery painting, incense making, papermaking, and local foods. Village set against Mount Fuji backdrop across Lake Saiko when conditions are clear. Smaller and quieter alternative to Shirakawa-go with similar architectural style. Photography extremely rewarding in all seasons. Located on Lake Saiko's western shore, accessible by bus from Kawaguchiko. Combined visit with Aokigahara Forest and lake natural. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
