📍
World-famous site where wild Japanese macaques (snow monkeys) bathe in natural hot springs, particularly during winter when surrounding mountains are snow-covered. Troop of approximately 160 monkeys visit the outdoor onsen pools daily — behaviour began in 1963 when young monkeys imitated humans bathing. Park reached by 30-minute forest walk from parking area through atmospheric woodland (path can be icy in winter — appropriate footwear essential). Monkeys most reliably present at pools in cold weather (December-March) — summer visits less guaranteed. Photography encouraged but flash prohibited. Monkeys are wild — maintain distance and avoid direct eye contact. Located in Yamanouchi, approximately 40 minutes northeast of Nagano city by bus. Allow 2-3 hours including walking time.
Pristine alpine valley at 1,500 metres elevation within Chubu Sangaku National Park. Kappa Bridge spanning crystal-clear Azusa River against backdrop of Hotaka mountain range (3,190m) is iconic image. Walking trails ranging from easy riverside paths (1-2 hours) to serious mountain ascents. Taisho Pond created by 1915 volcanic eruption contains ghostly standing dead trees in turquoise water. Myojin Pond reflects surrounding peaks. Private vehicles prohibited — access only by bus or taxi from Matsumoto (1.5 hours) or Takayama (1 hour). Open mid-April to mid-November only (road closed in winter). Bear country — bells recommended on trails. Discovered by British missionary Walter Weston in 1890s who helped establish Japanese mountaineering culture.

📍
One of Japan's twelve surviving original castles, featuring the oldest five-storey six-floor wooden keep in the country (completed circa 1594). Nicknamed Karasu-jo (Crow Castle) for its striking black exterior — contrasting dramatically with Himeji's White Heron. Castle keep reflected in surrounding moat with Japanese Alps backdrop creates one of Japan's most photographed castle scenes. Interior wooden structure preserves steep staircases, gun ports, and moon-viewing turret (Tsukimi Yagura) added in peaceful Edo era for aesthetic appreciation rather than defence. National Treasure designation alongside Himeji, Hikone, Inuyama, and Matsue. Red bridge over moat enhances composition. Cherry blossom season and winter snow create seasonal variations. Located in central Matsumoto, 10-minute walk from station. Allow 1-2 hours.
Preserved post towns along the historic Nakasendo highway that connected Kyoto to Edo (Tokyo) through mountain interior. Narai-juku stretches one kilometre with continuous wooden Edo-period facades — Japan's longest preserved post town street. Tsumago-juku (in neighbouring Gifu Prefecture) was first post town to be protected, banning powerlines and modern alterations. Walking section between Tsumago and Magome (approximately 8km, 2-3 hours) through forested mountain pass recreates experience of Edo-era travellers. Stone-paved paths, cypress forests, waterfalls, and tea houses along route. Both towns contain traditional ryokan inns, craft shops, and wooden architecture unchanged for centuries. Narai accessible by train from Matsumoto (approximately 1 hour). Tsumago-Magome walk requires bus connection between towns.

→
📍
One of Japan's most important temples, founded 7th century to house the first Buddhist image brought to Japan (now hidden and never publicly displayed — a replica shown every six years during Gokaicho ceremony). Non-sectarian temple welcoming all Buddhist schools and visitors regardless of belief — unusual in Japan where temples typically belong to specific sects. Okaidan passage beneath main hall allows visitors to walk through complete darkness searching for a metal key on the wall — touching it guarantees salvation. Main hall rebuilt 1707 is third largest wooden building in Japan. Approach street (Nakamise) lined with traditional shops, temples, and lodgings. Morning prayer ceremony (O-Asaji) at dawn open to all visitors. Located in central Nagano, 15-minute walk from station. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
Complex of five shrines spread across Mount Togakushi, connected by forest paths through 900-year-old cedar trees. Okusha (inner shrine) approached via two-kilometre path through avenue of 400-year-old giant cedars — one of Japan's most atmospheric shrine approaches. Middle shrine (Chusha) and lower shrine (Hokosha) more accessible near village. Shrines connected to Japanese mythology — Mount Togakushi believed to be fragment of heavenly rock cave that hid sun goddess Amaterasu. Historically associated with ninja training — Togakure-ryu ninja school originated here. Togakushi Folk Museum and Ninja Museum in village. Area famous for Togakushi soba noodles made with locally grown buckwheat. Located approximately 1 hour northwest of Nagano city by bus. Allow half day for full shrine walk.

📍
Museum in Matsumoto-born Yayoi Kusama's hometown featuring permanent collection of her works alongside other local and international artists. Giant outdoor tulip sculpture by Kusama at entrance — her trademark polka-dot motifs recognisable from across the street. Permanent Kusama gallery displays paintings, sculptures, and installations spanning her career. Rotating exhibitions feature contemporary Japanese and international art. Kusama's massive pumpkin sculptures and infinity room concepts represented (though smaller scale than dedicated Kusama museums). Located in central Matsumoto, 15-minute walk from station and en route to castle. Modern building with gallery shop carrying Kusama merchandise. Allow 1 hour.
Japan's largest wasabi farm spanning 15 hectares in Azumino, fed by crystal-clear snowmelt from the Japanese Alps. Wasabi requires exceptionally pure, cold, flowing water — Azumino's natural spring water creates ideal growing conditions. Farm open to visitors with walking paths through wasabi fields, waterwheel area (filmed by Kurosawa for "Dreams"), and clear-water streams. Restaurant and food stalls serve wasabi-flavoured specialities including wasabi ice cream, wasabi soba, wasabi beer, and wasabi croquettes. Fresh wasabi root available for purchase — flavour dramatically different from common paste imitation. Located 30 minutes from Matsumoto by car or bicycle (area popular for cycling). Free to enter grounds and walk through the farm. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
