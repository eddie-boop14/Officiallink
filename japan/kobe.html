Kobe's waterfront combines Harborland shopping and dining complex with adjacent Meriken Park public space on the harbour. Kobe Port Tower (108 metres, distinctive red lattice hyperboloid structure completed 1963) offers panoramic harbour and mountain views — reopened 2024 after renovation. Kobe Maritime Museum's dramatic white sail-shaped roof houses ship models and port history alongside Kawasaki Good Times World (motorcycle and transportation exhibits). BE KOBE monument letters at waterfront became city's defining selfie landmark after 2017 installation. Fish Dance sculpture by Frank Gehry in nearby park. Harbour cruises depart from piers. Evening illumination across harbour area creates atmospheric night walk. Mosaic shopping mall within Harborland faces harbour. Free to walk waterfront areas.
Herb garden and flower park on Mount Rokko slopes reached by ropeway from behind Shin-Kobe Station. Gondola ride ascends over Nunobiki Falls (one of Japan's Top 100 Waterfalls, visible from cabin) to hilltop garden spanning 12 themed areas. Over 200 varieties of herbs and flowers across terraced hillside with panoramic views of Kobe city, harbour, and Osaka Bay. Lavender and rose gardens peak in spring and early summer. Glasshouse features tropical plants. Herb restaurant and café serve herb-infused cuisine. Foot bath using herbal water. Outdoor concert stage for seasonal events. Downhill garden walk returns to city through forest. One of Kobe's most popular attractions. Allow 2-3 hours including ropeway and garden walk.
Hillside district preserving Western-style residences (ijinkan) built by foreign merchants and diplomats during Kobe's Meiji-era opening as international treaty port from 1868. Approximately 30 houses survive, with around a dozen open to visitors as museums showing period furnishings and architecture. Weathercock House (Kazamidori no Yakata) topped with rooster weather vane is district's most iconic building. Moegi House, Uroko House (fish-scale exterior tiles), and Former Chinese Consulate among most visited. Combined passes available for multiple houses. European, American, and Chinese architectural styles reflect Kobe's diverse international community. Sloped streets between houses lined with cafés and boutiques. District walkable but hilly. Views across Kobe from upper streets. Allow 2-3 hours for multiple houses.
Museum in former Yokohama Specie Bank building (1935 neoclassical architecture) specialising in Nanban art — Japanese paintings and screens depicting the arrival of Portuguese and Spanish traders and missionaries in 16th century Japan. Nanban screens show European ships, exotic dress, and cultural encounters through Japanese artistic lens — among Japan's most fascinating historical artworks. Kobe's 150-year history as international port documented through maps, photographs, and artifacts. Collection also includes archaeological finds from nearby Hanshin region. Housed in designated Important Cultural Property building in former foreign settlement area. Located in central Kobe near Motomachi shopping street. Rotating exhibitions supplement permanent collection. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
Museum documenting the Great Hanshin-Awaji Earthquake of January 17, 1995 — magnitude 6.9 event that killed over 6,400 people and devastated Kobe. Experience begins with 7-minute immersive film recreating the earthquake and its immediate aftermath projected on giant screens with realistic sound and effects. Subsequent floors trace rescue operations, evacuation shelter life, reconstruction process, and lessons learned for disaster preparedness. Personal stories from survivors throughout. Upper floors cover broader disaster reduction science applicable worldwide. Emotionally powerful experience — different in character from Hiroshima's peace museums but equally significant. Located in HAT Kobe waterfront redevelopment area (itself rebuilt from earthquake damage). Allow 1.5-2 hours.
One of Japan's oldest Shinto shrines, founded according to tradition in 201 AD — predating even Kobe's existence as a settlement. Shrine gave Kobe its name — "Kobe" derives from "kanbe," the families who maintained the shrine. Dedicated to Wakahirume no Mikoto, younger sister of sun goddess Amaterasu. Vermillion-painted main buildings surrounded by small forest (Ikuta no Mori) in heart of Kobe's busiest commercial district — creating dramatic contrast between ancient sacred space and modern shopping. Shrine survived 1995 earthquake though surrounding buildings collapsed. Popular for hatsumode (New Year's shrine visit) — over 1.5 million visitors in first three days. Wedding ceremonies regularly held. Located between Sannomiya and Kitano districts. Free to visit. Allow 20-30 minutes.
Coastal district east of central Kobe producing approximately 30% of Japan's sake — historically Japan's most important brewing region due to Rokko mountain water (miyamizu), ideal rice-growing climate, cold winter winds (Rokko-oroshi) for brewing, and port access for shipping. Multiple brewery museums open to visitors: Hakutsuru Sake Brewery Museum (largest, recreating Edo-period brewing with life-size figures), Kiku-Masamune Sake Culture Museum (emphasising traditional brewing tools — National Cultural Property collection), and Sawanotsuru Museum (with tasting). Each offers free tasting of fresh sake. Walking route connects several breweries along a 3-kilometre coastal path. Located between central Kobe and Nishinomiya, accessible by local train. Free entry to most brewery museums. Allow half day for multiple brewery visits.
One of Japan's three major Chinatowns (alongside Yokohama and Nagasaki), established in 1868 when Kobe opened as treaty port. Compact area centred on single pedestrianised street marked by ornate Chinese gates (paifang) at each entrance. Over 100 restaurants and shops selling Chinese street food — signature items include butaman (steamed pork buns), roasted chestnuts, sesame dumplings, and xiaolongbao. Kobe's Chinese community historically influential in city's international character. Guan Di Temple (Chinese temple) within district. Chinese New Year celebrations each February feature lion dances and dragon parades. Located in Motomachi area between harbour and Kitano-cho — natural walking route connects all three. Compact enough to explore in 30-45 minutes but dining extends the visit. Free to walk and browse.
Mountain range rising 931 metres directly behind Kobe, accessible by cable car from city base. Rokko Garden Terrace at summit provides panoramic views of Kobe, Osaka Bay, and on clear days Awaji Island and Shikoku. Night views from Rokko designated one of Japan's top three night panoramas. ROKKO Forest Sound Museum (outdoor art installations), Rokko Alpine Botanical Garden, and Rokko-Shidare Observatory (mesh-dome viewpoint designed by Hiroshi Sambuichi) on summit. Cable car from summit descends north side to Arima Onsen — one of Japan's oldest hot spring towns (1,000+ years history). Arima's kinsen (gold water, iron-rich brown) and ginsen (silver water, clear carbonated) are distinctive spring types. Multiple public bathhouses and luxury ryokan. Ropeway and cable car connected on single route from Kobe through mountain to Arima.
World's longest suspension bridge with main span of 1,991 metres, connecting Kobe (Honshu) to Awaji Island (and onward to Shikoku). Completed 1998, total length 3,911 metres. Bridge World guided tour takes visitors inside bridge tower and up to maintenance walkway 300 metres above the Akashi Strait — one of Japan's most unique experiences (advance reservation essential, tours spring through autumn, weather-dependent). Maiko Marine Promenade — enclosed glass walkway 47 metres above sea level built within bridge structure — offers walking experience on the bridge with transparent floor sections. Bridge Exhibition Centre at Maiko end explains construction engineering. Adjacent Maiko Park provides classic bridge photography viewpoints. Located at Maiko, western Kobe, accessible by train. Allow 1-2 hours depending on tour level.
