Japan's largest and most sacred cemetery, stretching two kilometres through ancient cedar forest to the mausoleum of Kobo Daishi (Kukai), founder of Shingon Buddhism. Over 200,000 tombstones line moss-covered path beneath towering cedars — graves of feudal lords, samurai, corporate memorials, and ordinary citizens spanning over 1,000 years. Major feudal clan tombs include Oda Nobunaga, Toyotomi Hideyoshi, and Tokugawa family monuments. Torodo (Hall of Lamps) before Kobo Daishi's mausoleum contains over 10,000 lanterns, two burning continuously for 1,000 years. Belief holds that Kobo Daishi is not dead but in eternal meditation — monks deliver meals to mausoleum twice daily. Night walking tour through cemetery creates unforgettable atmosphere. Path free to walk at any time including after dark. Allow 2 hours.
Head temple of Koyasan Shingon Buddhism administering over 3,600 temples nationwide. Current main building dates from 1863. Banryutei rock garden — Japan's largest at 2,340 square metres — represents pair of dragons emerging from clouds using 140 granite stones from Shikoku. Ohiroma reception rooms feature painted sliding doors (fusuma) by master Kano school artists. Yanagi-no-Ma (Willow Room) is site where Toyotomi Hidetsugu was ordered to commit ritual suicide in 1595 — willow painted on door panels symbolises the event. Matcha served in tatami room overlooking garden. Temple serves as Koyasan's administrative headquarters. Located centrally on the mountain plateau. Allow 1 hour.
Central temple complex marking the spot where Kobo Daishi established Koyasan in 816 AD. Konpon Daito (Great Pagoda) — vermillion two-storey pagoda standing 48.5 metres — represents centre of a mandala encompassing all of Japan. Interior houses five large Buddha statues and 16 pillar paintings creating three-dimensional mandala. Kondo (Golden Hall) serves as main ceremonial hall. Fudo-do (oldest building on Koyasan, circa 1197) predates other structures. Complex contains multiple halls, pagodas, and sacred spaces across landscaped grounds. Legend states Kobo Daishi threw a three-pronged ritual instrument (sanko) from China that landed in a pine tree here — the pine still stands. Located at western end of Koyasan's central plateau. Allow 1 hour.
Over 50 of Koyasan's 117 temples offer shukubo (temple lodging) — overnight accommodation where guests sleep in tatami rooms, eat Buddhist vegetarian cuisine (shojin ryori), and can participate in morning prayer ceremonies and meditation. Meals prepared entirely without meat, fish, onion, or garlic following Buddhist precepts — elaborate multi-course vegetarian kaiseki served in guest rooms. Morning prayer (gongyo) typically begins 6:00-6:30am with chanting in main hall — attendance optional but strongly recommended. Ajikan meditation sessions offered at some temples. Gardens and architecture vary by temple — some date from medieval period with Important Cultural Property buildings. Advance reservation essential — booking through central Koyasan Shukubo Association or individual temples. Unique accommodation experience found nowhere else in Japan at this scale.
Massive vermillion gate marking the traditional western entrance to Koyasan's sacred mountain precinct, standing 25.1 metres tall. Current structure rebuilt 1705 after fire destroyed previous gate. Two Nio guardian statues (Kongorikishi) flanking entrance are second largest in Japan after those at Todai-ji in Nara. Gate historically marked the point where pilgrims entered the sacred realm after ascending mountain trails. Panoramic sunset views across Kii mountain range visible from gate area — one of Koyasan's finest viewpoints. Located at the end of the main road through town, short walk from central Koyasan. Free to visit. The Choishi-michi pilgrimage trail from the base of the mountain ends here — hikers completing the traditional ascent pass through this gate as their arrival point.
Museum housing Koyasan's collection of Buddhist art spanning over 1,200 years — approximately 50,000 items including 21 National Treasures and 143 Important Cultural Properties. Displays rotate seasonally and include Heian and Kamakura-period Buddhist paintings, mandala scrolls, sculpture, ritual implements, and calligraphy by Kobo Daishi himself. Collection represents one of Japan's finest assemblages of esoteric Buddhist art. Building located between Danjo Garan and Kongobu-ji in central Koyasan. Many items too fragile for permanent display, making each visit potentially unique. Allow 1 hour. Provides essential context for understanding Shingon Buddhist art and iconography seen throughout Koyasan's temples.

Ancient 24-kilometre pilgrimage trail ascending from Kudoyama (base of mountain) to Koyasan's Daimon Gate, marked by 180 stone pillars (choishi) placed at approximately 109-metre intervals along the route. Trail follows path used by pilgrims for over 1,000 years through cedar forest, past small shrines, and over mountain passes. Designated UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of Sacred Sites and Pilgrimage Routes in the Kii Mountain Range. Full walk takes 6-7 hours of steady hiking. Trail begins at Jison-in temple in Kudoyama, accessible by train from Osaka/Namba. Well-maintained path with stone markers — navigation straightforward. Shorter sections possible from intermediate points. Physically demanding with significant elevation gain. Best attempted spring through autumn.
Among the grandest individual tomb complexes within Okunoin cemetery, the Tokugawa family mausoleum comprises twin structures for first shogun Ieyasu and second shogun Hidetada. Buildings feature elaborate stone and wood construction reflecting the shogunate's wealth and power — among the most impressive individual tombs in the cemetery. Numerous other feudal lord (daimyo) tombs cluster nearby, as maintaining proximity to both Kobo Daishi and the Tokugawa was politically significant. Stone-lined approach passes through forest. Tombstones of rival clans who fought against each other in life now stand peacefully side by side — a reflection of Okunoin's sacred neutrality. Located along main Okunoin path, approximately halfway between Ichinohashi bridge entrance and Kobo Daishi's mausoleum. Free to visit as part of Okunoin walk.
