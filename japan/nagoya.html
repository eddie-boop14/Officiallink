Built 1612 by Tokugawa Ieyasu as seat for his ninth son, one of Japan's largest and most important castles. Famous for twin golden shachihoko (dolphin-tiger fish) ornaments atop the keep — symbols of Nagoya. Original castle destroyed by World War II bombing in 1945, concrete reconstruction completed 1959 houses museum. Honmaru Goten (palace) reconstructed 2018 in wood using original 17th-century techniques — lavishly decorated with Kano school paintings on sliding doors across multiple audience chambers recreating feudal opulence. Palace considered most authentic large-scale wooden reconstruction in modern Japan. Ambitious plan to rebuild main keep entirely in wood ongoing (current concrete keep closed to entry). Surrounding moat and stone walls substantially original. Cherry blossom season draws significant visitors. Allow 2 hours for castle and palace.
One of Japan's most revered Shinto shrines, believed to house Kusanagi no Tsurugi — the sacred sword that is one of Japan's three Imperial Regalia alongside the mirror at Ise and jewel at Imperial Palace. Founded approximately 1,900 years ago. Sword never publicly displayed. Shrine grounds span 19 hectares of dense forest creating isolated atmosphere within city. Main hall rebuilt 1955 in shinmei-zukuri style matching Ise Grand Shrine. Treasure Hall displays over 4,000 donated historical artifacts including swords, masks, and paintings. Over nine million visitors annually — second only to Ise Grand Shrine in attendance. Oda Nobunaga prayed here before his famous Battle of Okehazama victory in 1560. Free to visit grounds. Allow 1 hour.
Private museum housing over 10,000 heirlooms of the Owari Tokugawa family — one of three senior Tokugawa branch families. Collection includes swords, armour, tea ceremony objects, Noh masks and costumes, lacquerware, and paintings. Most celebrated treasure is the 12th-century Illustrated Tale of Genji handscroll — oldest surviving illustrated narrative scroll in Japan and National Treasure (displayed briefly each November, high-quality replicas shown year-round). Recreated feudal lord's living quarters show aristocratic Edo-period lifestyle. Adjacent Tokugawa-en Garden designed in Edo-period strolling style with pond, tea house, waterfalls, and bridges — separate admission or combined ticket. Located in Nagoya's eastern district. Allow 1.5-2 hours for museum and garden.
Housed in original 1911 red-brick Toyota spinning plant, tracing Toyota's evolution from Toyoda Sakichi's automatic loom inventions through to modern automobile manufacturing. Textile Machinery Pavilion demonstrates working looms from hand-operated to Sakichi's revolutionary Type G automatic loom (still operating). Automobile Pavilion shows car manufacturing from engine casting through final assembly with working industrial robots performing tasks on display models. Technoland hands-on area lets children operate textile machines and experience engineering concepts. Fascinating even for visitors with no automotive interest — story of Japanese industrialisation told through one family's innovations. Located in Nishi ward near Nagoya Station. Allow 2-3 hours.
Railway museum operated by JR Central displaying 39 historic rolling stock vehicles from steam locomotives through Shinkansen bullet trains to experimental SCMAGLEV (superconducting magnetic levitation) trains. Centrepiece vehicles include original Series 0 Shinkansen (1964), current N700 series, and the L0 Series SCMAGLEV that achieved world speed record of 603km/h. Shinkansen simulator allows visitors to drive virtual bullet train. Massive HO-gauge model railway layout recreates journey from Tokyo to Osaka. Exhibits explain engineering behind high-speed rail. Located at Kinjofuto Station on Aonami Line from Nagoya Station. Extremely popular — advance online ticket purchase recommended. Allow 2-3 hours. Essential for anyone interested in Japan's defining transportation technology.
Science museum recognisable by massive silver sphere housing Brother Earth planetarium — world's largest planetarium dome at 35 metres diameter. Planetarium shows project over 9,000 stars with realistic Milky Way projection (shows in Japanese but visual experience transcends language). Three-building complex covers Modern Technology, Life Sciences, and General Science across multiple floors. Highlights include minus-30°C aurora experience room, 9-metre high artificial tornado demonstration, and deep-freeze laboratory. Large-scale electricity discharge demonstrations. Located in Nagoya's Sakae central district within Shirakawa Park alongside Nagoya City Art Museum. Advance planetarium ticket reservation recommended as sessions sell out. Allow 2-3 hours.
Buddhist temple founded 1333, relocated to current site by Tokugawa Ieyasu in 1612. Main hall houses Kannon (goddess of mercy) statue. Temple library holds over 15,000 classical texts including Japan's oldest copy of the Kojiki (Record of Ancient Matters, 712 AD) — Japan's oldest surviving literary work. Surrounding Osu Shopping Arcade is Nagoya's most vibrant commercial district — covered shopping streets radiating from temple filled with electronics shops, second-hand clothing, anime merchandise, cosplay stores, traditional craft shops, and international food stalls. Monthly antique market (18th and 28th) held in temple grounds. Osu district considered Nagoya's answer to Tokyo's Akihabara merged with temple-town atmosphere. Free to visit temple. Shopping arcade free to browse.
Unique non-sectarian Buddhist temple founded 1904 to enshrine relics of Buddha gifted by King of Siam (Thailand) to Japan. Buildings reflect multiple Asian Buddhist architectural traditions — Thai, Chinese, Japanese, and Indian elements across different structures within single complex. Five-storey pagoda blends Japanese and Indian design. Gate donated by Chinese Buddhist community. Grounds include memorial monuments and donated structures from Buddhist nations across Asia. Located in leafy Kakuozan neighbourhood — surrounding streets form charming shopping area with traditional sweet shops, cafés, and boutiques. Kakuozan Shopping Street extends from temple. Monthly market (21st of each month) draws antique and craft vendors. Peaceful temple grounds free to visit. Allow 30-45 minutes for temple, longer exploring Kakuozan.
Oasis 21 is a multi-level public park and transit hub in Sakae featuring "Spaceship Aqua" — an illuminated glass water-filled roof structure elevated above ground level. Visitors walk atop the glass ceiling looking down through water to shopping levels below. Evening illumination creates signature Nagoya night scene. Ground level contains shops, restaurants, and bus terminal. Adjacent Nagoya TV Tower (Chubu Electric Power MIRAI TOWER), completed 1954 as Japan's first concentrated radio tower, predating Tokyo Tower by four years. Recently renovated with Sky Balcony at 90 metres and luxury hotel installed within tower structure. Combined Oasis 21 and tower create Nagoya's most recognisable modern landmark pairing. Located in central Sakae district. Free to access Oasis 21 rooftop.
Cultural complex on original site of Noritake porcelain company (founded 1904), one of Japan's most prestigious ceramics brands. Craft Centre demonstrates porcelain making from painting to firing — visitors can try hand-painting their own plate. Noritake Museum displays historical pieces from Meiji era through Art Deco designs to modern tableware, tracing evolution of Japanese porcelain for international markets. Red-brick former factory buildings (1904) preserved as historical monuments within landscaped gardens. Biotope garden with pond and wildlife habitat on former industrial land. Noritake Shop sells current lines and factory seconds at discount. Located near Nagoya Station, 15-minute walk. Free to enter garden grounds — museum and craft centre require tickets. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
