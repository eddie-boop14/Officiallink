📍
Head shrine of over 30,000 Inari shrines across Japan, dedicated to god of rice and prosperity. Famous for thousands of vermillion torii gates forming tunnels up Mount Inari (233m). Full hiking trail to summit takes approximately 2-3 hours round trip with multiple sub-shrines along route. Senbon Torii (thousand gates) section near base is most photographed area. Gates donated by businesses and individuals — names and dates inscribed on reverse. Fox (kitsune) statues throughout grounds serve as Inari's divine messengers. Open 24 hours with gates illuminated at night. Founded 711 AD, predating Kyoto's establishment as capital. Early morning or evening visits avoid peak crowds.
Zen temple whose top two floors are completely covered in gold leaf, reflected in surrounding mirror pond (Kyoko-chi). Originally built 1397 as retirement villa for Shogun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu. Current structure dates from 1955 reconstruction after monk's arson in 1950 — event immortalised in Yukio Mishima's novel. Each floor represents different architectural style: aristocratic Shinden, samurai Bukke, and Chinese Zen. Surrounding strolling garden designated Special Place of Scenic Beauty. Admission ticket is a paper charm (ofuda) rather than standard ticket. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Particularly striking in winter snow and autumn foliage. Allow approximately 1 hour for temple grounds and garden.
Founded 778 AD, famous for massive wooden stage extending from main hall over hillside supported by 139 pillars without single nail. Stage stands 13 metres above ground with panoramic views across Kyoto. Japanese expression "jumping off the stage at Kiyomizu" means taking a leap of faith. Otowa Waterfall below stage has three streams — visitors drink from one for longevity, academic success, or love (drinking from all three considered greedy). Approach via Sannen-zaka and Ninen-zaka preserved historical lanes lined with traditional shops. Night illuminations during spring cherry blossom and autumn foliage seasons. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Major renovation completed 2020 restored cypress bark roof.
Zen temple renowned for its karesansui (dry landscape) rock garden — 15 stones arranged on raked white gravel in a rectangle viewed from temple veranda. Garden designed so that from any seated viewing angle, only 14 stones are visible simultaneously. Creator and exact meaning remain debated since 15th century. Surrounding temple grounds include large pond garden (Kyoyochi) with walking paths predating rock garden. Oil clay wall surrounding rock garden has faded over centuries creating subtle patterns valued as part of the aesthetic experience. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Best experienced in contemplative silence rather than crowds. Allow 1 hour for rock garden viewing and pond garden walk.
Built 1482 as retirement villa for Shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa, inspired by grandfather's Golden Pavilion but never actually covered in silver despite its name. Represents wabi-sabi aesthetic — beauty in imperfection and simplicity. Kogetsudai sand cone and Ginshadan raked sand platform designed to reflect moonlight. Two-storey pavilion combines Japanese and Chinese architectural elements. Moss garden and hillside walking path offer elevated views of temple grounds and eastern Kyoto mountains beyond. Located at northern end of Philosopher's Path canal walk linking several temples. Admission ticket is calligraphic paper charm. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Allow approximately 45 minutes to one hour.
120-metre-long wooden hall (Japan's longest wooden structure) containing 1,001 life-size gilded Kannon (goddess of mercy) statues arranged in ten rows of fifty flanking a central seated figure. Each statue carved in 13th century with slightly different face — said that visitors will find one resembling someone they know. Twenty-eight guardian deity statues stand before the rows representing Hindu and Buddhist protective figures including Garuda and Ashura. Building name means "33 spaces between pillars" referencing 33 forms Kannon takes to save humanity. Famous for annual Toshiya archery contest held each January along building's length. No interior photography permitted.
One of Kyoto's Five Great Zen Temples, founded 1236. Tsutenkyo Bridge spanning ravine offers spectacular views of maple trees in autumn — considered one of Kyoto's finest foliage viewpoints attracting enormous crowds during peak colour in late November. Hojo garden designed in 1939 by Mirei Shigemori combines traditional elements with modern geometric patterns in four cardinal-direction gardens. Sanmon Gate is Japan's oldest surviving Zen main gate (15th century). Temple complex contains 24 sub-temples. Main hall features dragon ceiling painting. Less crowded than Kinkaku-ji and Kiyomizu-dera outside autumn season. Spring moss garden equally beautiful. Allow 1-2 hours for main temple and gardens.
Ranked first among Kyoto's Five Great Zen Temples, founded 1339 by Shogun Ashikaga Takauji. Sogenchi Pond Garden designed by famous monk Muso Soseki is one of Japan's earliest surviving landscape gardens — designated first Special Place of Scenic Beauty and first Special Historic Site. Garden borrows scenery from Arashiyama mountains behind (shakkei technique). Current buildings largely reconstructed after eight fires over centuries. Painting of dragon on ceiling of Dharma Hall by Kayama Matazo completed 1997. Rear exit connects directly to Arashiyama Bamboo Grove. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Allow 1 hour for temple and garden exploration.

Built 1603 by Tokugawa Ieyasu as Kyoto residence of the Tokugawa shoguns. Ninomaru Palace features famous "nightingale floors" (uguisubari) that chirp when walked upon — designed as security measure against intruders. Interior decorated with over 3,000 paintings by Kano school artists across sliding doors and walls. Ohiroma reception hall where Tokugawa Yoshinobu announced return of power to Emperor in 1867, ending shogunate rule. Extensive gardens include Ninomaru Garden (designed by tea master Kobori Enshu) and Seiryu-en Garden. Honmaru Palace undergoing multi-year restoration. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
Served as Emperor's residence from 1331 until capital moved to Tokyo in 1869. Current structures date from 1855 reconstruction in traditional Heian-period style. Shishinden (Ceremonial Hall) where enthronement ceremonies took place, Seiryoden (Emperor's daily quarters), and Kogosho (council chamber) visible on guided route through grounds. Oikeniwa pond garden designed for imperial enjoyment. Palace sits within Kyoto Gyoen National Garden — expansive park surrounding palace open to public year-round for walking, cycling, and seasonal flower viewing. Free guided tours of palace interior available through Imperial Household Agency without advance reservation. Surrounding parkland popular with locals for relaxation and picnicking.
Path through dense bamboo forest on outskirts of western Kyoto. Towering bamboo stalks create cathedral-like atmosphere with light filtering through canopy overhead. Main path runs approximately 500 metres from Tenryu-ji temple north gate to Okochi Sanso villa. Sound of wind through bamboo designated by Japanese Ministry of Environment as one of "100 Soundscapes of Japan." Extremely popular resulting in significant crowds midday — early morning visits strongly recommended for atmospheric experience and best photography. Rickshaw rides available through bamboo and surrounding Arashiyama district. Nearby attractions include Togetsukyo Bridge, monkey park, and riverside dining. Free to walk through at any time.

Founded 1897, one of Japan's three major national museums specialising in pre-modern Japanese and Asian art. Heisei Chishinkan Wing (2014) designed by Yoshio Taniguchi displays permanent collection across three floors including Buddhist sculpture, ceramics, textiles, and metalwork. Meiji-era red brick building (original museum) used for special exhibitions. Collection exceeds 12,000 items spanning pottery, painting, calligraphy, and lacquerwork. Located in Higashiyama area near Sanjusangen-do and accessible on foot from Kiyomizu-dera area. Major special exhibitions draw significant attendance. Garden between buildings contains tea house. Allow 2 hours minimum. Photography policies vary by exhibition.
Narrow five-block covered shopping street operating for over 400 years as Kyoto's principal food market. Over 100 shops and stalls selling Kyoto-specific specialities including tsukemono (pickled vegetables), tofu, yuba (tofu skin), matcha sweets, fresh seafood, and Japanese knives. Many shops offer tasting samples allowing visitors to experience Kyoto's distinctive culinary traditions. Several specialist shops have operated from same location for generations. Aritsugu knife shop established 1560 remains one of Japan's most respected cutlery makers. Market runs parallel to Shijo shopping street between Teramachi and Takakura streets. Each shop independently operated. Busiest midday to early afternoon.
Phoenix Hall (Hoo-do) built 1053 is the only surviving original structure — depicted on Japanese 10-yen coin. Designed to represent Amida Buddha's palace in the Pure Land paradise, reflected perfectly in frontal pond. Interior houses seated Amida Buddha statue by master sculptor Jocho surrounded by 52 cloud-riding bodhisattva figures. Modern Hoshokan museum displays original phoenix roof ornaments, temple bell, and cloud bodhisattvas up close. Located in Uji, approximately 30 minutes south of central Kyoto by train. Uji also famous as Japan's premier matcha tea region — tea shops and cafés line approach to temple. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Interior viewing requires timed entry.
Ancient shrine dedicated to water deity Takaokami, nestled in mountain valley north of central Kyoto. Stone lantern-lined approach path ascending through cedar forest is one of Kyoto's most atmospheric walks and among the city's most photographed scenes. Water fortune papers (mizuura mikuji) placed on water surface to reveal fortune — unique to this shrine. Three shrine buildings along mountain path: Hongu (main), Yui-no-Yashiro (marriage deity), and Oku-no-Miya (inner shrine). Summer months, restaurants along Kibune river build platforms (kawadoko) directly over rushing water for riverside dining. Eizan Railway and short hike from Kurama area. Cooler temperatures than central Kyoto in summer. Beautiful in all seasons.
