🇯🇵 Kanazawa Castle Park
Former seat of the Maeda clan, second wealthiest feudal domain after the Tokugawa.
Free
→
📍
Ranked alongside Okayama's Koraku-en and Mito's Kairaku-en as Japan's three most beautiful landscape gardens. Name means "garden combining six attributes" — spaciousness, seclusion, artifice, antiquity, waterways, and panoramas. Developed over two centuries by successive Maeda feudal lords from 1676. Kotoji-toro stone lantern with two legs of different length standing at Kasumigaike Pond is Kanazawa's most photographed symbol. Oldest fountain in Japan uses natural water pressure from elevated reservoir. Snow-hanging ropes (yukitsuri) protect pine trees each winter creating iconic cone-shaped silhouettes. Garden changes dramatically across seasons — cherry blossom, irises, autumn foliage, and snow. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
Former seat of the Maeda clan, second wealthiest feudal domain after the Tokugawa. Ishikawa-mon Gate (1788) and Sanjikken Nagaya storehouse survive as Important Cultural Properties. Hishi Yagura diamond-shaped turret, Gojikken Nagaya multi-defence storehouse, and Hashizume-mon Tsuzuki Yagura turret reconstructed using traditional techniques without modern nails or bolts. Distinctive white lead-tile roofing unique to Kanazawa Castle. Gyokusen-inmaru Garden restored from historical records features tiered waterfall and tea house. Castle grounds connect directly to Kenroku-en. Park grounds free to explore. Stone wall construction techniques vary across castle — guided stone wall tours explain different eras. Allow 1 hour.

Best-preserved geisha district in Japan alongside Kyoto's Gion, dating from 1820. Traditional wooden tea houses (chaya) with characteristic lattice facades line atmospheric streets. Ochaya Shima and Kaikaro tea houses open to visitors — interiors display traditional instruments, lacquerware, and red-walled entertainment rooms where geisha performed. Geisha still practise in the district. Gold-leaf workshops open for viewing and purchasing — Kanazawa produces 99% of Japan's gold leaf. Converted tea houses now serve as cafés, craft shops, and galleries. Hakuza gold-leaf shop has gold-leaf-covered room and sells gold-leaf ice cream. District designated Important Preservation District for Groups of Traditional Buildings. Free to walk through streets.
Circular glass building designed by SANAA (Sejima and Nishizawa) with no front or back — entry from any direction. Permanent installations include Leandro Erlich's "Swimming Pool" — visitors view people appearing to walk underwater through glass ceiling, while others enter room beneath pool looking up through water layer. James Turrell's "Blue Planet Sky" creates meditative open-roofed room framing sky. Rotating exhibitions showcase international and Japanese contemporary artists. Public zones around building free to access with outdoor sculptures. Exhibition zone requires ticket. Located central Kanazawa between Kenroku-en and shopping district. One of Japan's most visited contemporary art museums. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
Residential quarter where middle-ranking samurai of the Maeda clan lived during Edo period. Earthen walls (tsuchikabe), private gates, and narrow waterways along streets preserve feudal-era atmosphere. Nomura Samurai House open to visitors — interior displays armour, swords, scrolls, and features small but exquisite garden with waterfall, stone bridge, and ancient trees ranked among Japan's top gardens despite tiny size. Shinise Memorial Hall (former pharmacy) shows merchant family life. Kanazawa Shinise Memorial Hall displays local crafts. Located between Kanazawa Castle and Katamachi shopping area. Free to walk through district streets. Earthen walls draped with straw mats (komogake) each winter to protect from snow — seasonal tradition unique to Kanazawa.
Museum dedicated to Suzuki Daisetsu Teitaro, Kanazawa-born philosopher who introduced Zen Buddhism to the Western world through writings and lectures. Designed by Yoshio Taniguchi (also designed MoMA renovation in New York). Three wings — Learning, Thinking, and Contemplation — progress from biographical exhibits through study space to Water Mirror Garden where shallow reflecting pool creates meditative stillness. Architecture itself embodies Zen principles — clean lines, natural light, and deliberate emptiness. Interior Contemplation Space invites seated reflection facing water. Located in quiet residential area south of Kenroku-en. Small museum best appreciated slowly. Allow 45 minutes to 1 hour.

📍
Fresh market operating continuously for over 300 years, supplying Kanazawa's restaurants and households. Sea of Japan location gives Kanazawa access to exceptional seafood — market specialises in crab (winter season), sweet shrimp (amaebi), yellowtail, and seasonal catches. Over 170 shops across covered arcades. Fresh kaisendon (seafood rice bowls) available at market restaurants — queues at popular stalls indicate quality. Fruit shops sell premium Japanese fruits. Local vegetables including Kaga vegetables (traditional Kanazawa varieties) available seasonally. Second floor food court offers seated dining. Located centrally near Kanazawa Station. Morning visits see professional chefs purchasing. Each shop independently operated with varying specialities.
Nichiren Buddhist temple built 1643 as outpost for Kanazawa Castle's defences, disguised as ordinary temple to comply with Tokugawa building regulations. Interior contains hidden staircases, trap doors, secret rooms, escape tunnels, and concealed passages across four floors (externally appears to have only two). Features include pitfall traps, rooms with concealed exits behind altars, and well containing escape tunnel to Kanazawa Castle. Despite "ninja temple" nickname, no connection to actual ninja — defences designed by Maeda clan samurai. Guided tours only (Japanese language, approximately 30 minutes) — advance reservation required by telephone. No photography inside. Located in Teramachi temple district. One of Japan's most architecturally inventive historical buildings.
