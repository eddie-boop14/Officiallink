Mountain village of approximately 114 gassho-zukuri farmhouses — steep thatched roofs resembling hands in prayer (gassho) designed to shed heavy snowfall in this isolated valley. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1995. Several houses open as museums showing traditional multi-storey interiors where upper floors were used for silkworm cultivation. Wada House and Kanda House among largest and best-preserved open to visitors. Shiroyama Viewpoint above village provides classic panoramic photograph. Village remains inhabited — residents live in traditional houses alongside tourism. Winter illumination events (January-February) light snow-covered village — tickets allocated by lottery months in advance. Located in remote Gifu Prefecture, accessible by bus from Takayama (50 minutes) or Kanazawa (75 minutes). Allow 2-3 hours.
Two small hamlets (Ainokura and Suganuma) sharing UNESCO World Heritage status with Shirakawa-go but significantly smaller and quieter. Ainokura contains 20 gassho-zukuri houses in mountain valley — more intimate and less commercialised than Shirakawa-go. Suganuma's nine houses include gunpowder production museum reflecting valley's historical role manufacturing saltpetre for feudal lords. Several houses offer minshuku (traditional guesthouse) accommodation — staying overnight allows experiencing village after day visitors depart. Folk music performances featuring traditional kokiriko dance (one of Japan's oldest folk dances) held at Suganuma. Located in Toyama Prefecture between Shirakawa-go and Kanazawa. Bus connections from both cities. Allow 1.5-2 hours per hamlet.

Three parallel streets (Ichi-no-machi, Ni-no-machi, San-no-machi) preserving Edo-period merchant house facades with dark wooden lattice fronts, white-walled storehouses, and sake brewery cedar balls (sugidama). Sake breweries open tasting rooms — Takayama's mountain water produces distinctive local sake. Traditional craft shops sell Takayama's famous yatai spring/autumn festival floats miniatures, Ichii wood carvings, and Shunkei lacquerware. Morning markets (asa-ichi) operate daily along Miyagawa River and outside Takayama Jinya. Cafés in converted merchant houses serve Hida beef (regional wagyu), mitarashi dango (grilled rice dumplings), and local sweets. Atmospheric streets best experienced early morning before tour groups arrive. Free to walk through at any time.
Japan's only surviving Edo-period provincial government office (jinya), used by Tokugawa shogunate officials administering the Hida region from 1692 to 1868. Complex includes magistrate's offices, audience chambers, living quarters, rice storehouses (largest surviving Edo-era rice store in Japan), and courtroom complete with implements used during interrogations. Tatami-matted rooms with painted screens show hierarchy through room decoration levels. Garden viewable from multiple interior rooms. Audio guide explains government functions and daily administration. Located near Sanmachi historic streets — combine visits naturally. Morning market operates in grounds daily. Allow 1 hour.
Exhibition hall displaying actual yatai festival floats used in Takayama Festival — ranked alongside Kyoto's Gion Matsuri and Chichibu Night Festival as Japan's three most beautiful festivals. Floats stand up to 8 metres tall with intricate wood carvings, gold leaf decoration, lacquerwork, and embroidered curtains crafted by generations of Takayama artisans. Karakuri mechanical puppet demonstrations show 17th-century engineering that animates figures on float tops during festivals. Rotating display shows four floats at a time from collection of 23. Located within Sakurayama Hachiman Shrine grounds. Actual festivals held mid-April (spring) and early October (autumn). Hall provides year-round access to float artistry. Allow 45 minutes.

📍
Open-air museum preserving over 30 traditional houses relocated from the Hida mountain region, including gassho-zukuri thatched-roof farmhouses. Buildings arranged around pond with mountain backdrop providing insight into rural mountain life spanning several centuries. Interiors display traditional tools, furniture, and household items. Craft workshops within village buildings offer hands-on experiences — woodworking, weaving, and straw craft. Compared to Shirakawa-go, provides closer interior access to more buildings in concentrated setting. Located on hillside overlooking Takayama, short bus ride or 30-minute walk from town centre. Winter snow creates atmospheric scenes. Less crowded alternative to Shirakawa-go for understanding gassho architecture. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
Pristine alpine valley at 1,500 metres elevation within Chubu Sangaku National Park (Japanese Alps). Kappa Bridge spanning crystal-clear Azusa River against backdrop of Hotaka mountain range (3,190m) is iconic image. Walking trails ranging from easy riverside paths (1-2 hours) to serious mountain ascents. Taisho Pond created by 1915 volcanic eruption contains ghostly standing dead trees in turquoise water. Myojin Pond reflects surrounding peaks. Private vehicles prohibited — access only by bus or taxi from Takayama (1 hour) or Matsumoto (1.5 hours). Open mid-April to mid-November only (road closed in winter). Bear country — bells recommended on trails. Valley discovered by British missionary Walter Weston in 1890s who helped establish Japanese mountaineering culture.
Takayama's oldest temple, established 746 AD by order of Emperor Shomu as one of provincial temples (kokubunji) built across Japan. Three-storey pagoda (16th century) is Hida region's only remaining pagoda. Main hall houses Yakushi Nyorai (Medicine Buddha) and other Important Cultural Properties. Giant ginkgo tree in grounds estimated at over 1,200 years old — autumn golden foliage draws photographers. Tree designated National Natural Monument. Archaeological remains of original 8th-century temple layout visible in grounds. Located centrally in Takayama near train station, short walk from Sanmachi historic streets. Compact temple — allow 20-30 minutes. Often combined with Sanmachi and Jinya visits. Flea market held in grounds on specific dates.
