📍
Mausoleum of Tokugawa Ieyasu, founder of the Tokugawa shogunate that ruled Japan for 260 years. Completed 1636 using over 15,000 artisans, the shrine complex is Japan's most lavishly decorated — every surface covered in gold leaf, intricate carvings, and vibrant colour. Famous carvings include the three wise monkeys ("see no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil") on Sacred Stable, and the Sleeping Cat (Nemuri-Neko) by legendary sculptor Hidari Jingoro above gate to Ieyasu's tomb. Yomeimon Gate alone contains over 500 carvings of sages, children, birds, and mythological figures. Stone stairway beyond Sleeping Cat leads to Ieyasu's bronze tomb in cedar forest. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Allow 2 hours minimum.
Founded 766 AD by Buddhist monk Shodo Shonin who first opened Nikko as a sacred site. Sanbutsudo (Three Buddha Hall) is Nikko's largest building, housing three 8-metre gilded wooden Buddha and bodhisattva statues representing deities of Nikko's three sacred mountains. Shoyoen Garden behind Sanbutsudo is strolling garden with pond designed to incorporate surrounding mountain scenery. Treasure House displays temple's collection of Buddhist art, scrolls, and Tokugawa-era objects. Taiyuin Mausoleum (separate ticket) contains tomb of third Tokugawa shogun Iemitsu — ornate but deliberately less elaborate than grandfather Ieyasu's Tosho-gu out of respect. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
Founded 782 AD, dedicated to deities of Nikko's three sacred mountains — Mount Nantai, Mount Nyoho, and Mount Taro. Main shrine (Honsha) located between Tosho-gu and Taiyuin contains Nikko's oldest structure (1619 Honden main hall). Separate Chugushi shrine on shores of Lake Chuzenji and Okumiya inner shrine at summit of Mount Nantai. Sacred Shinkyo Bridge (separate ticket) at entrance to Nikko's shrine area spans Daiya River — original bridge built 1636 reserved for shoguns and imperial envoys. Believed to be site where monk Shodo crossed river on the backs of two serpents. Main shrine grounds include garden with ghost lantern (Bake-Toro) and haunted bronze lantern. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Allow 30-45 minutes.
Mausoleum of Tokugawa Iemitsu (third shogun, grandson of Ieyasu), deliberately designed to be slightly smaller and less ornate than Tosho-gu to show deference to his grandfather. Ascending stone stairway passes through elaborately carved gates of increasing grandeur — Nitenmon, Yashamon, and Karamon — each guarded by deity statues. Final Haiden (worship hall) and Honden (main hall) lavishly decorated with gold, black lacquer, and polished colours. Tomb faces direction of Tosho-gu — Iemitsu eternally serving his grandfather. Fewer visitors than Tosho-gu despite comparable artistry. Night-blooming celestial maidens carved on Yashamon gate. Set within atmospheric cedar forest. Part of Rinnoji Temple complex. Allow 45 minutes to 1 hour.

📍
One of Japan's three finest waterfalls, plunging 97 metres from Lake Chuzenji. Elevator descends 100 metres through rock to observation platform at base of falls providing close-up views of the main cascade and twelve smaller falls emerging from surrounding cliffs. Upper viewing platform accessible without elevator. Water volume varies seasonally — most dramatic after rainy season and snowmelt. Winter creates partial ice formations around falls. Named by Buddhist monk Shodo Shonin who discovered Lake Chuzenji. Located in Oku-Nikko area approximately 30 minutes by bus from central Nikko shrine area via Irohazaka switchback road (48 curves). Autumn foliage frames falls spectacularly. Combined visit with Lake Chuzenji natural. Allow 1 hour.
Formed over 20,000 years ago when Mount Nantai's lava flows dammed the valley. Located at 1,269 metres elevation, the lake stretches 25 kilometres around its shoreline. Lake's outflow creates Kegon Falls at its eastern end. Sightseeing boats cruise the lake offering views of surrounding mountains and lakeside shrines. Hot spring resorts along northern shore. Futarasan Shrine's Chugushi branch shrine sits on northern shore — starting point for Mount Nantai climbing season (May-October). Chuzenji Temple nearby. Italian Embassy Villa Memorial Park and British Embassy Villa Memorial Park on eastern shore preserve historic diplomatic summer retreats with lakeside gardens. Popular retreat from summer heat due to mountain elevation. Autumn colours around lake shores outstanding.

📍
Vermillion-lacquered bridge spanning Daiya River at the entrance to Nikko's shrine and temple area. One of Japan's three finest bridges. Legend states monk Shodo Shonin was unable to cross the river in 766 AD until the god Jinja-Daioh summoned two serpents (one red, one blue) to form a bridge. Current structure dates from 1636 reconstruction in Tokugawa period — historically reserved exclusively for shogun and imperial messengers. Bridge crossing requires ticket — visitors walk across and return along same route. Best photographed from downstream river bank where vermillion colour contrasts with surrounding cedar forest and river gorge. Autumn foliage and spring cherry blossom enhance setting. Located at base of path leading up to Tosho-gu and other shrines.
Largest existing wooden building in Japan combining sections from three historical periods — Edo (1632), Meiji (1899), and Taisho (1921). Originally built as summer retreat for imperial family, incorporating rooms from former Tokugawa Shogun residence in Tokyo and Kishu Tokugawa mansion. 106 rooms displaying tatami-matted formal chambers, imperial bathrooms with Western fittings, and audience halls. Japanese garden features weeping cherry trees (spectacular in spring), manicured hedges, and traditional landscaping. Emperor Hirohito spent wartime years here as child. Restored and opened to public in 2000. Located 15-minute walk from central Nikko shrine area. Less visited than shrines despite architectural significance. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
