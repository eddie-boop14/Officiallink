🇯🇵 Yakusugi Land

Free
→
📍
Yakushima's most famous tree, estimated between 2,170 and 7,200 years old (exact age debated as core too rotten to date). Trunk circumference of 16.4 metres makes it the largest conifer in Japan. Located at 1,300 metres elevation, reached by strenuous 10-hour round-trip hike (approximately 22 kilometres) from Arakawa Trailhead. First 8 kilometres follow abandoned logging railway tracks through forest, then mountain trail ascends to tree's viewing platform. Hike requires early start (4-5am), adequate fitness, proper equipment, and sufficient water and food. Advance shuttle bus reservation to trailhead required during peak season (March-November). Viewing platform keeps visitors at distance to protect root system. Despite difficulty, Japan's most popular long-distance day hike. Guide recommended but not mandatory.
Primeval forest ravine covered in over 600 species of moss, creating the ethereal green landscape that inspired animator Miyazaki Hayao's "Princess Mononoke" forest scenes. Three hiking courses available: short loop (1 hour), medium loop (3 hours reaching Bugyou Sugi cedar), and long course (5 hours reaching Taikoiwa rock with panoramic island views). Moss forest section along medium course is most atmospheric — every surface carpeted in vivid green with ancient cedars rising through mist. Yakusugi-land (separate area) offers additional cedar forest walks at varying difficulty levels. Rain enhances rather than diminishes experience — Yakushima receives extraordinary rainfall making moss forests most vivid when wet. Located 30 minutes from Miyanoura port by car. Allow 3-5 hours depending on course.
Forest park at 1,000 metres elevation providing accessible encounters with yakusugi (ancient cedar trees over 1,000 years old) through four courses of increasing difficulty. 30-minute boardwalk course suitable for most visitors passes named ancient trees including Buddhasugi and Sennensugi (Thousand Year Cedar). Longer courses (50 minutes, 80 minutes, 150 minutes) venture deeper into forest with fewer boardwalks and more natural trail. Named cedars along routes each have distinct character — twisted trunks, hollow centres, fused pairs. Less physically demanding alternative to Jomon Sugi trek while still experiencing ancient cedar forest atmosphere. Located in island's interior highlands, accessible by car or bus. Boardwalk sections wheelchair-accessible on shortest course. Allow 1-3 hours depending on course chosen.

Japan's most important loggerhead sea turtle nesting beach — one of the largest nesting sites in the North Pacific. Female turtles come ashore to lay eggs May through July under cover of darkness. Hatching season runs July through September when baby turtles emerge and scramble toward the sea. Yakushima Sea Turtle Museum adjacent to beach provides educational displays and information about conservation efforts. Night-time turtle observation tours available during nesting season with strict protocols — red light only, quiet approach, guided groups to minimise disturbance. Beach itself is beautiful 800-metre stretch of golden sand backed by forest. Outside nesting season, beach open for swimming and walking. Located on Yakushima's northwest coast. Turtle observation requires advance registration.
Yakushima's tallest waterfall at 88 metres, designated one of Japan's Top 100 Waterfalls. Plunges from granite cliff face surrounded by dense subtropical forest. Viewing platform accessible by short walk from roadside parking — one of Yakushima's most accessible natural attractions requiring no serious hiking. Water volume varies significantly with rainfall — most dramatic during and after rain periods. Senpiro Falls nearby (60 metres) offers alternative waterfall viewing and swimming pool at base during summer. Toroki Falls on the southern coast is Japan's only waterfall that drops directly into the ocean. Multiple waterfalls across the island reflect Yakushima's extraordinary rainfall — said to "rain 35 days a month." Allow 30 minutes for viewing.
Yakushima's coastal road (approximately 100km circumference) passes dramatic scenery including volcanic rock formations, tidal hot springs, and subtropical vegetation. Hirauchi Kaichu Onsen — natural rock-pool hot springs on the shoreline only accessible at low tide (submerged at high tide). Yudomari Onsen — another seaside hot spring on southern coast. Isso Beach on the northern coast offers swimming with clear water. Western coast features dramatic granite boulder coastline. Coastal villages between mountains and sea showcase traditional Yakushima life. Full island circumference drive takes approximately 3 hours without stops. Rental car essential for island exploration — public buses operate but infrequently. Driving counter-clockwise from Miyanoura port recommended. Combine coastal stops with interior forest visits across multiple days.

Free
→
📍
Exhibition centre explaining Yakushima's unique ecology — why this small island (500 square kilometres) contains climate zones from subtropical coast to subarctic mountain summit (1,936m Miyanoura-dake). Giant screen theatre shows documentary covering island's ecosystems, forest life, and geological formation. Exhibits cover yakusugi cedar ecology (why trees live so long — slow growth from poor granite soil and natural resin preserving wood), island wildlife including Yakushima macaques and deer, and human settlement history. Useful first stop upon arrival for understanding island's significance before hiking. Located in Miyanoura near main ferry port. Allow 1 hour. Provides context that enriches subsequent forest and nature visits.
Yakushima's two main port towns serving as base for island exploration. Miyanoura on the north coast is primary ferry terminal receiving high-speed hydrofoil (1 hour 45 minutes) and regular ferry (4 hours) from Kagoshima. Small town with accommodation, restaurants, rental car agencies, and supermarkets. Anbo on the east coast serves as island's administrative centre with additional accommodation and services. Flying fish (tobiuo) is Yakushima's culinary speciality — served grilled, as sashimi, and in ramen. Local shochu distilleries produce spirit from sweet potatoes and water filtered through island's granite. Both towns function as overnight bases — most visitors spend 2-3 nights minimum to cover major attractions. Yakushima accessible by air from Osaka, Fukuoka, and Kagoshima (30 minutes flight).
