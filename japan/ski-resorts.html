Four interconnected resorts on Mount Niseko Annupuri: Grand Hirafu (largest), Hanazono, Niseko Village, and Annupuri. Legendary for consistent dry powder snow — averaging 14-18 metres annual snowfall from Siberian weather systems crossing the Sea of Japan. All-Mountain Pass connects all four resorts on single ticket. Extensive backcountry access through resort gates (avalanche awareness essential). Night skiing at Grand Hirafu among Japan's best. English widely spoken — most international of Japan's ski areas with significant Australian, European, and Asian visitor base. Village nightlife, dining, and onsen (hot spring) culture after skiing. Season typically late November to early May. Located 2 hours from Sapporo New Chitose Airport. Accommodation ranges from luxury hotels to budget lodges. Summer season offers rafting, hiking, and golf.
Ten interconnected resorts across Hakuba Valley in the Japanese Alps, hosting 1998 Winter Olympic alpine and ski jumping events. Happo-one is the valley's flagship resort with Olympic downhill course and highest vertical drop. Hakuba47 and Goryu offer interconnected terrain with excellent tree skiing. Cortina offers Japan's steepest in-bounds terrain and deepest snow in the valley. Valley lift pass connects all ten resorts. More varied terrain than Niseko with steeper alpine runs and greater vertical. Significantly less crowded than Niseko on weekdays. Strong traditional Japanese village atmosphere in Hakuba town with onsen and izakaya. Located 4 hours from Tokyo by car/bus or 1 hour from Nagano Station. Season December to April with peak powder January-February.
Atmospheric ski resort inseparable from its traditional onsen village — 13 public hot spring bathhouses (sotoyu) scattered through narrow streets, free to use, fed by natural volcanic springs. Skiing spans 297 hectares across Mount Kenashi with excellent variety from groomed runs to deep powder bowls and tree skiing. Yamabiko area at top receives heaviest snowfall. Village atmosphere more authentically Japanese than Niseko — wooden buildings, temple streets, and local restaurants. Dosojin Fire Festival each January 15 is one of Japan's most spectacular fire festivals. Strong community-owned ethos — locals maintain bathhouses and welcome visitors. Located 25 minutes from Iiyama Station (Shinkansen from Tokyo, 1 hour 40 minutes). Season mid-December to early May.
Japan's largest interconnected ski area comprising 18 resorts linked by lifts and shuttle buses on single pass. Hosted 1998 Olympic giant slalom and slalom events. Highest base elevation of any major Japanese ski area (approximately 1,340-2,307 metres) ensuring reliable snow conditions and cold temperatures for quality preservation. Terrain ranges from gentle beginner slopes to Olympic-standard runs. Vast area means crowds dispersed even in peak season. Yokoteyama and Okushiga areas offer most challenging terrain. Located within Joshinetsu Kogen National Park alongside snow monkey park — combine ski trip with Jigokudani monkey visit (30 minutes away). Accommodation clusters around different resort bases. Season November to May (one of Japan's longest seasons due to altitude). Located 45 minutes from Nagano Station.
Four resorts on Mount Myoko — Akakura Onsen, Akakura Kanko, Suginohara, and Ikenotaira — each with distinct character. Suginohara boasts Japan's longest single run (8.5 kilometres). Akakura receives among the heaviest snowfall in Japan (often exceeding 12 metres annually). Tree skiing through birch forest. Strong traditional onsen culture — Akakura Onsen village has numerous hot spring ryokan (traditional inns). Less international tourism than Niseko or Hakuba — predominantly Japanese visitors creating authentic atmosphere. Direct Shinkansen access from Tokyo to Joetsu-Myoko Station (2 hours) then 30-minute bus. Emerging as alternative powder destination as Niseko grows more crowded and expensive. Season December to April. Red-tinted water in Akakura onsen from iron-rich volcanic springs.
Resort on Mount Kitanomine and Furano zones offering Hokkaido powder quality with less crowd intensity than Niseko. Hosted ten FIS Ski World Cup events. Two zones (Furano and Kitanomine) connected at summit with varied terrain across 24 courses. Excellent tree skiing in designated off-piste zones. Furano town atmosphere distinctly Japanese — famous for its lavender fields in summer, cheese and wine production, and Furano-brand melon. Night skiing available. Ningle Terrace craft village in forest at resort base. Located 2 hours from Sapporo New Chitose Airport. Combined winter/summer destination — same town transforms from ski resort to flower tourism. Less English spoken than Niseko but increasingly international. Season late November to early May.
Hokkaido's largest single ski resort spanning three mountains (Mount Isola, East Mountain, West Mountain) with 37 courses and extensive tree skiing terrain. Receives same Siberian powder systems as nearby Niseko but with significantly fewer visitors. Mount Isola's Steamboat and Heavenly Ridge areas offer deep powder tree runs. Resort-style amenities include indoor wave pool, carousel, and multiple restaurants within resort complex. Gondola-connected accommodation to slopes. Night skiing on West Mountain. Located 90 minutes from Sapporo New Chitose Airport, 30 minutes from Niseko (combined trip possible). Amusement park operates summer season. Less village atmosphere than Niseko or Hakuba — self-contained resort model. Season late November to early April.
Two connected resorts linked by Dragondola (5.5-kilometre gondola, Japan's longest). Naeba offers wide groomed runs and extensive night skiing — popular with Tokyo weekenders due to proximity (2 hours by Shinkansen plus shuttle). Kagura (accessible via Dragondola from Naeba or separately from Mitsumata/Tashiro base) offers higher elevation, deeper snow, backcountry gate access, and one of Japan's longest seasons — often skiing into late May on Kagura's upper slopes. Combined terrain extensive for all ability levels. Naeba Prince Hotel (1,200+ rooms) dominates base area. Fuji Rock Festival held on Naeba resort grounds each July (Japan's largest outdoor music festival). Heavy snowfall from Sea of Japan weather systems. Season mid-December to late May (Kagura).
