11.3-metre-tall bronze Amida Buddha seated outdoors since original wooden hall was destroyed by tsunami in 1498. Cast in 1252, the statue weighs approximately 121 tonnes and is hollow — visitors can enter interior to see casting marks and internal structure. Second tallest bronze Buddha in Japan after Todai-ji's Daibutsu in Nara. Calm facial expression and meditative posture make it one of Japan's most recognisable images. Located in Hase area of western Kamakura. Designated National Treasure. Simple temple grounds allow focus on statue itself without elaborate surrounding architecture. Best appreciated from slight distance to see full proportions against open sky. Allow 30-45 minutes.
Founded 1063, expanded by Minamoto no Yoritomo when he established Japan's first military government (shogunate) in Kamakura in 1180. Shrine complex built along central axis of Wakamiya-oji boulevard running from coast to hillside. Wide stone stairway ascends to vermillion-painted main hall on hilltop. Genpei Ponds at entrance represent Minamoto (white lotus) and Taira (red lotus) clans. Museum of National Treasures within grounds displays swords, masks, and historical documents. Ancient ginkgo tree beside stairway toppled in 2010 storm after approximately 1,000 years — replanted trunk has resprouted. Major festivals include September horseback archery (yabusame) demonstration. Located at Kamakura's geographical and spiritual centre. Grounds free to explore.
Temple known for 9.18-metre gilded wooden Kannon (goddess of mercy) statue — Japan's largest wooden sculpture, carved from single camphor tree according to legend. Upper terrace observation platform provides panoramic views across Sagami Bay and Kamakura coastline. Jizo-do hall surrounded by thousands of small Jizo statues placed as memorials for unborn children. Beautiful gardens change seasonally — hydrangeas in June draw significant visitors, autumn foliage, and winter plum blossoms. Cave system (Benten-kutsu) dedicated to goddess Benzaiten contains carved figures along narrow passages. Amida Buddha in separate hall was personal prayer figure of Minamoto no Yoritomo. Located near Great Buddha in Hase district. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
Rinzai Zen temple founded 1334, famous for dense bamboo grove (Take-dera) behind main hall containing approximately 2,000 moso bamboo stalks. Tea house within bamboo grove serves matcha while surrounded by towering bamboo creating meditative atmosphere. Sunlight filtering through bamboo canopy creates shifting patterns throughout day. Zen garden with raked gravel in front of main hall. Temple has tragic history — Ashikaga clan members committed ritual suicide here during fall of Kamakura government. Rock garden and main hall precede bamboo grove entrance. Located in eastern Kamakura, short bus ride or 30-minute walk from Kamakura Station. Less crowded than Kyoto's Arashiyama bamboo. Allow 45 minutes to 1 hour.
Founded 1282 by regent Hojo Tokimune to honour Japanese and Mongol soldiers who died during Mongol invasion attempts. One of Kamakura's Five Great Zen Temples and second in rank. Shariden (relic hall) is Kamakura's only National Treasure building — Chinese Song-dynasty architectural style housing what is believed to be a tooth of Buddha. Massive Sanmon gate rebuilt 1785 contains Buddhist statues viewable during special openings. Engaku-ji bell (1301) is Kamakura's largest — designated Important Cultural Property. Active Zen monastery offering meditation sessions to public (zazen). Grounds spread across forested hillside with sub-temples and tea houses. Located directly beside Kita-Kamakura Station. Autumn foliage outstanding. Allow 1 hour.
Founded 1253 as Japan's first dedicated Zen training monastery, ranked first of Kamakura's Five Great Zen Temples. Established by Chinese monk Rankei Doryu at invitation of regent Hojo Tokiyori. Sanmon gate, Buddha Hall, and Dharma Hall aligned along central axis in classic Chinese Zen monastery layout. Juniper trees beside Dharma Hall reportedly grown from seeds brought from China by founder 770 years ago. Hojo (abbot's quarters) garden designed by Zen master Muso Soseki features pond in shape of kanji character for "heart." Trail behind temple ascends to Hansobo shrine with tengu statues and panoramic views of Kamakura and coastline. Walking trail continues to Tsurugaoka Hachimangu. Allow 1-1.5 hours.
Hidden shrine in rocky valley reached through tunnel carved into hillside. Founded 1185 when Minamoto no Yoritomo received divine vision instructing him to worship at this spring. Visitors wash money in spring water within cave, believing it will multiply — tradition dates from 13th century. Incense smoke fills cave interior where water flows through carved channels and bamboo baskets hold coins and notes during washing. Shrine dedicated to Benzaiten (Ugafukujinja), goddess of wealth and good fortune. Approached via narrow mountain path through residential area — signposted but feels like discovery. Torii gates mark entrance through rock tunnel. Located in western hills between Kamakura Station and Great Buddha. Unique among Japan's shrines. Allow 30-45 minutes including walk.
Zen temple founded 1160, famous as Kamakura's premier hydrangea destination — hillside paths lined with approximately 2,500 predominantly blue Meigetsuin-blue hydrangea bushes blooming in June. Circular window (marumado) in main hall frames rear garden like a living painting — photographs of this window are iconic Kamakura images. Rear garden opens only during hydrangea season (June) and autumn foliage season (November-December). Yagura cave tombs carved into surrounding cliffs date from Kamakura period. Garden features rabbits kept as temple animals. Located near Kita-Kamakura Station adjacent to Engaku-ji. Expect significant crowds during peak hydrangea season — early morning visits recommended. Outside June and November, temple is peaceful and uncrowded. Allow 30-45 minutes.
