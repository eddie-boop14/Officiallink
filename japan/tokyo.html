🇯🇵 Meiji Shrine

📍
Founded 645 AD, Tokyo's oldest and most visited temple. Kaminarimon (Thunder Gate) with giant red lantern marks entrance to Nakamise-dori shopping street leading to main hall. Five-storey pagoda stands beside main temple grounds. Current buildings reconstructed after World War II. Incense burner in front courtyard where visitors waft smoke for good health. Omikuji fortune papers available at stalls. Temple illuminated at night creating dramatic photography. Asakusa district surrounding temple retains old Tokyo atmosphere. Main hall enshrines golden image of Kannon (goddess of mercy) discovered by fishermen in Sumida River.
Dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken, completed 1920. Set within 70-hectare forest of 120,000 trees donated from across Japan. Massive torii gate at entrance is Japan's largest wooden torii, made from 1,500-year-old cypress. Inner garden features iris garden and well associated with Empress Shoken. Traditional Shinto wedding ceremonies regularly visible. Treasure Museum displays personal effects of Emperor and Empress. Approached via wide gravel path through dense forest creating immediate escape from surrounding Shibuya and Harajuku bustle. New Year's first shrine visit draws over three million people.

🇯🇵 Shibuya Sky
Opened 2019 atop Shibuya Scramble Square building at 229 metres.
See official site
→
📍
Completed 2012, standing 634 metres as world's tallest tower and second tallest structure. Two observation decks: Tembo Deck at 350m and Tembo Galleria at 450m with glass floor sections. Views extend to Mount Fuji on clear days. Height of 634 references old name for Tokyo region (Musashi — 6-3-4 in Japanese wordplay). Base contains Tokyo Solamachi shopping complex with over 300 shops. Sumida Aquarium located at base. Evening illumination alternates between blue "Iki" and purple "Miyabi" lighting schemes. Located in Sumida ward near Asakusa. Advance online booking recommended to avoid queues.
Completed 1958, standing 333 metres tall. Designed as broadcast tower inspired by Eiffel Tower but 13 metres taller. Two observation levels: Main Deck at 150m and Top Deck at 250m. Distinctive international orange and white colour required by aviation safety regulations. Constructed using steel partly recycled from American tanks from Korean War. Night illumination changes seasonally — orange in winter, white in summer. Top Deck tour includes guided experience with LED lighting and audio. Symbol of postwar Japan's recovery. Located in Minato ward near Zojo-ji temple. Still functions as broadcast tower alongside tourism.
Opened 2019 atop Shibuya Scramble Square building at 229 metres. Open-air rooftop Sky Stage offers 360-degree panoramic views including Shibuya Crossing directly below, Tokyo Tower, Tokyo Skytree, and Mount Fuji. Sky Edge glass-walled section allows leaning over city. Indoor Sky Gallery at 46th floor features immersive digital displays. Sunset and evening visits popular for city light views. Located directly above Shibuya Station making access straightforward. Advance ticket purchase strongly recommended as capacity is limited. Photography and video encouraged on rooftop. One of Tokyo's newest observation experiences.

📍
Founded 1872, Japan's oldest museum housing over 120,000 objects including 89 National Treasures. Five buildings across Ueno Park campus: Honkan (Japanese Gallery) displays samurai armour, swords, ceramics, and paintings; Toyokan covers Asian art; Heiseikan houses archaeological finds including Jomon-period pottery. Gallery of Horyuji Treasures contains 7th-century Buddhist art in striking modern building by Yoshio Taniguchi. Seasonal exhibitions rotate highlights from vast collection. Japanese garden behind Honkan opens during spring and autumn. Allow 3-4 hours minimum. Largest collection of Japanese art and antiquities in the world.
Comprehensive science museum in Ueno Park with Japan Gallery and Global Gallery. Japan Gallery traces archipelago's natural history from formation through to modern era including preserved specimens of now-extinct Japanese wolf and Japanese sea lion. Global Gallery covers evolution, biodiversity, and technology across three floors. Full-size blue whale model outside building. Interactive exhibits cover space exploration, earthquakes, and robotics. Hachiko's preserved remains displayed here — the famous loyal dog. 360-degree immersive theatre simulates natural phenomena. Allow 2-3 hours. Popular with families.
Contemporary art museum on 53rd floor of Roppongi Hills Mori Tower. Rotating exhibitions focus on contemporary art, architecture, and design from Asian and international artists. No permanent collection — entire space dedicated to large-scale temporary exhibitions changing several times yearly. Museum ticket includes access to Tokyo City View observation deck on 52nd floor with panoramic city views. Sky Deck rooftop (weather permitting) offers open-air experience at 270 metres. Located in Roppongi Hills complex with restaurants, shops, and cinema. Late evening hours allow combining art with night city views. Allow 2 hours.
Relocated to Azabudai Hills in 2024 after original Odaiba location closed. Immersive digital art museum where artworks move between rooms, merge with other works, and respond to visitor presence. Created by art collective teamLab, each installation uses projections, LEDs, and sensors to create boundary-free art that visitors walk through. En Tea House serves tea in digital art bowls where flowers bloom in your cup. No set route — visitors wander freely discovering interconnected spaces. Advance online booking essential as daily capacity limited and dates sell out weeks ahead. Photography encouraged. Allow 2-3 hours. One of Tokyo's most sought-after experiences.
Main building designed by Le Corbusier, designated UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of his international architectural contribution. Collection built around Matsukata Collection — amassed by industrialist Matsukata Kojiro in early 20th century and returned to Japan from France in 1959. Holdings include works by Monet, Renoir, Van Gogh, Picasso, Rubens, and Rodin. Rodin sculptures including The Thinker and Gates of Hell displayed in forecourt. New Wing designed by Kunio Maekawa (Le Corbusier's student) houses expanding collection. Located in Ueno Park alongside Tokyo National Museum and other cultural institutions. Permanent collection always accessible. Temporary exhibitions feature major international loans. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
Private art museum in Omotesando housing collection of pre-modern Japanese and East Asian art assembled by railway magnate Nezu Kaichiro. Over 7,400 works including seven National Treasures — highlights include Ogata Korin's Irises folding screens (displayed briefly each spring). Modern building designed by Kengo Kuma features bamboo-lined entrance corridor. Expansive Japanese garden behind museum with tea houses, stone paths, ponds, and seasonal plantings creating peaceful contrast to surrounding fashion district. Rotating exhibitions drawn from permanent collection. Located steps from Omotesando shopping boulevard. Allow 1.5-2 hours including garden.
Tokyo's principal kabuki theatre in Ginza, rebuilt in 2013 preserving original 1924 baroque Japanese design. Full kabuki performances run in multi-act programs lasting several hours with elaborate costumes, staging, and traditional music. Single-act tickets available for visitors wanting shorter experience without committing to full performance. English audio guides and subtitle screens available for international visitors. Kabukiza Gallery on fifth floor displays costumes, props, and stage machinery. Theatre basement connects to Ginza subway station. Traditional Japanese sweets and bento available in theatre. Fifth generation of building on this site since 1889.

📍
Former site of Edo Castle's innermost circles, opened to public in 1968. Massive stone walls and moats remain from Tokugawa-era castle. Ninomaru Garden features traditional Japanese landscaping with pond, waterfall, and seasonal plantings. Remains of castle keep (tenshu-dai) mark where Japan's largest castle tower once stood before burning in 1657. Museum of Imperial Collections displays art from imperial family. Gardens closed Mondays and Fridays. Located adjacent to active Imperial Palace where Emperor resides (palace interior only accessible on guided tours requiring advance reservation through Imperial Household Agency). Free to enter the East Gardens. No reservation required.
58-hectare garden combining three distinct styles: French Formal with symmetrical plantain tree avenue, English Landscape with sweeping lawns, and Japanese Traditional with pond, tea house, and bridges. Over 1,000 cherry trees make it one of Tokyo's premier hanami (cherry blossom) viewing locations in spring. Large greenhouse contains tropical and subtropical plants. Originally completed 1906 as imperial garden, opened to public 1949. Alcohol prohibited inside gardens. Surrounded by Shinjuku skyscrapers creating striking contrast. Chrysanthemum exhibition held each November. Autumn foliage equally spectacular. Allow 2-3 hours for full exploration.
Former duck hunting grounds of Tokugawa shoguns, designated Special Place of Scenic Beauty. Unique seawater tidal ponds change level with Tokyo Bay tides — only surviving Edo-period tidal garden. Nakashima tea house sits on island in main pond serving matcha with traditional sweets. 300-year-old pine tree planted by sixth Tokugawa shogun. Gardens bordered by Tsukiji on one side and modern Shiodome skyscrapers creating dramatic juxtaposition of old and new Tokyo. Water bus connects garden pier to Asakusa along Sumida River — popular way to combine with Senso-ji visit. Peony garden blooms winter and spring. Allow 1-2 hours.

🇯🇵 Ueno Zoo
Opened 1882 as Japan's first zoological garden, located within Ueno Park.
See official site
→
📍
Outer market area surrounding former Tsukiji fish market site (wholesale operations moved to Toyosu in 2018 but outer market remains). Over 400 shops and restaurants along narrow lanes selling fresh seafood, Japanese knives, dried goods, pickles, and street food. Famous for fresh sushi and sashimi breakfasts. Tamagoyaki (Japanese omelette) stalls, fresh oysters, and grilled seafood available for eating while walking. Morning visits recommended for freshest selection and atmosphere. Tsukiji Hongwanji temple located adjacent to market area. Shops and stalls operate independently with varying hours. Popular with both tourists and professional chefs sourcing ingredients.
Opened 1882 as Japan's first zoological garden, located within Ueno Park. Home to giant pandas since 1972 — panda viewing area draws significant queues. Over 2,500 animals representing approximately 300 species across East and West gardens connected by monorail bridge. Western lowland gorillas, Sumatran tigers, polar bears, and Japanese macaques among highlights. Five-storey pagoda from 1631 stands within zoo grounds (originally part of Kan'ei-ji temple). Shinobazu Pond within grounds attracts wild waterfowl. Children's zoo area allows animal interaction. Located in Ueno Park alongside multiple museums. Allow 2-3 hours.
