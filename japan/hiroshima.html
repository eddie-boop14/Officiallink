Museum documenting the atomic bombing of August 6, 1945 through personal belongings, photographs, survivor testimonies, and scientific explanations. East Building provides historical context of wartime Japan and events leading to bombing. Main Building displays possessions of victims including school uniforms, watches stopped at 8:15am, and shadow imprints. Renovated 2019 with redesigned exhibition emphasising individual stories. Audio guides available in multiple languages. Emotionally intense experience — visitors should be prepared. Located within Peace Memorial Park designed by architect Kenzo Tange. Allow 2 hours minimum. Combination of physical evidence and personal narrative creates one of world's most significant war museums.
Skeletal ruins of former Hiroshima Prefectural Industrial Promotion Hall, almost directly below the hypocentre of the atomic blast. Building's dome frame survived because the bomb detonated almost directly above — blast force was vertical rather than lateral against the reinforced structure. Preserved as bombed since 1945, designated UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1996 as symbol of nuclear devastation and symbol for world peace. Exterior viewing only — building structurally unsafe for entry. Regular reinforcement work maintains ruins in current state. Located at northern end of Peace Memorial Park along Motoyasu River. Illuminated at night. Most powerful when combined with museum visit. Visible from Peace Memorial Park's cenotaph on direct axis.
12-hectare park designed by architect Kenzo Tange on former busy commercial district destroyed by the bombing. Cenotaph holds register of all known victims — arch frames view of Peace Flame and Atomic Bomb Dome in deliberate alignment. Peace Flame has burned continuously since 1964 and will remain lit until all nuclear weapons are eliminated. Children's Peace Monument topped with figure of Sadako Sasaki holding golden crane — inspired by her story of folding 1,000 paper cranes while suffering radiation illness. Visitors leave strings of folded paper cranes at base. Memorial mound contains ashes of approximately 70,000 unidentified victims. Peace Bell available for visitors to ring. Annual ceremony held August 6. Free to visit. Expansive grounds with tree-lined paths along two rivers.

📍
Shinto shrine built over water so that entire complex appears to float at high tide. Great Torii gate standing 16.6 metres in open water is one of Japan's most iconic images — current gate dates from 1875 and underwent major restoration completed 2022. Shrine buildings connected by covered corridors over tidal flats. Founded 593 AD, current structures date from 1168 design by Taira no Kiyomori. Noh theatre stage extends over water — oldest noh stage in Japan. Located on Miyajima (Itsukushima) island in Hiroshima Bay, reached by short ferry from mainland. UNESCO World Heritage Site. At low tide, visitors can walk to base of torii gate. Wild deer roam island freely. Island considered so sacred that historically no births or deaths were permitted.
Shingon Buddhist temple at base of Mount Misen, founded 806 AD by Kobo Daishi (founder of Shingon Buddhism). Sprawling complex ascends hillside with multiple halls, caves, and gardens. Five hundred Rakan statues (disciples of Buddha) line paths, each with unique expression — many wearing hand-knitted hats and scarves placed by visitors. Prayer wheels along stairways can be spun while ascending. Henjokutsu Cave contains 88 icons representing Shikoku pilgrimage — walking through cave considered equivalent to completing full pilgrimage. Maniden Hall contains sacred fire said to have burned continuously for 1,200 years (same flame used to light Peace Flame in Hiroshima). Often overlooked by tourists focused on Itsukushima Shrine despite rich history and atmosphere.
Two-stage ropeway ascending Mount Misen (535m), Miyajima's highest peak and sacred mountain. First stage uses gondola cabins to Kayatani Station, second stage uses larger aerial tramway to Shishi-iwa Station near summit. From upper station, 30-minute walk through primeval forest (designated natural monument) reaches summit observatory with panoramic views across Seto Inland Sea. Summit area contains Misen Hondo hall and Reikado hall housing Kobo Daishi's eternal flame. Alternative hiking trails from sea level take 1.5-2 hours ascending through ancient forest. Wild deer and macaques inhabit mountain forest. Round trip by ropeway plus summit walk takes approximately 2 hours. Weather-dependent operation — check conditions before planning.

📍
Original castle built 1589 by feudal lord Mori Terumoto, completely destroyed by atomic bombing in 1945. Current five-storey tower reconstructed 1958 in concrete houses museum covering Hiroshima's pre-war history and samurai culture. Displays include armour, swords, and scale models showing castle's development. Observation deck on top floor provides city views including Peace Memorial Park. Surrounding moat and stone walls partially original. Second compound (Ninomaru) reconstructed in wood following original techniques. Located within walking distance of Peace Memorial Park. Castle grounds include several reconstructed guard towers and gates. Exhibition focuses on castle culture and daily life rather than atomic bombing (covered by Peace Museum). Allow 1-1.5 hours.

📍
Compact garden designed 1620 by tea ceremony master Ueda Soko for feudal lord Asano Nagaakira. Name means "shrunken scenery garden" — design miniaturises famous landscapes into concentrated form around central pond. Arched rainbow bridge (Kokokyo) spans pond as garden's focal point. Severely damaged by atomic bombing — garden restored from 1951 using historical records and surviving elements. Walking circuit passes through mountains, valleys, and forests represented in miniature. Tea house overlooking pond serves matcha. Located near Hiroshima Castle within central city. Particularly beautiful during cherry blossom season and autumn foliage. Koi and turtles inhabit pond. Allow 1 hour for leisurely circuit walk.
Circular domed building within Hijiyama Park housing collection focused on French Impressionism and modern European art. Works by Monet, Renoir, Degas, Cézanne, Matisse, Picasso, and Chagall alongside Japanese Western-style painters. Distinctive round gallery space designed to encourage contemplative viewing without corners or dead ends. Founded 1978 by Hiroshima Bank to bring world-class art to the city as cultural recovery. Surrounding gardens and fountain complement museum experience. Small but high-quality collection displayed in single circular route. Located in central park area near Shukkeien Garden. Allow 1 hour. Museum shop carries art prints and postcards. Peaceful alternative to intense Peace Memorial experiences.
Atmospheric temple hidden in forested valley on Hiroshima's outskirts, founded 809 AD. Named for three waterfalls (Komyono-taki, Baryuno-taki, Sanbono-taki) flowing through grounds. Moss-covered stone paths wind through dense forest past pagoda, Buddha statues, and prayer halls. A-bombed survivors came here seeking water after the bombing — memorial stone marks this history. Thatched-roof tea house rebuilt using timbers from a building that survived the atomic bombing. Significantly cooler than central Hiroshima in summer due to tree canopy and waterfalls. Reached by short train ride to Mitaki Station followed by 15-minute uphill walk. Few foreign tourists despite atmospheric beauty. Allow 1 hour including walk from station.
