One of the world's largest aquariums, centrepiece of Ocean Expo Park on Okinawa's northern coast. Kuroshio Sea tank holds 7,500 cubic metres of water housing whale sharks (multiple individuals), manta rays, and hundreds of fish species visible through massive acrylic panel (22.5m wide, 8.2m tall). Four floors descend from shallow coral reef displays through tropical fish to deep ocean. Outdoor sea turtle pools and manatee enclosure included. Ocean Expo Park surrounding aquarium is free and contains Tropical Dream Centre botanical garden, reconstructed traditional Okinawan village, and Emerald Beach. Dolphin shows in adjacent Ocean Theatre. Located approximately 90 minutes drive from Naha. Allow 3-4 hours for aquarium and park.
Archipelago of 30 islands approximately 40 kilometres west of Naha, designated National Park in 2014. Water clarity nicknamed "Kerama Blue" with visibility exceeding 50 metres. Green and loggerhead sea turtles regularly encountered while snorkelling. Coral reefs support over 250 species of coral and abundant marine life. Main inhabited islands: Tokashiki (largest), Zamami, and Aka. High-speed ferry from Naha reaches islands in 35-50 minutes. Whale watching season January-Marché as humpback whales migrate to breed in warm waters. Beaches include Furuzamami and Aharen consistently ranked among Japan's finest. Accommodation limited — day trips possible but overnight stays recommended. Each island has distinct character.

📍
Royal palace of the Ryukyu Kingdom that governed Okinawa from 15th to 19th century, blending Chinese and Japanese architectural influences in distinctive red-lacquered buildings. Main hall (Seiden) destroyed by fire in October 2019 — reconstruction underway with expected completion around 2026, with the rebuilding process itself now a visitor experience with viewing areas showing traditional construction techniques. Surrounding stone walls, gates (Shureimon — depicted on 2,000-yen banknote), and courtyards accessible. UNESCO World Heritage Site inscription covers stone foundations and walls, which survived the fire. Located on hilltop in Naha overlooking city. Ryukyu cultural performances in courtyard. Castle has been destroyed and rebuilt multiple times throughout history. Allow 1.5-2 hours.
Most sacred spiritual site in Okinawan religion, where the Ryukyu Kingdom's highest priestess performed ceremonies. UNESCO World Heritage Site consisting of natural rock formations, forest groves, and stone-paved paths rather than built structures. Triangular rock opening (sangui) frames view of Kudaka Island — believed to be where Okinawan creation deity Amamikiyo descended. Site remains active place of prayer for Okinawan spiritual practitioners. Forest path leads through several prayer points before reaching main sacred area. Located on Chinen Peninsula in southern Okinawa, approximately 50 minutes from Naha by car. Respectful behaviour essential — visitors asked not to touch sacred stones or stray from paths. Quiet, atmospheric experience contrasting with Okinawa's resort areas.
Hilltop castle ruins (gusuku) considered best-preserved of Okinawa's UNESCO-listed fortresses. Six interconnected enclosures built 14th-15th century using precisely fitted limestone blocks without mortar — construction technique impressed Commodore Perry's expedition in 1853 who documented the walls in detailed drawings. Curved wall design unique to Okinawan castle architecture, influenced by Chinese fortress building. Panoramic views from hilltop across both Pacific Ocean and East China Sea coasts of Okinawa. Built by legendary lord Gosamaru. Located in central Okinawa, accessible by car. Fewer visitors than Shuri Castle allowing quiet exploration. Walking circuit around six enclosures follows castle's natural hilltop contours. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Allow 1 hour.

Naha's 1.6-kilometre main thoroughfare lined with shops, restaurants, bars, and market arcades. Rebuilt from wartime devastation as "Miracle Mile" — now Okinawa's primary shopping and entertainment street. Side streets lead to Makishi Public Market (Naha's kitchen) where ground floor sells fresh seafood, tropical fruit, and Okinawan ingredients, and vendors cook your purchases on the upper floor. Tsuboya Pottery Street nearby preserves traditional Okinawan ceramic workshops. Okinawan craft specialities include shisa (guardian lion-dog) figurines, Ryukyu glass, and bingata dyed textiles. Live Okinawan music featuring sanshin (three-stringed instrument) in many restaurants. Street performers weekends and evenings. Free to walk and explore at any time.
Theme park centred on Gyokusendo Cave, a 300,000-year-old limestone cave system stretching over five kilometres (800 metres open to public). Stalactites and stalagmites illuminated along paved walkway through massive chambers. Above ground, Ryukyu Village recreates traditional Okinawan settlement with restored 100-200 year old houses relocated from across the islands. Traditional craft workshops include glass blowing, pottery, textile weaving, and Okinawan paper-making. Eisa dance performances showcase traditional Okinawan drumming and dance. Habu snake park features Okinawa's native pit viper. Tropical fruit orchard. Located in southern Okinawa, approximately 30 minutes from Naha. Allow 2-3 hours for cave and village.
Dramatic cliff-top headland on Okinawa's western coast, famous for natural rock arch resembling an elephant's trunk extending into the East China Sea. Name derives from Ryukyu King Sho Kei's remark that the grass meadow atop the cliff could "seat ten thousand people" (man-za-mo). Coral limestone cliffs drop dramatically to clear turquoise water below. Sunset viewing point — western orientation provides unobstructed Pacific sunsets. Visitor centre (2020) with elevated walkway and observation platform replaced former informal access. Short circular walking route along cliff edge provides multiple viewing angles. Located on west coast near resort area of Onna Village, approximately one hour from Naha. Brief stop — allow 30-45 minutes. Free to access viewing areas.
