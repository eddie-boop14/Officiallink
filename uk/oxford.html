Bodleian Library

One of Europe's Oldest Libraries - 13 Million Items

Established 1602, second-largest library in Britain. Holds copy of every book published in UK under legal deposit. Guided tours visit medieval Duke Hu...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
One of the oldest libraries in Europe, established in 1602 by Sir Thomas Bodley on the foundation of an earlier 15th-century university library. The Bodleian holds over 13 million items as a legal deposit library receiving a copy of every book published in the United Kingdom and Ireland. Guided tours visit the medieval Duke Humfrey's Library — a stunning 15th-century reading room with painted ceiling beams and original chained-book shelves that served as a filming location for the Harry Potter series. The Divinity School below features one of England's finest examples of Gothic fan-vaulted ceiling, completed in the 1480s after fifty years of construction. The library complex includes the Radcliffe Camera, Clarendon Building, and Weston Library exhibition galleries. Extended tours explore additional rooms not open to the public.

Radcliffe Camera

Iconic Circular Library Building

Completed 1749, designed by James Gibbs. One of Oxford's most photographed landmarks. Functions as reading room of Bodleian Library. Interior not open...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Circular domed library building completed in 1749, designed by James Gibbs in English Palladian style as England's first purpose-built round library. The building was funded by a bequest from physician John Radcliffe and originally housed a science library before becoming a reading room of the Bodleian Library. The neo-classical rotunda rises 46 metres with rusticated base, paired Corinthian columns, and a lead-covered dome topped by a lantern. The interior — not generally open to the public except on guided Bodleian tours — features a double-height circular reading room beneath the dome. One of Oxford's most photographed landmarks, best viewed from the surrounding Radcliffe Square, Brasenose Lane, or the tower of the University Church of St Mary the Virgin opposite.

Magdalen College

15th-Century College with Deer Park

Founded 1458, one of Oxford's wealthiest colleges. Grounds include deer park, water meadows, and Addison's Walk along River Cherwell. Chapel contains ...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Founded in 1458 by William of Waynflete, Bishop of Winchester, Magdalen is one of Oxford's wealthiest and most beautiful colleges. The grounds encompass a deer park where fallow deer have grazed since the 18th century, water meadows along the River Cherwell, and Addison's Walk — a circular tree-lined path named after the essayist Joseph Addison. The Great Tower, completed in 1509, is the setting for the famous May Morning tradition where the college choir sings from the top at 6am on May 1st, attracting thousands of listeners on Magdalen Bridge below. C.S. Lewis was a Fellow here for 29 years. The chapel contains original medieval stained glass, and the cloisters feature carved stone grotesques known as hieroglyphics. College buildings span late medieval to Victorian Gothic periods.

New College

14th-Century College with Medieval Cloisters

Founded 1379 by William of Wykeham. First Oxford college built as complete quadrangle around courtyard. Chapel features medieval stained glass and El ...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Founded in 1379 by William of Wykeham, Bishop of Winchester, as the first Oxford college designed and built as a unified architectural scheme around a quadrangle — a pattern that became the template for all subsequent Oxford and Cambridge colleges. The chapel contains a dramatic Lazarus painting attributed to El Greco and extraordinary medieval misericord carvings beneath the choir stalls, alongside stained glass designed by Sir Joshua Reynolds. The college incorporates a substantial section of Oxford's 13th-century city wall, accessible via a wall walk through the gardens. The medieval cloisters — among the best preserved in Oxford — feature in the Harry Potter films as Hogwarts corridors. The ancient holm oak in the garden is one of the oldest trees in Oxford. New College's name distinguished it from the older colleges at its founding.

Ashmolean Museum

Britain's First Public Museum

Free admission

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Britain's first public museum, founded in 1683 when Elias Ashmole donated his collection of curiosities to the University of Oxford. The current neoclassical building on Beaumont Street houses collections spanning ancient civilisations to contemporary art across five floors of interconnected galleries. Highlights include Egyptian mummies, the Alfred Jewel (a 9th-century Anglo-Saxon treasure), Stradivarius violins, the Parian marble, Guy Fawkes' lantern, and works by Michelangelo, Raphael, Turner, Picasso, and a major Pre-Raphaelite collection. A £61 million redevelopment in 2009 by architect Rick Mather created a dramatic glass atrium connecting the historic building with modern gallery spaces. The rooftop restaurant offers views across central Oxford. Allow two to three hours minimum for the main galleries.

University Church of St Mary the Virgin

Medieval Church with Viewing Tower

Official church of Oxford University since 13th century. Tower (127 steps) offers panoramic views across Oxford's dreaming spires. Site of trial of Ox...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
The official church of the University of Oxford since the 13th century, situated on the High Street opposite the Radcliffe Camera. The church tower — reached by climbing 127 narrow spiral steps — offers the classic panoramic view across Oxford's dreaming spires, with the Radcliffe Camera directly below and college towers stretching to the horizon. The church was the site of the trial of the Oxford Martyrs in 1555 — Archbishop Thomas Cranmer, Hugh Latimer, and Nicholas Ridley were condemned here before being burned at the stake in nearby Broad Street. The ornate Baroque south porch, added in 1637, features twisted columns. The Congregation House attached to the church served as the university's first library and meeting place. Active parish church with daily services and regular concerts.

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Oldest botanic garden in Britain, founded 1621. Compact 4.5-acre walled garden contains over 5,000 plant species. Historic glasshouses feature tropical plants, carnivorous plants, and alpine collection. River Cherwell borders garden. Beautiful stone gateway dates to 1632. Peaceful spot for walking. Research garden for Oxford University still used for plant science. Entry fee applies. Allow 1-2 hours.

Pitt Rivers Museum

Anthropology and World Archaeology Museum

Free admission

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Extraordinary anthropological museum displaying over 500,000 objects from cultures around the world, arranged by type rather than geography — all masks together, all weapons together, all textiles together — following the original classification system of founder Lieutenant-General Augustus Henry Lane Fox Pitt Rivers. The museum occupies a Victorian cast-iron and glass extension behind the Oxford University Museum of Natural History, entered through a doorway at the rear of the dinosaur gallery. Atmospheric dimly-lit cabinets crammed with objects create a cabinet-of-curiosities experience unique among modern museums. Collections include shrunken heads from South America, totem poles, Japanese armour, magic charms, and musical instruments from every continent. Founded in 1884, the museum maintains its Victorian display philosophy while adding contemporary interpretation and addressing the colonial contexts of its collections.
