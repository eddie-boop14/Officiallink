Roman Baths

2,000-year-old bathing complex built around Britain's only natural hot springs

The Roman Baths were built around 70 AD over Britain's only natural hot springs. The site includes the Sacred Spring, the Roman Temple, the Roman Bath...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
One of the best-preserved Roman religious spa complexes in the world, built around natural hot springs that produce over one million litres of mineral-rich water daily at a constant 46°C. The Romans constructed a temple to the goddess Sulis Minerva and elaborate bathing facilities here in the 1st century AD. Visitors walk above and around the Great Bath — a lead-lined pool still filled with steaming green mineral water — while viewing the original Roman stonework, carved pediment showing the Gorgon's head, gilded bronze head of Sulis Minerva, and thousands of Roman coins thrown as offerings. Audio guides narrate the site's history from Celtic shrine to Roman spa to Georgian rediscovery. The terrace above the baths offers views of Bath Abbey.

Bath Abbey

15th-century church with fan vaulting and the largest collection of wall memorials in Britain

Free entry (donations requested)

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Late medieval church in the heart of Bath, founded in 1499 on the site of an earlier Norman cathedral. The west front features famous carved stone ladders with angels climbing to heaven — a depiction of the founder Bishop Oliver King's dream. The interior is a masterpiece of Perpendicular Gothic with soaring fan-vaulted ceiling, enormous stained-glass windows filling the church with light, and over 600 memorial tablets lining the walls and floor. The tower offers panoramic views across Bath's Georgian rooftops after climbing 212 steps. Recent restoration of the floor revealed medieval tile pavements and created a new underground museum displaying Roman, Saxon, and Norman archaeological finds beneath the church. An active Anglican parish church with daily choral services.

The Royal Crescent

Iconic curved terrace of 30 Georgian townhouses — one of Britain's most photographed streets

Free to view

🌍 Maps ✅
Sweeping crescent of 30 terraced houses built between 1767 and 1774, designed by John Wood the Younger and considered the finest example of Georgian architecture in Britain. The 150-metre curved Palladian façade of 114 Ionic columns fronts a sloping lawn overlooking Royal Victoria Park and the countryside beyond. Each house behind the uniform façade was individually designed by its owner, creating hidden variety behind the classical exterior. The Royal Crescent represents the peak of Bath's 18th-century transformation from medieval town to fashionable spa resort. The building material — honey-coloured Bath stone quarried from nearby Combe Down — gives the crescent its distinctive warm golden appearance. A Grade I listed building and one of the most iconic architectural compositions in Europe.

No. 1 Royal Crescent

Georgian townhouse museum recreating 18th-century life

The first house in the Royal Crescent has been restored and furnished as a museum showing how the wealthy lived in Georgian Bath. The rooms contain pe...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Museum occupying the first house in the Royal Crescent, authentically restored and furnished to show how a wealthy Georgian family would have lived in the late 18th century. Period rooms include a gentleman's study, a lady's bedroom with original Chinese wallpaper, a drawing room with Gainsborough-era furniture, a dining room laid for dessert, and below-stairs servants' quarters with a working Georgian kitchen. The museum underwent a major restoration revealing original paint colours and Georgian decorative schemes hidden beneath later additions. Furniture, paintings, textiles, and ceramics are all authentic to the 1770s-1790s period. Provides essential context for understanding the Royal Crescent as a living space rather than just an architectural monument.

Pulteney Bridge

18th-century bridge lined with shops spanning the River Avon

Free

🌍 Maps ✅
One of only four bridges in the world lined with shops on both sides, designed by Robert Adam and completed in 1774. The Palladian bridge spans the River Avon connecting the city centre to the Bathwick estate, originally built to increase property values on the eastern bank. Three arches carry the roadway with small domed pavilions at each end and shop units extending over the water on both sides. The best view is from the downstream side at Parade Gardens, where the bridge and its horseshoe weir form one of Bath's most photographed scenes. The shops today house independent boutiques, cafes, and galleries. Inspired by Florence's Ponte Vecchio and Venice's Rialto Bridge, Pulteney Bridge is a Grade I listed structure.

The Circus

Circular Georgian plaza of townhouses arranged in three curved segments

Free

🌍 Maps ✅
Circular ring of 33 terraced townhouses designed by John Wood the Elder, begun in 1754 and completed after his death by his son John Wood the Younger in 1768. Three curved segments separated by three entrance streets form a perfect circle, with each segment featuring a different classical order of columns — Doric at ground level, Ionic on the first floor, and Corinthian above. The design inverts the Colosseum in Rome, turning the amphitheatre inside out so the columns face inward. Carved friezes running above the ground-floor columns depict nautical emblems, serpents, arts, and sciences — some connected to Freemasonry. Former residents include Thomas Gainsborough, Clive of India, and David Livingstone. Five mature London plane trees fill the central garden.

Fashion Museum

World-class collection of historical and contemporary fashion

Located in the Assembly Rooms, the Fashion Museum contains clothing from the 16th century to the present day. The collection includes court dress, eve...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅
Collection of over 100,000 objects spanning 500 years of fashion, previously housed in Bath's Assembly Rooms and currently preparing for relocation to a new permanent home. The collection ranks among the world's most significant fashion archives, holding garments from the 1600s to the present day including pieces by Vivienne Westwood, Alexander McQueen, and Mary Quant. The annual Dress of the Year award — selected by a leading fashion journalist — has been running since 1963 and provides a unique timeline of contemporary fashion history. The textile collection includes lace, embroidery, and accessories documenting changing techniques and social customs across centuries. Bath's historical connection to fashion dates from its Georgian heyday as England's most fashionable city.

Jane Austen Centre

Museum dedicated to Bath's most famous resident author

Jane Austen lived in Bath from 1801 to 1806 and set two of her novels (Northanger Abbey and Persuasion) in the city. The centre contains exhibits on h...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Museum and exhibition dedicated to Bath's most famous literary resident, located on Gay Street where Austen herself lived. The exhibition explores Austen's two periods of residence in Bath (1801-1806) and the city's influence on her novels — Northanger Abbey and Persuasion are both substantially set in Bath. Costumed guides lead visitors through period rooms, Regency dress-up areas, and displays explaining Georgian social customs, assembly rooms, and the marriage market that Austen satirised. A waxwork figure of Austen based on forensic research provides the best approximation of her appearance. The Regency Tea Room upstairs serves tea in period style. Located in the heart of the streets Austen walked and wrote about.

Thermae Bath Spa

Modern spa complex using the same natural hot springs as the Romans

Britain's only natural thermal spa open to the public. The rooftop pool offers panoramic views over Bath while bathing in the same mineral-rich waters...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Britain's only natural thermal spa open to the public. The rooftop pool offers panoramic views over Bath while bathing in the same mineral-rich waters the Romans discovered over 2,000 years ago. The complex includes the New Royal Bath with four floors of spa facilities and the smaller Cross Bath, a more intimate open-air bathing experience on the site of one of the original medieval baths. The natural spring water rises at 46°C. Sessions are timed and booking is recommended.

Victoria Art Gallery

British art from the 15th century to present day

The gallery contains paintings by Gainsborough, Turner, and contemporary artists. The collection focuses on British art with particular strength in pa...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Bath's civic art gallery overlooking Pulteney Bridge, housing a permanent collection spanning 600 years of European painting, decorative arts, and ceramics. Upper galleries display works by Thomas Gainsborough (who lived and worked in Bath), Walter Sickert, JMW Turner, and other British painters, alongside Delftware pottery, Georgian glassware, and Bohemian garnet jewellery. The ground-floor gallery hosts a changing programme of temporary exhibitions ranging from contemporary art to historical retrospectives. The gallery's collection of 18th-century painting reflects Bath's role as a centre of artistic patronage during the Georgian period — artists flocked to the city to paint wealthy visitors taking the waters. The building dates from 1897 in a Victorian Baroque style.

Sally Lunn's Historic Eating House & Museum

Tudor house dating to 1482 — one of Bath's oldest buildings with kitchen museum

Museum free with purchase

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Claimed to be one of the oldest houses in Bath, the building dates from around 1482 and houses both a restaurant and a small basement museum. The museum reveals the original medieval and Tudor kitchen with a Roman and medieval archaeological excavation visible through glass flooring. Sally Lunn's is famous for the Sally Lunn Bun — a large, light brioche-style bread served with sweet or savoury toppings — said to have been created by a young Huguenot refugee named Solange Luyon who arrived in Bath in the 1680s. The building's four storeys show architectural layers from the 15th century through Georgian remodelling. The narrow medieval street façade on North Parade Passage contrasts with Bath's later grand Georgian developments.

Prior Park Landscape Garden

18th-century landscape garden with Palladian bridge

Created in the 18th century by Ralph Allen with advice from Capability Brown and the poet Alexander Pope. The garden contains one of only four Palladi...

🌐 Official Site ✅🌍 Maps ✅🎟 Book Official ✅
Eighteenth-century landscape garden created by Bath entrepreneur Ralph Allen on a hillside south of the city, designed with advice from Alexander Pope and Capability Brown. The centrepiece is one of only four Palladian bridges in the world — a decorative covered bridge spanning the lake at the garden's lowest point, virtually identical to the original at Wilton House. The garden descends through woodland, a serpentine lake, a wilderness area, and open meadows offering panoramic views across Bath's skyline from an elevated vantage point. No car parking at the garden — visitors walk, cycle, or take the bus from the city centre, which is itself part of the experience. Managed by the National Trust. The garden formed the designed landscape setting for Allen's Prior Park mansion.

Alexandra Park

Victorian park with views over Bath from the hillside

A Victorian park on a hillside east of the city centre offering views over Bath and the surrounding valleys. The park contains formal gardens, open gr...

🌍 Maps ✅
Hilltop public park on Beechen Cliff offering one of the finest panoramic views in Bath — the entire Georgian city laid out below with the Royal Crescent, Bath Abbey, Pulteney Bridge, and the surrounding hills all visible in a single sweep. The park spans seven hectares of sloping grassland, mature trees, and children's play areas on the southern hillside above the River Avon. The viewpoint at the top of the park is considered the best free vantage point in the city and is particularly dramatic at sunset when the Bath stone glows golden. Local families and visitors use the park for picnics and walking. Shakespeare's Cliff Walk runs along the hillside below connecting to the Kennet and Avon Canal towpath. Open at all times with no entry restrictions.
